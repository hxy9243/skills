## Education vs. Alzheimer's

A number of studies have shown that education prevents Alzheimer's. Learning constitutes a vital part of the [brain hygiene](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Literacy_helps_prevent_Alzheimer%27s "Literacy helps prevent Alzheimer's"). However, schooling, education and learning are all different things. Brain can also be abused. Stress, sleep deprivation, alcohol, and drugs are best known factors of brain abuse. Not everyone knows that learning can also be a tool of [brain abuse](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/On_the_superiority_of_a_rat_over_a_schooled_human "On the superiority of a rat over a schooled human").

In early childhood, brain abuse may, paradoxically, [accelerate outwardly observed progress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Daycare_misery "Daycare misery"). In adolescence, brain abuse will lead to [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness_vs._learn_drive "Learned helplessness vs. learn drive"), depression, addictions, and other mental disorders. Cumulative brain abuse over the course of a lifetime will result in Alzheimer's disease. Its primary cause is [excitotoxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excitotoxicity) and [neuroinflammation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuroinflammation) resulting from inadequate processing power vis-a-vis the cognitive load.

To say that learning prevents Alzheimer's is a bit like saying [cold causes colds](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Myth:_We_catch_a_cold_from_cold "Myth: We catch a cold from cold"). The common denominator is that both cold and learning are great preventive measures when employed correctly. On the other hand, they can both backfire. In an extreme case, you can freeze a man to death. Otherwise, [cold is far safer in cold prevention](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Formula_for_common_cold_prevention "Formula for common cold prevention") than learning is in preventing Alzheimer's. The two main areas where learning is frequently injurious are (1) the school system, and (2) the modern lifestyle involving stress, sleep deprivation, multitasking, and cognitive overload.

Learning is the acme of the evolutionary achievement in the area of neural computing. When powered by the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") and guided by the [pleasure of cognition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning"), learning is safe and it is a great neurotrophic and neuroprotective tool. However, the school system perverted learning in that it employs coercive measures that turn learning, literally, into a brain damaging activity. Even worse, in the quest for more and better test results, schools go deeper and deeper into that dark quagmire. The end is near. Short-term damage inflicted by schools is not obvious, and is most apparent in various aspects of mental health, incl. depression. However, after many years of abuse, Alzheimer's is the ultimate and pretty obvious outcome of bad learning and bad cognitive habits. Those habits begin at school. For a highly educated patient, pathology may show up later, but its impact may be more pronounced and lead to [higher mortality](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Depression_in_highly_educated_patients_increases_mortality_of_Alzheimer%27s "Depression in highly educated patients increases mortality of Alzheimer's").

## How schools pervert learning

Schools contribute to neurodegenerative pathology by conditioning bad learning habits. Those habits are born in learning corrupted by the following factors:

- [coercive learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion_in_learning "Coercion in learning"), replaces [coherent knowledge structuring](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") with disruptive learning based on high [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") that accelerates [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting_curve "Forgetting curve")
- incoherent knowledge results in increased neural burden in knowledge processing during the learning process
- emphasis on short-term outcomes ([testing](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Testing "Testing")), undermines long-term outcomes (lifelong knowledge)
- low [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") of memories further contributes to undermining the [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") of knowledge and further overload injury
- all the above factors contribute to lower levels of knowledge, which in turn require more activity in new learning
- increased volumes of learning magnify the [network burden](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two-process_model_of_sleep_regulation "Two-process model of sleep regulation")
- [fidgeting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fidgeting_may_improve_cognitive_performance "Fidgeting may improve cognitive performance") might be neuroprotective, but it is usually disallowed or discouraged
- violations of the [circadian cycle hygiene](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle") result in [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") with [network unreadiness in the learning process](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_and_learning "Sleep and learning")
- shortened sleep takes away protective value of [memory optimization in sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_optimization_in_sleep "Memory optimization in sleep"). It also undermines [memory consolidation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_consolidation "Memory consolidation")
- [sleep deficits](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") contribute to the amyloid buildup in the brain
- [stress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stress_resilience "Stress resilience") in the wake of learning reduces neurogenesis, leads to brain cell loss, and undermines the neuroprotective value of learning
- [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory") magnify stress in the wake of learning, and undermine knowledge [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence")
- toxic memories lead to the [hate of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Why_kids_hate_school%3F "Why kids hate school), which results in learning in conditions of perpetual displeasure, which destroys the main factor that protect the coherent build up of knowledge: the [pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning")

In addition to cognitive aspects of schooling there are also negative influences in the area of overall health. Obesity or diabetes are risk factors in Alzheimer's and are well fostered by oppressive rules of schooling. Even PE classes that are to promote exercise will often have the opposite effect in discouraging exercise through their coercive nature, often executed in wrong circadian frame or in conditions of sleep deprivation. In the end, to get my point, all you need is just one peek at those sad faces of kids rolling slowly to school, and contrast them with those long-lived blue zone centenarians of Sardinia or happy nonagenarian nuns in a sunny convent garden.

The educational formula for preventing Alzheimer's is very simple: **==Good learning prevents Alzheimer's, bad learning contributes to Alzheimer's==**. In terms of Alzheimer's disease, learning can be a good guy or a villain depending on the respect for the **[Fundamental law of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fundamental_law_of_learning "Fundamental law of learning")**. As for the absence of learning, its main contribution to Alzheimer's is that lower knowledge leads to higher processing burden in all cognitive tasks.

I do not recommend Elon Musk's formula for creativity: *"you need to think until your brain hurts"*. I have no doubts about Elon's creative genius, however, his phrasing is just unfortunate. He probably meant, *"you need to think about your problem from morning till evening"*, to which I would add *"and in sleep too"*. However, [mental hygiene](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle") is as vital for a creative breakthrough as it is for brain health. These days, for genetic reasons, even Bill Gates worries he might be at risk of Alzheimer's. Bill is an avid reader, and good reading is a great preventive measure. However, Bill also used to be a crazy workaholic with a combative streak and a degree of sleep abuse, as well as insufficient respect for physical exercise. Neither Elon nor Bill should be used as best models for a healthy use of the brain, even if they managed to drive their brains to genius level performance.

If you take correlations between the years of education and the incidence of Alzheimer's, the reality may easily hide amongst the data. For starters, cognitive capacity correlates both with one's academic performance and prospects for fending off Alzheimer's. Illiteracy leads to faster diagnosis. Finally, even the worst school will have some contribution to learning and overall knowledge. A tiny bit of good learning in a mass of bad learning may still bring a positive net outcome. Despite all my dramatic depictions, the data may still make us reassured that schooling is good and no change is necessary. In the meantime, **we are squandering an immense global asset: youth and its unmeasurable creative potential**.

## Memory overload in Alzheimer's

In 2000, Robin P. Clarke wrote in Medical Hypotheses that [network overload in learning can lead to Alzheimer's](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Network_overload_may_lead_to_Alzheimer%27s "Network overload may lead to Alzheimer's"). I [criticized the hypothesis](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bad_learning_contributes_to_Alzheimer%27s "Bad learning contributes to Alzheimer's") for it ignored the value of good learning, which might be one of the best preventive factors. However, more and more evidence shows how an incomplete theory can make a major contribution to science. Clarke is little more wrong about Alzheimer's than Newton is wrong about the laws of motion.

New [computational models](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Brain_models_confirm_vulnerability_of_hub_regions_to_Alzheimer%27s_pathology "Brain models confirm vulnerability of hub regions to Alzheimer's pathology") seem to confirm what we always thought was the case: the damage in Alzheimer's progresses from areas that are most burdened in heavy cognitive tasks that rely strongly on working memory. No peer review journal would accept a thesis that schooling may be a cause of Alzheimer's. The chain of causality is a bit too long. However, the chain is also pretty obvious. For that you need to keep in mind:

**Bad learning habits fostered by schooling underlie cognitive maladaptations in adult life, and might be the chief behavioral cause of Alzheimer's**

In his writings, Clarke mentioned TV viewing as leading to memory overload. In that sense, sheer information load cannot be a hallmark of cognitive overload. For example, all-night cramming for a test is far more injurious. There is hardly any excess cognitive burden caused by watching TV. This is a self-regulating passive activity. In a tired state, we naturally narrow the channel and perceive less in a less creative manner with lesser degree of deep processing. We can easily watch TV in a semi-asleep state, register a mere 1-5% of the standard volume of information, and still comfortably fall into a nap in a warm armchair. No burden, no stress, no damage. It is the demands of schooling that provide no cognitive safety valve. Sleep deprivation, deadlines, and cognitive overload are the chief components of excitotoxic abuse.

There is a similar story with the modern lifestyle where knowledge plays more and more role at work, and may be obtained in conditions that are even worse than those in an average public school. We learn in a hurry, in stress, in overload, while multitasking and while being sleep deprived. Those factors are highly destructive and only a good happy evening followed by a full night of sleep can prevent chronic build up of injury.

I keep hailing [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") as the best way to [read](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Speed-reading "Speed-reading") and the best way to learn. However, beginners should be aware of the steep learning curve, and a degree of chaos that [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") may introduce to learning for an inexperienced adept. As in all other forms of learning, it is good to [start slowly](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading_step_by_step "Incremental reading step by step"), methodically, and in a [happy state of mind](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning").

[Knowledge is good](http://www.super-memory.com/articles/goodness.htm) on average, but learning requires a bit of deliberation to make it good too.

Healthy brain regimen should include: [free running sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_running_sleep "Free running sleep"), respect for the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle"), [pleasurable learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning"), and exercise.

Sturdy brain is the best insurance against senile dementia

## Switchboard metaphor

The switchboard metaphor makes it easy to understand the impact of schooling on the brain in a layman language:

The hippocampus and the surrounding structures take a heavy load of processing in associative learning. The hippocampus is like a switchboard. It connects various areas of the brain for speedy associations. Alzheimer's pathology can be explained with a **switchboard metaphor**. Excitotoxicity and neuronal death can be compared to a wire meltdown in an overheated switchboard.

Imagine a dense mesh of wiring that makes up your telephone switchboard. In an ideal case, you will want to connect just a few callers at the same time. If you get too much traffic, the switchboard can overheat, and, in extreme cases, overheating can result in melting down some of the wires.

In a genius mind, [fast thinking](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fast_thinking "Fast thinking") makes life easy. Only a few wires are needed to find access to golden abstract knowledge resources. Seemingly hard problems can be [solved fast](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem). The brain does not need to work hard. Knowledgeable thinkers are less likely to suffer from Alzheimer's.

In Down syndrome or in Alzheimer's, the switchboard is often overloaded. If a wire melts, other wires need to take more traffic. If the overload repeats, this can lead to a chain reaction. In a runaway situation, a regular exposure to heavy loads pops more wires up to the point of a collapse of the entire network. This kills the switchboard, memory, and cognition.

In the context of [schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_of_schooling "Problem of schooling"), the switchboard metaphor easily explains the damage caused by [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming"), [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation"), and other [bad habits conditioned at school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/50_bad_habits_learned_at_school "50 bad habits learned at school"):

- [coercion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion_in_learning "Coercion in learning") forces more calls than the switchboard would accept voluntarily
- [incoherent knowledge](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") is like a set of tangled connections that provide little relief to traffic
- short-term learning provides connections that fade in time and stop being useful
- [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability") provides connections that don't melt easily and are efficient in relieving traffic
- low knowledge equates to inefficient wiring that require more traffic per task
- increased volumes of learning are the same as an increased number of phone calls
- [fidgeting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fidgeting "Fidgeting") is a distraction that prevents some phone calls from being made
- learning in wrong [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") state is like doing phone calls in a switchboard that has already been overheated
- learning in [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") is like doing phone calls in a switchboard that malfunctions due to short-circuiting of irrelevant connections
- shortened sleep prevents nighttime rewiring by the maintenance crew that replaces faulty connections and looks for faster routes for reduced traffic
- amyloid buildup is like wires rusting and losing their conductivity
- stress increases voltage that increases the current and the rate of wire meltdown failure
- [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory") are particularly bad connections that result in futile traffic that leads to further traffic buildup
- [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory") magnify stress, which bad connections can trigger the failure runaway
- [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory") magnify the [hate of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Why_kids_hate_school%3F "Why kids hate school) as much as bad wiring discourages the operator from using the switchboard in the first place. This results in less build up of preventive knowledge

As much as all electronic equipment provides conditions of good use, the brain manual calls for brain hygiene that includes smart learning and plenty of sleep

## Summary: Schools and Alzheimer's

- the root neuronal cause of Alzheimer's is excitotoxicity, which comes from [excessive cognitive loads](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neuronal_activity_increases_amyloid_plaque_deposition "Neuronal activity increases amyloid plaque deposition") on networks with insufficient processing power
- good learning is preventive, while bad learning may contribute to Alzheimer's
- the best reassurance for the positive impact of learning on prevention of Alzheimer's is the adherence to the [Fundamental law of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fundamental_law_of_learning "Fundamental law of learning")
- schooling inherently violates the [Fundamental law of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fundamental_law_of_learning "Fundamental law of learning") through its [coercive nature](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion_in_learning "Coercion in learning")
- for many kids, schooling leads to neural injury, which may be instantly apparent, e.g. in developing depression
- despite all calls for reforms, mainstream schooling in the western world still keeps evolving in the wrong direction: more haste, more stress, greater loads, less coherence, and more neural injury
- main factors of schooling that lead to excitotoxic injury are: overload, low coherence, high interference, low retention, low [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), sleep deprivation, [circadian violations](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle"), [stress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stress_resilience "Stress resilience"), and [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory")
- bad learning and bad habits taken from school may end up in Alzheimer's pathology later in life
