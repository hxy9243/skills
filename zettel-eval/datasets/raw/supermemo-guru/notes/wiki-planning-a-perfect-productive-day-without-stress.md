## Planning a creative day

In a [creative](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") profession, there is a powerful tool that ensures high productivity and low [stress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stress "Stress"): the routine. The best creative minds in their best creative periods employ a [well-tuned daily routine](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Darwin_intuitively_followed_the_natural_creativity_cycle "Darwin intuitively followed the natural creativity cycle"). Such a routine must always be perfectly adjusted to the [natural creativity cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle") to achieve the best outcomes. Repeatability of the schedule makes it easy to make **minor** adjustments with **major** consequences. While a modern lifestyle can be visualized as a meandering trip on a bumpy road with frequent traffic jams, a creative lifestyle is more like a rocket where minor nudges in the trajectory ensure a steady course towards a remote target. [SuperMemo for Windows](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_for_Windows "SuperMemo for Windows") includes a schedule manager known as **[Plan](http://help.supermemo.org/wiki/Plan)**. This simple schedule manager provides an escape from the chaos of modernity. By protecting the creative lifestyle, **Plan** has a powerful impact on increasing productivity and reducing stress.

Some of the general ideas behind **Plan** can be implemented without a computer. If you do not want to use a computer or [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), read about the [Natural creativity cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle") and/or [How to solve any problem?](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem). Both explain how to employ the power of the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle") for maximum [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity").

## Central idea

The main idea behind **Plan** is to list what you want to do, and how much time you want to allocate for individual activities. The **Plan** will compress your desires into the frame of a waking day. It will help you fix the activities that must occur at a specific time (e.g. [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta")), and keep rigid the activities that cannot be compressed (e.g. a shower). It will also compensate for mishaps and irregularities that are bound to surprise you during the execution of the schedule.

## Circadian perfection

The biggest part of the magic of **Plan** is the adjustment of the schedule to the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle"). All learning, [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity"), [problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_solving "Problem solving"), writing, etc. must proceed immediately after a good night's sleep or a good [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta"). It is the first 3 hours after sleep that count the most. If that best slot is missed and creative pursuits are delayed, the efficiency drops, the pleasure plummets and the enthusiasm might be a third of what is possible. If kids could get to their own favorite learning in the morning, there could be smiles for the rest of the day. If they have to get up early and go to school, they hit the other end of the spectrum: torture! To an uneducated eye: these are just two forms of learning. In reality, very different processes occur in the mind that is fresh, ready, interested and eager.

That emphasis on starting days from creative productivity does not imply that later portions of the day are wasted or less fun. There are dozens of fun things that do not demand that crisp morning brain. There is exercise, socializing, family, making orders, DIY, social media, to-do chores, etc. A degree of [eustress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Eustress "Eustress") can then be dosed into the schedule to propel the mind. Finally, before sleep, there is still a great room for fun. When the brain is slowing down and not too happy to take on new intellectual challenges, passive learning is still an option. I love to listen to lectures and interviews in the last 2 hours before sleep. Listening requires no light and is great to keep a healthy [sleep phase](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_phase "Sleep phase"). News or movies are also fun. Even a novel is great for those who do not struggle with [phase delays](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Phase_delay "Phase delay") as a result of late night reading. The passive and undemanding nature of those activities is a perfect match for the time of day. Too many people these days, busy with work during the day, and busy with family in the evening, try to squeeze demanding activities into the time before sleep. Not only does it have a bad impact on the quality of sleep due to stress, bright lights, computer screens, etc., it is also a violation of brain hygiene, and an exercise in futility. The brain ready for sleep, shows a fraction of its ability to solve problems creatively. The brain can be flogged into action with the demands of life, however, the magic of Plan comes precisely from avoiding this type of displeasure. Activities must be matched to the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle")!

## Maximization of focus and energy

One of the main advantages of time-management techniques such as [Pomodoro](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pomodoro "Pomodoro") is that small blocks of time tend to improve focus and energy. This is particularly true of jobs that are less pleasant and can be tackled more easily if the end of the job is clearly marked on a timer. Timeboxing is a great remedy against [procrastination](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Procrastination "Procrastination").

**[Plan](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Plan "Plan")** makes it possible to adjust the length of slots to needs and capabilities. If the slot is increasing unpleasant and ineffective, it can always be shortened. It makes more sense to make little progress than to get stuck in a [procrastination](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Procrastination "Procrastination") loop. If focus on the job can be sustained for longer, e.g. due to an increased perception of [productivity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Productivity "Productivity"), slots can be increased incrementally.

## The value of 1 minute

The perception of the value of one minute of your life changes dramatically when using **[Plan](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Plan "Plan")**. If a friend calls you at 10 am and you have the whole day ahead, one minute does not feel like much. However, if a friend calls you in the middle of your 10 minute slot allocated to processing e-mail, you are likely to be quite upset. There is always more e-mail to respond to than time available in life. Every disruption adds to the backlog and stress. Tight schedules in **Plan** can be very stressful. They can even make you sick. It is only a matter of time before you learn all the tricks to avoid disruption. To avoid stress, you will quickly employ simple tricks like, for example, [[wiki-planning-a-perfect-productive-day-without-stress|turning off your phone]]. If you are on a **Plan** schedule, you value every minute of your time. After a bit of training, your stress levels go well below the baseline. Both you and the world will have been conditioned to respect your time. In the long run, **Plan** can actually reduce stress, and increase the fun of [productivity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Productivity "Productivity").

## No breaks needed

All productivity gurus tell you to *[take breaks](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pomodoro_technique "Pomodoro technique")* or *pace yourself*. This is not needed with **Plan**. This comes from the fact that there are only two types of breaks you really need:

- Breaks for sleep
- Breaks due to fatigue

Breaks for night sleep and a [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") are already wired in your schedule. They are part of your plan, and they cause no disruption.

Breaks due to fatigue can easily be remedied by a change of activities. If it is motor fatigue, you can easily rest by employing your brain. If it is brain fatigue, you can easily remedy it by changing the type of activity. If you get into a good rhythm, you will know very precisely when and what kind of fatigue shows up. You can then plan your day in such a way that one type of activity is a form of rest from another type of activity.

Naturally, there are also imperfect days and bad days. Those may call for some changes to the schedule on that particular day (including breaks).

## Imperfect days

In the ideal case, a good schedule should be executed step-by-step without modification and in allocated time. However, perfection can never be achieved. All days differ to a degree. Even the tiniest details of work that are part of the schedule can affect execution. High creativity may call for longer slots. If creativity does not come often, it is particularly precious. Low creativity may also change allocations to add to some less inspiring slot allocations. High creativity may delay sleep. This can cause [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") ripples and lower efficiency in the days that follow. There are always risks to health that never seem to be constant. Even a minor diet violation can make an impact. There are also other people and their needs. There are many defenses against imperfection. [[wiki-planning-a-perfect-productive-day-without-stress|Swapping activities]] is one.

Then there are particularly bad days, e.g. caused by bad sleep, bad health, bad mood, etc. If fatigue comes faster, the remedy I recommend is to speed up the schedule (to provide more room for "lazy" slots that come later).

## Speeding up the schedule

On a bad day, when fatigue comes faster, you can try to speed up the schedule. Instead of 90 minutes of reading, you can do 30 minutes of reading. This approach will decompress later activities, which are usually less demanding, or more energizing. You can then spend more time in those later activities, or more time in activities you particularly enjoy. Alternatively, you can just go to sleep a bit earlier. Inserting a not-so-productive "break" activity, e.g. "doing social media", "browsing gossip websites" should be the very last resort. You could still aim to insert something of value: yoga, exercise, meditation, etc. You know best what works for you.

## Swapping activities

Drag and drop makes it easy to swap slots without changing the total allocation of time devoted to individual activities. Such an operation can be helpful in case of temporary setbacks such as:

- **Circadian mismatch**: even the best schedule can be messed up by circadian irregularites. Minor shifts to schedule sequence resolve the problem, e.g. by pushing a more stressful slot into a low-energy period
- **Homeostatic fatigue**: prolonged learning or high creativity may result in homeostatic fatigue, which can be remedied by advancing low-priority high-fun slots
- **Health setbacks**, e.g. a stomach ache may call for stretching on a sofa, which may also be the time for a phone call or whatever else can be done while lying flat

Naturally, those are only minor and temporary setbacks that are unavoidable even in the best regulated lifestyle. A bigger problem comes when there are bad days of bad health or bad mood. Even then **Plan** can come to some help. You may think *"this is the day when I will get nothing done. I am just not in the mood"*. One peek at your schedule may reveal that you can still negotiate with your weaker self a slot that would be acceptable at the moment. It may turn out that this slot will just turn your mind. Little productivity brings more productivity. This is a self-perpetuating process in people who care about spending their lives on meaningful things. With **Plan** you should never face the option of inactivity or time-wasting.

## Smartphones

In [How to solve any problem?](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem), I concluded that distraction and multitasking might be the main reason why big-time problem solving is rare.

Friedrich August Kekulé needed a tiny conceptual step to realize that a benzene molecule is circular. The difference between a sequence of atoms and a ring seems trivial. However, for the brain it is often extremely difficult to jump away from a local minimum of an established concept. In Kekulé, sleep provided the seed for a breakthrough. Now imagine Friedrich August sitting down to his breakfast with an "itching idea" on his mind, only to be disrupted by a phone ringing from his friend with some celebrity news. Luckily, in 1865, there were no phones.

Smartphones are a blessing, but they should never be creative work disruptors. **Plan** teaches you respect for every single minute of your day. It also teaches others that you exercise that self-respect. Is it possible that after many months of being turned off, your phone will never ring during your creative hours? All your friends and family will know this is not the right time.

## Emergencies

Emergencies and disruptions are hard to avoid. If you did not listen to my advice about the phone, or, at the top pro level, you just need to [[wiki-planning-a-perfect-productive-day-without-stress|go to the bathroom]], you need to insert an extra activity in the schedule. If this is just 5 minutes early in the day, you can easily insert a new slot and the remaining slots will be compressed accordingly. You may lose mere seconds of your most important activities. If you insert an emergency slot late in the evening, the compression may be more painful, and you may lose a shorter activity slot or two. For insertion during longer activities, you will need the **Split** option to cut activates into sub-slots.

If you keep too many rigid slots that cannot be compressed (R), even minor emergencies can wreak havoc in the schedule. If you insert emergencies shortly before a fixed slot (F), the compression may be so severe that you will lose a slot (or need to move it beyond the fixed activity). All those little inconveniences have a wonderful side effect: you condition yourself to avoid emergencies. **You build respect for every minute of your life!**

Emergencies can ruin the schedule. A typical **Plan** dropout mechanism is to spend too much time on morning slots or insert too many emergency slots. In the end, the second half of the day never gets executed. This defeats the purpose of the **Plan**. If this happens to you on a regular basis, the **Plan** is, sadly, not for you. Some [self-discipline](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Self-discipline "Self-discipline") is necessary until the plan perfectly accommodates your needs and compensates for your weaknesses. It makes sense to keep your slots long and relaxed at the beginning.

It is easy to have a morning impression: *I have many hours for work today*. It is also easy to realize in the evening: *the whole day passed, and I got little done*

## Bathroom break

If you record a bathroom break in your schedule, you may wonder if this is an extreme case of quest for productivity where perfection actually leads to futile optimizations. There have been cases of extreme OCD documenters who have recorded all their bathroom breaks for decades. Their statistics have been adorned with picturesque descriptions.

If your bathroom break follows a specific ritual, you should definitely have a slot dedicated to that particular ritual or its components. However, in case of **Plan**, even an unplanned emergency bathroom break slot may actually make sense. Inserting a slot in **Plan** carries a cost of some 4 seconds. Splitting a longer slot with a new activity takes roughly 11 seconds. If you plan for extreme productivity, you must also employ extreme precision measurements. To plan the future, you need to know your past. One of the most precious features of **Plan** are statistics. They make it possible to compare your effort on a monthly or annual basis.

I document all the time I spend writing articles like this one. This helps me understand how much writing I can afford in a year, and what outcomes I can expect from such efforts. If I happen to have a 40 minute slot devoted to writing, and this slots gets interrupted by a 7 minute toilet break, I end up with 33 minutes done. This could result in a 21% overestimate, which is a whopping number to my standards. Check your numbers, and bathroom breaks may actually take up more time than you spend jogging or swimming. This could only mean that you do not take those forms of exercise seriously.

Moreover, schedule optimization is based on **Delays**. This is to make sure that there is sufficient room for planned activity, and, importantly, sufficient freedom for executing the schedule in a healthy frame of mind. **Plan** is about harmony, not about the [stress of productivity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Harms_of_self-discipline "Harms of self-discipline"). Minor exceptions, such as a bathroom break, should be reflected in the lax setting for the duration of individual activities. If I did not have that room, I might often be forced to skip answering e-mail because of running out of time before exercise.

Last but not least, the toilet might be the last refuge for paper books. You can easily read several books per year by just reading during toilet breaks. That should count for productivity too. Even productivity freaks should take it easy. The bathroom is not a place to hurry.

## Oversleeping

There is no room for the concept of "oversleeping" in a creative lifestyle. For maximum creativity, sleep must be natural. As such it cannot be planned for a specific waking time, or interrupted with an alarm clock. It is possible to aim to wake up daily at 8:00 am, but natural sleep will rarely terminate at the desired time. I have documented cases of people who have an uncanny ability to wake up with just 2-4 minute variations from day to day. However, these are elderly people with perfectly polished rituals. Explosive creativity quarrels with regular sleep. If you hit on a great idea, it is normal to have your sleep delayed and/or shortened. I have no doubt that creativity contributes to the [DSPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/DSPS "DSPS") epidemic. **Plan** makes it easy to handle irregularities in sleep. If you wake up late, you just adjust your schedule accordingly. You do not "oversleep". You just sleep a bit longer, set the new start time, and the schedule will adjust accordingly. In case of circadian irregularities, with some experience, you can also adjust schedule length (e.g. adding minutes on a great day in advance or cutting the schedule short when high sleepiness arrives in the evening).

## Time anchors

The concept of fixed activities in **Plan** can be used to schedule appointments and other events that must happen at a specific time. Fixed activities are generally not welcome. Life is most pleasurable and productive when it is free of disruptions, obligations, appointments and deadlines. However, there is still a need for fixed activities in a perfect lifestyle without disruption. Some fixed activities may play a role of **time anchors** that make sure that rampant creativity does not interfere with the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle").

**Time anchors** make sure you finish specific activities at the desirable time. Even if you believe you are on a path to a great creative breakthrough, it makes little sense to proceed at the cost of sleep or circadian harmony. The best strategy for interrupting creative work is to document the point of progress and take a break. Creativity delayed for the purpose of sleep is creativity doubled.

**Time anchors** can delineate vital components of the schedule that underlie circadian harmony. For example: exercise slot, [siesta slot](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta"), sleep-protecting [evening routine](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem), etc. Naturally, the most important anchor is bedtime. In **Plan**, the bedtime is always determined by the optimum length of the waking day, which you will easily determine after just weeks of experimenting (e.g. with the use of [SleepChart](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SleepChart "SleepChart")). An optimum length of the waking day is essential for the quality of sleep. If the day is too short, you risk [insomnia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Insomnia "Insomnia"). If the day is too long, you risk [DSPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/DSPS "DSPS") or [sleep deficit](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deficit "Sleep deficit").

If you wake up late (or early), all your **time anchors** can be shifted in **Plan** so as to minimize the impact of the variation. Late waking can be handled with **Shift in proportion to time left**. This produces diminishing shift for anchors occurring later in time. This is the way to ease your whole schedule back to your circadian optimum. Naturally, longer sleep hurts morning slots the most. If you wake up early, **Shift all equally** may be welcome if early waking is a rare occurrence that stabilizes the cycle.

## Plan is prison

Many great people I admire, e.g. David Deutsch, [Tom Durrie](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Tom_Durrie "Tom Durrie"), or Nassim Taleb say they do not like schedules because they feel like prisoners of timelines. They have a knack for organizing their days on instinct. The boundaries of plan and instinct can be blurry. Did Charles Darwin follow a schedule or was the schedule the reflection of his natural needs (see: [Darwin's day](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Darwin_intuitively_followed_the_natural_creativity_cycle "Darwin intuitively followed the natural creativity cycle")).

We are all prisoners of greater forces such as winters, nights, or the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle"). We need to economize time for sleep, meals, and exercise. Poor timing has a dramatic impact on efficiency and health. I have many personalities during the day and can adapt the schedule accordingly.

To maximize productivity, I place all learning, creative and problem-solving tasks in a protected zone in the morning (see: [My morning routine](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/My_morning_routine "My morning routine")). To make myself a bit more aggressive when arranging little business issues (e.g. broken chair, or a faulty router), I push the tasklist slot right before the exercise slot when I am always worked up and always in a hurry. This helps the action without affecting morning creativity. In a similar fashion I stick to [optimally timed meals](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Optimum_diet "Optimum diet"). I maximize speed and [eustress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Eustress "Eustress") for e-mail. I maximize sleepiness right before [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep"), etc. See: [[wiki-planning-a-perfect-productive-day-without-stress|Circadian perfection]].

On the other hand, I avoid interfering factors such as deadlines, scheduled meetings, phone calls, conferences, unexpected visits, etc. Those are true forms of enslavement! My formula is to stay free from factors I can eliminate (e.g. phone calls). There are however factors that are part of our biological design: sleep, meals, exercise and, most of all, [natural creativity cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle"). Those better be respected.

It does not mean I do not meet people. I like to convert meetings to walk-talk brainstorming sessions in the right circadian frame. As for deadlines, I prefer the "good enough" criterion for finishing all projects. Perfectionism is a [bad school habit](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bad_school_habit "Bad school habit").

In 2022, I actually observed that **Plan** is closer to liberty than the life with relaxed schedule rules (see: [Cost of modern lifestyle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cost_of_modern_lifestyle "Cost of modern lifestyle")).

## Plan to Enlightenment

The main source of unhappiness in society comes from limits on individual freedoms. In childhood, those limits are imposed by parents and by [compulsory schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling "Compulsory schooling"). In adulthood, [modern lifestyle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Modern_lifestyle "Modern lifestyle") and self-imposed limits conditioned in childhood are the main culprits. This global unhappiness results in [reward deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Reward_deprivation "Reward deprivation") that leads to [depression](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Depression "Depression"), [addictions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Addiction "Addiction") and even suicide. Dr Andrew Huberman expressed the problem of [reward deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Reward_deprivation "Reward deprivation") in a neat formula: *"addiction is a progressive narrowing of the things that bring you pleasure"*. That formula is also true for stress [resilience](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Resilience "Resilience") that guards against depression.

Interestingly, Huberman cautiously added symmetry to his well-documented claim by saying *"the opposite is Enlightenment, which is the progressive expansion of the things that bring you pleasure"*. This hints that **Plan** might be an essential component of my own personal Enlightenment (not necessarily easy to reproduce). Nearly all things in a schedule can be pleasurable. However, they need the right context and the right duration. Only wild creativity based on a passion may drive the entire day of hard work without a plan. However, all problem-solving hits obstacles, periods of stagnation and failure. The entire constellation of little productive pleasures is a good emotional buffer for periods of lesser success. In my own schedule there are several diametrically different areas of creativity that live in **synergy without synchrony**. A failure in one area can easily be compensated by success in another area. I do even swap creative slots in case of the arrival of a major idea. This serves maximization of the creative effect. A few days later, the same creative slot may bring stagnation, and the swap can be reversed.

The thing I might not have fully appreciated is the power to continually re-learn the value of individual activities. This occurs via re-adaptation. If I improvised and became monothematic, I might gradually unlearn the fun of those things that I keep neglecting on a daily basis. **Plan** prevents that. It would be very easy to unlearn the fun of exercising by just not exercising, and losing all the necessary adaptations that make exercising fun. The same refers to writing, programming, e-mail, an evening routine or even a well-timed meal ritual.

## Origins of Plan

As soon as **Plan** showed up in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") some two decades ago, it was hailed a "ballast option" incongruent with the program. In contrast, I have always been an enthusiastic user. In the entire year 2017, there wasn't a single day that I would "improvise". This millennium, I occasionally skipped **Plan** -ing only while on vacation away from the computer. Even then, I would run my schedule from "muscle memory", i.e. I would follow a similar circadian ritual with a book in place of incremental reading, etc. Long before SuperMemo, I needed two main things to improve productivity: (1) a well organized day, and (2) better memory. With my first computer, ZX Spectrum, I took on **Plan** first. I started programming in 1985, i.e. before even having the microcomputer on my desk. In those days, **Plan** was computationally too difficult. I tried to match the algorithm to the mess in my life. I wanted the computer to organize the mess hoping to extract more value. ZX Spectrum turned out too slow for that idea. A good schedule required 2-3 hours of calculations and I could hardly afford 10 minutes in the morning. A decade later, I was smarter in understanding that eliminating the randomness of "mess" in life is key to solving the problem of low productivity. Today, **Plan** mutated into a simple tool for proportional allocation of time. The complexity is gone. As for [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), it had to wait until I had [my first PC with a floppy disk drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_1.0_for_DOS_\(1987\) "SuperMemo 1.0 for DOS (1987)") (360 kB). To keep things in memory, I first needed non-volatile memory in the computer. Until now, **Plan** could have lived as a standalone application. This hopefully will change. As much as I needed [SleepChart](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SleepChart "SleepChart") integrated with [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") to look for correlates between [sleep and learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_and_learning "Sleep and learning"), **Plan** is needed for the analysis of circadian aspects of productivity

## Evolution of Plan

The evolution of my thinking about productivity passed the following stages:
- 1976 (aged 14) - 1980 (aged 18): my productivity was driven by the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), interest in sports, etc.
- 1980-1983: due to school and college obligations, exhausting schedule, and a resulting decline in the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), I started noticing that I do not live up to my potential. There was a growing gap between: *Who am I?* and *Who do I want to be?*
- July 1983: starting a progression towards higher productivity with a set of simple micro-rules such as *30 min. of learning every day*, *10 min. of exercise every day*, etc.
- 1983-1990: systematic progress in tightening the rules of productivity, increasing the compulsory quotas for learning, exercise, improving sleep, etc.
- 1991-1993: due to work obligations, exhausting schedule, and a resulting decline in the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), I was increasingly unhappy with my productivity
- 1994: starting work along a fixed whole-day schedule (e.g. 8:00 get up, 9:00 repetitions with SuperMemo, etc.)
- 2000: starting flexible schedule in [Plan](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Plan "Plan"), which was a true breakthrough for me
- 2000-present: high productivity based on [Plan](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Plan "Plan")
The overarching conclusion that seems to emerge from the above history is that the progression from low to high productivity must be incremental. It pays to start small and to make progress relentlessly. This makes it possible to transition from a life based on self-discipline to a harmonious life based on the fun of doing good things
