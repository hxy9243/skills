## Hard data and progress

A demand for hard data can be a powerful inhibitor of progress in science (see: [100 bad habits learned at school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/100_bad_habits_learned_at_school "100 bad habits learned at school")). We would all love to reduce science to perfect deductive reasoning and answer questions like we solve mathematical equations. However, where data is scarce, even the [flimsiest anecdote](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Democratic_school "Democratic school") can provide a seed of an new idea that will provide a skeleton for a new [model](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Model "Model") that will lead to a paradigm shift. This is why the modern take on [peer review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Peer_review "Peer review") is a strong inhibitor of free thinking in science. Luckily, we have an increasing number of journals that focus on [scientific hypotheses](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Abstract_knowledge "Abstract knowledge"). Now we also have free speech on the net that no skeptical mind should fear. My favorite example speaks of the impact of [*excess learning* on Alzheimer's](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bad_learning_contributes_to_Alzheimer%27s "Bad learning contributes to Alzheimer's") (2002)(compare a decade later: [2012](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Brain_models_confirm_vulnerability_of_hub_regions_to_Alzheimer%27s_pathology "Brain models confirm vulnerability of hub regions to Alzheimer's pathology")).

## Coercion causes walking problems

You do not need much grounding in neurology to get a grasp on the problem of [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia"), dysgraphia, dyscalculia, stuttering, and other similar handicaps with neurological basis. All you need is a nice eye-opening metaphor. Pause and ponder: *Can coercion cause problems with walking?* Your first intuitive reply might be: *no way!*

Now consider that problems with reading can be as varied as problems with walking. As walking can be hindered by issues with knees or ankles, with tendons or ligaments, or with a shot of vodka, so can [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia") be a result of a myriad of underlying causes. These can be genetic in nature, result of a trauma, or a result of bad learning that can be remedied or prevented with good learning. Coercion can cause problems with walking too. Anyone with a tiny bit of social anxiety may recall that the brightest walking skills can disintegrate when a human being needs to walk in front of a major auditorium under a pressure to perform to perfection. Even the best gymnast or acrobat may walk as if she was put in an invisible straight jacket.

## Coercion in reading

Can coercion cause [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia")? Certainly coercion should be avoided in remedial programs for dyslexics, or kids with developmental delays.

Coercion, by definition, violates the [Fundamental law of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fundamental_law_of_learning "Fundamental law of learning")

It is only natural for a parent of a kid with problems to conclude that if little drilling does not help, more drilling might. This intuition is so powerful that even learning "experts" will tell you to drill your skills to fluency even if the procedure is boring or unpleasant. Robert Bjork will tell you that fluency is a very bad measure of progress in learning. He is right. Patient and spaced approach can do wonders. If pondering the roots of [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia") here helps relieve a coercive pressure on a single kid, it will be worth it.

Most dyslexics can recall stories from childhood when they were subject of ridicule. There is no way of knowing how those mental constraints affected their ability to overcome the problem, esp. that slow development is often a hallmark of extraordinary cognition. The same slow progress exposes kids to peer ridicule when expectation benchmarks are not hit (see: [Peter Gray's research](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Gray:_Mechanics_of_educational_dyslexia "Gray: Mechanics of educational dyslexia")).

By virtue of my job, I have seen, touched and documented thousands of cases of [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories") developed in various contexts. Coercion is always a perfect breeding ground for toxic memories. Toxic memories can produce reading difficulty symptoms like those you observe in [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia"). Every dyslexic knows that anxiety increases symptoms. For that, toxic memories associated with reading will worsen the symptoms as well. You can call it QED.

For details see: [Educational dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Educational_dyslexia "Educational dyslexia")

## Coercion and dyscalculia

In a chapter on [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories"), I provide an example in which a kid could not learn the sequence of months. This resulted in a great deal of stress, and pressure. Years later, she did not have problems with learning the same sequence of months in German. In the end, when confused about months in her native Polish, she would translate in her mind back and forth between Polish and German. This is a classic case of toxic memory that may masquerade as sort of *"menstrual dyscalculia"* (problem with counting months using their name labels). The funny name is mine. If there was a disease causing problems with month counting, that girl would get the diagnosis. In the end, all she needed to "recover" was time and space to do it on her own terms.

## Language-dependent dyslexia

We know that English is a nightmare for dyslexics. English-speaking countries have much higher incidence of [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia"). That should be reason enough to advocate for simplified spelling. When a case of a bilingual boy with monolingual [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia") was brought to world's attention by a publication in [Cognition in 1999](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bilingual_boy_with_dyslexia_in_English_only "Bilingual boy with dyslexia in English only"), scientists rushed to interpret language difference between English and Japanese. There is no doubt that English spelling, as much as schooling benchmarks, lead to a sampling bias in the diagnosis of [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia"). However, I would urge everyone to pay more attention to the family background of the boy in question. He was raised in an English speaking household. What is often seen as an advantage, highly literate parents, and mom who is a teacher, might have been the cause of extra pressure to perform early and perform well. The kid learned Japanese at school, which in this case might have been an advantage. Schools can exert toxic pressure, but nothing is tougher for the kid than an ambitious parent with great and well-intended plans for kid's future. At the age of 16, the boy was an excellent reader in Japanese, including syllabic Kana, and a case of severe [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia") in English.

There are many possible ways in which the reported case could be explained, however, few people would suspect that Polish and German are much different with respect to month counting as described [[wiki-can-coercion-cause-dyslexia-3f|here]].

## ADHD and dyslexia

There is an association between [ADHD](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/ADHD "ADHD") and [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia"). The suggested approach for ADHD kids is to remedy ADHD to relieve [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia"). The [democratic school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Democratic_school "Democratic school") approach is different in that it provides the relief from the burden of schooling instead. With a proven record of success in managing ADHD kids, we can show that at least a subset of dyslexics can benefit from eliminating the coercion factor. [Danny Greenberg](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Danny_Greenberg "Danny Greenberg") noticed no cases of dyslexia developing at [Sudbury Valley School](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sudbury_Valley_School "Sudbury Valley School")

## Late talking and dyslexia

There is an association between late talking, and [dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia"). It is therefore possible that for a subset of children, [slow developmental trajectory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Precocity_paradox "Precocity paradox") may augur well for the future cognitive potential. For example, if gray matter peaks late, it may show up as late talking, early reading difficulty, early problems with counting, etc. The same late peak may show in talented musicians, or gifted mathematicians. In other words, lateness should be not seen as tardiness. Instead of a remedy in the shape of early instruction, the focus should be on maximum caution against the possible development of [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories").

## Employ an expert

Expert mind is precious. Modeling based on expertise can help overcome a shortage in data. It can make fast evaluations and often be able to explain why. It is easy to explain on an example. An atheist or a believer can easily be rendered speechless when criticizing or arguing for the Bible. The question is: *"Have you ever read it to criticize?"* or *"Do you remember anything of what you read?"* or *"Can you provide any evidence?"*. An atheist might have had a peek at the Bible in childhood, draw a conclusion, remember the conclusion and forget its roots. In the course of life, he will reinforce the conclusion by listening to trusted experts, say Richard D. By the age of 70, an "expert in Bible" will be totally unable to provide good evidence for or against his own Biblical claims. All that is left in his memory are generalizations with temporal and probabilistic judgements based on memory [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). The system for expert decision is based on the exact same neural mechanism as [knowledge valuation network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_valuation_network "Knowledge valuation network"). The output of the system is simply a probabilistic evaluation of a hypothesis. Availability of new evidence will clash with confirmation bias, but can, in the end, overcome the most [entrenches memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability"). A good expert, like [Peter Gray](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Peter_Gray "Peter Gray"), should be employed to speak about his intuitions.

Hard data is useful, but lack thereof does not render a good mind impotent

## Update 2024

In 2024, I actually have lots of data from [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). In short, as expected, the data reveal that for a "dyslexic", certain simple words or syllables can form a [toxic mix](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory") that becomes virtually unmemorizable (dozens of repetitions fail to prevent [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting")). At the same time, the data demonstrate no memory deficit, no mnemonic impairment, and even within the domain of print, memories form effortlessly as long as they are not presented in the "toxic" context (for example, if reading occurs in another language, such as Polish, English or Chinese). For more see: [Don't teach your child to read](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Don%27t_teach_your_child_to_read "Don't teach your child to read").
