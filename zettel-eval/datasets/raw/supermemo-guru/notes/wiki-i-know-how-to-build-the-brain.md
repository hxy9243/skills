## Intro

*" [I know how to build the brain](https://www.youtube.com/watch) "* is a podcast recorded on Oct 27, 2025, for [Pleasurable learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasurable_learning "Pleasurable learning"). [Palace](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Palace "Palace") invited [me](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Me "Me") to speak about [Algorithm SM-20](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Algorithm_SM-20 "Algorithm SM-20") ([spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition")).

I suggested that it would be more fun to talk about [SuperMemo World](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_World "SuperMemo World") 's plan for the nearest future. SuperMemo 20 API is scheduled for release in March 2026. In the meantime, already in 2025, we want to make first steps towards building an artificial brain! Naturally, this kind of project goes well beyond the scope of company's ability to provide research funding. This is why we plan to escalate with help from venture capital. At the bottom layer of the project, we want to implement a new type of neural network architecture that is supposed to make AI cheaper and smarter: [SireNNs](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SireNNs "SireNNs").

My impression is that Polish initiatives in the area of AI, with the bright exception of Eleven Labs, are underwhelming. We want to light up a new star!

As expected, half of the podcast was devoted to the credibility of Dr Wozniak! After all, [this blog](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_Guru "SuperMemo Guru") is chock-full of outlandish ideas, with an emphasis on the fact that few would pass [peer review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_with_peer_review "Problem with peer review"). I say, we can finally demonstrate that corporate research is a great alternative to academic research. The limitation of trade secrets is painful, but short-lasting in most situations. Limitations of [peer-review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Peer-review "Peer-review") and "peer pressure" of mainstream science lead to a "lemming effect" as in the case of [Dyslexia Conspiracy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia_Conspiracy "Dyslexia Conspiracy").

The limitations of [peer-review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Peer-review "Peer-review") and the *"peer pressure"* within mainstream science can foster a collective *"lemming effect"*, exemplified by the case of [Dyslexia Conspiracy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dyslexia_Conspiracy "Dyslexia Conspiracy").

The podcast is the first public mention of the *"artificial brain"* idea ([SireNNs.ai](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SireNNs.ai "SireNNs.ai")).

It seems, the most controversial point of the podcast is *"alignment is a non-issue"*. Here is then my quick explanation: [The inevitability of super-intelligence. ## Questions

### Neurocracy

- **Q:** At 18:25, [Woz](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Woz "Woz") says that *"the most compressed and efficient way of expressing human wishes. So the whole population can express their wishes"*. The problem is that people in general have really bad and poor wishes. So basically, the entire system will achieve very bad and poor things. And also, people can be easily manipulable, so I don't like a future where the true desires of the majority of people are realized
- **A:** [Neurocracy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurocracy "Neurocracy") is no democracy. When you say "majority", you bring back democracy. In the brain, it may be one single neuron that may fire a major decision, and that decision is networked. It expresses maximum competency. Individual neurons can make much worse decisions than humans (e.g. decisions that would result in instant death), and yet the brain is still a great example of smooth conflict resolution. I believe that [neurocracy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurocracy "Neurocracy") is inevitable due to the [force of intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/IVS "IVS"). Takeover by super-intelligence will be incremental and voluntary. All incremental decisions will be made by human individuals. The best way to like and love that future is to understand it!

### Magic formula

- **Q:** The claim that the core idea is both "simple" yet somehow missed by thousands of AI researchers worldwide requires extraordinary evidence. The history of AI is filled with "secret sauce" claims that often don't work
- **A:** You are right. However, until investors pitch in, the secret sauce will remain secret, and we will wait until someone invents it independently. AI researchers are busy with many innovations. There is not big loss to humanity by keeping it secret until we get a chance to forge a better path than current status quo

### Alignment

- **Q:** While Wissner-Gross's causal entropic forces are interesting, most AI safety researchers would argue alignment is far from a "non-issue". The transition from human values to superintelligent values isn't automatically guaranteed by intelligence seeking freedom
- **A:** You are right. However, the force of intelligence forms a very clear vector. Analogously, you could say that the second law of thermodynamics does not guarantee non-emergence of complex structures formed by intelligence!

### Knowledge Darwinism

- **Q:** While knowledge Darwinism is a compelling metaphor for human learning, translating it into artificial neural architectures that outperform today's LLMs is a massive engineering challenge
- **A:** That part of the podcast does not speak of what AI might do. It actually speaks of what AI can do already! Which was a surprise for many

### Peer review

- **Q:** While peer review has flaws, it remains our best mechanism for collective verification.
- **A:** I think our best verification today comes from AI. Individual human brains still push science more effectively with means other than peer review, which is slowly becoming a relic of the past

### Costs

- **Q:** Piotr says that this intelligence will be cheaper, but then says it requires to be attached to an LLM. So basically, this means that the cost will be an LLM + this artificial brain. So, the end result will not be cheaper compared to today AI that are only LLMs
- **A:** The brain architecture does the heavy lifting of reasoning and creativity, while the knowledge base is just cold storage accessed sparingly. Storage is cheap, computation is expensive. Imagine LLM as a neurally connected library that provides only the books that are needed to fill up critical gaps
