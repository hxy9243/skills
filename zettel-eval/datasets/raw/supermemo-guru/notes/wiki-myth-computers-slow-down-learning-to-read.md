## Myth

✗ Myth: **Computers and smartphones do damage to the reading skills of children**

## Fact

> Computers and smartphones increasingly infiltrate households. The age of initiation keeps decreasing. Psychologists and psychiatrists lament over the [negative impact of screens on child psychology and the brain](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dangers_of_imposing_screen_time_limits_on_children "Dangers of imposing screen time limits on children"). Every parent can see the negative impact of computers on reading. Children no longer seem interested in books. Some of them refuse reading books whatsoever. The more literate the household, the bigger the war about limiting access to computers. Nobody seems to question the fact that computers tend to replace books in the life of children. In 2020, if you ask a 7-9 year old about her reading skills and love for books, you will definitely see a clear contrast between children who do and do not have a computer at home. Very often, those without computers read fluently and even love reading.
> 
> Unfortunately, all the above true observations lead to a harmful and pernicious myth that computers slow down the progression of reading. The opposite is true. Computers help children learn to read on their own. This means that they foster the best ways of reading: semantic reading, which maximizes reading with comprehension. They are the best tool to prevent [educational dyslexia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Educational_dyslexia "Educational dyslexia"), i.e. a trouble with reading due to a fear of reading (induced at school). The only "problem" with computers is that they carry an opportunity cost. Children who play computer games, do not have time, patience or even curiosity to read books.
> 
> To understand the impact of computers on reading we need to put children in comparable situations. We cannot deprive one group of the technology, subject them to a coercive reading curriculum, and claim that without computers reading is easier. The best litmus test comes in [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning"). Dr [Peter Gray](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Peter_Gray "Peter Gray") who monitors all developments in the world of [unschooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Unschooling "Unschooling") and [democratic schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Democratic_school "Democratic school") noticed that in conditions of freedom, computers tend to decrease the age of reading. In [democratic schools](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Democratic_school "Democratic school"), the age of reading falls on a bell curve between 3 and 15 for a vast majority of readers. In recent years, that age seems to be decreasing.
> 
> In [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning"), children often pick up reading late. They learn to read on their own schedule. They never learn to read if they do not want to. Computers affect the reading environment. They naturally introduce children to the world of text. The scorn of young boys for book reading is understandable: *"why bother with books if I understand all texts with the help of icons?"*. The same boy who might prefer long hours with trucks and Lego is now more likely to transfer his interests to a computer screen which is jam-packed with text messages that show up in the dumbest games in the market. When children get a smartphone, they quickly get down to texting. They do it often long before they learn to read. They write with voice. However, the smartphone still provides feedback by converting voice to text. The mere act of texting and communication should give educators a long pause: technology brings the world of learning to a child's fingertips

Access to computers and smartphones makes it easier for children to learn to read on their own
