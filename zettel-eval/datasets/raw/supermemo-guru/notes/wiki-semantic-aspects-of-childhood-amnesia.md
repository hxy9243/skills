## New data on the futility of early instruction

In this text, I will argue against early academic instruction, and present novel evidence in favor of [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning"), and its critical role in early development. We have known for a while that early instruction may actually slow down development, and children who start formal instruction late usually catch up with their peers only to surpass them in the long-run. Many teachers who work with little children have solid and healthy intuitions about the futility and harm of coercion. However, on a grand scale, for cultural reasons, they still support [compulsory schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling "Compulsory schooling"), which breeds bureaucratic machinery that leads to the enslavement of children at school and within the confines of the [curriculum](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Curriculum "Curriculum"). Instead of calling for a major reform, teachers may good-heartedly favor easing children gently into the process of formal learning. Little do they realize that this is a form of assistance in sending kids onto a slow descent into [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness"). This is the first step towards forming a rich bank of [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories") that kill the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") and the [joy of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Joy_of_learning "Joy of learning") for many years of adulthood. Sometimes for life. This is also a process that undermines societal intelligence on a massive scale.

Using data from [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), I show that a [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") may protect memories in the period of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia"). This should not be hastily interpreted as a possibility of efficient early instruction. The data clearly shows the futility and the high cost of [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning"). Instead, we can see that [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") may be particularly important in early years of brain development. Early in life, [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") is best accomplished by play or following natural passions (as determined by the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") system). The value of [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") looms large for the following reasons:

- it is the only form of learning in full compliance with the [Fundamental Law of Learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fundamental_Law_of_Learning "Fundamental Law of Learning")
- it generates the rudimentary [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") necessary for further learning, incl. high-level [abstract knowledge](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Abstract_knowledge "Abstract knowledge") at later stages
- [I](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Piotr_Wozniak "Piotr Wozniak") hypothesize, it may also provide specific molecular and neural mechanism for preserving early semantic memories, where [memory coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_coherence "Memory coherence") determines the layout of early connectivity in the developing brain (possibly with the involvement of sleep)

On average, children may hit their peak in the number of synapses in the brain at the age of 5-7 with boys peaking a bit later. By that time the brain size might be 90-95% of the adult brain, and the brain peaks at around 15,000 synapses per neuron. However, peaking late gives the brain some more time to grow and to optimize its structure before massive pruning begins. Slow growing male brains will continue their advantage in synaptic density throughout the rest of life. White matter highways may have a strong impact on who we become and how our preferences shape in adulthood. Possibly, it is at this stage that our trajectory cone gets narrower. We retain the potential for genius, however, genius is rarely universal. This formative stage may affect our ultimate destination, occupation, or passions. As much as early neglect can mark the brain for life, early errors in educational strategy may affect the brain and intelligence for years to come. Instead of teaching, we should let kid brains grow free.

Early instruction may provide a semblance of learning, while affecting passions, preferences, and brain potential for decades

## Learning difficulty in childhood

Understanding biological, semantic, and metacognitive aspects of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") could save millions of children from unnecessary distress. It could also shelter the young generation from psychological injury that can span a lifetime.

It is a very sad picture that repeats from household to household for children in the age range from 2 to 10 or wider: a mom is trying to assist a little child in making progress with early academic instruction. For the mom it often involves tearing her hair out, or boiling inside, or falling into despair, or similar. For a child it is usually distress combined with slow progress that generates [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories") that will affect all future learning. For a teacher, a young child is often the cause of [Alfie Kohn's proverbial "toothmarks on the tongue"](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Alfie_Kohn:_Teachers_need_extraordinary_patience "Alfie Kohn: Teachers need extraordinary patience"). When dads get involved, things may get ugly.

The educational outcomes of early instruction range from bad to neutral. The neutral outcome is when a child is branded as a trouble kid or as an [ADHD kid](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/ADHD "ADHD"). As long as there are no drugs involved, the distress prevails on the adult side. Trouble kids have good defenses. Less often, precocious children love learning and cherish every opportunity to interact with a parent, or please the tutor. The bad outcome is when a child enters a path of steady educational advance with a gradual progression of [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness"). This leads to a systematic sterilization of personality. There are no happy outcomes to [coerced instruction](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion_in_learning "Coercion in learning").

The root cause of all the trouble is the adult dream of educating literate and smart populations, and the mistaken illusion than the remedies applicable to the adult brain can be used at earlier stages of development, including toddlers with virtually no long-term memory due to [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia"). In the mad rush to higher intellectual achievements, for some parents the education begins in the womb with speakers blurting the educational content through mama's stomach before a functional ear can even take shape.

All teachers and parents who fail to make little children learn should realize that it is not the little brain that is limited in its capacity, but the adult brain that shows the ignorance of the effective learning procedure. Children are innocent. Adults are guilty.

Adult ignorance of brain science explains why children fail to learn

## Three aspects of childhood amnesia

Frankland's hypothesis on the origins of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") is that prolific neurogenesis hinders the establishment of long-term memories. This hypothesis keeps garnering evidence. In one of his seminal experiments, Frankland slowed down neurogenesis and achieved better learning. This simulates the effects of [daycare](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Daycare "Daycare"): seeming acceleration due to stress (see: [Stress and neurogenesis](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stress_reduces_neurogenesis "Stress reduces neurogenesis"), and [Stress and learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learning_acceleration_via_stress "Learning acceleration via stress")).

I have no doubt that many aspects of brain development stand in opposition to memory formation. This is not only neurogenesis. In simple terms, long-term memories rely on [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability") and the younger the brain the lesser the stability of its structure and function. That instability favors [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") based on rampant [knowledge darwinism](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_darwinism "Knowledge darwinism"). As much as it is the case in machine learning, this dramatic case for "constructive destruction" underlies the foundations of human intelligence.

In this text I would like to provide some evidence for the dramatic impact of prior knowledge on the possibility of forming memories in childhood. Again the problem derives from a simple observation: the lesser the extent of prior knowledge, the lesser the opportunity to associate new pieces of knowledge with the existing semantic structure. Metaphorically, we speak of the awareness of our ignorance by using the phrase *the greater the clearing, the longer the perimiter*. The same metaphor applies to knowledge: the greater the bank of knowledge, the larger the surface for the [coherent](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") acquisition of new memories.

Last but not least, simple metacognitive skills such mnemonic tricks require a bit of training and exposure to [abstract knowledge](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Abstract_knowledge "Abstract knowledge").

For a child, structured learning is hard because of the difficulty in establishing long-term memories, the limited size of prior knowledge, and the limited set of metacognitive skills. In addition, all forms of acceleration in all three areas can be harmful. Accelerating brain development brings down the ceiling of growth (see: [Precocity paradox](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Precocity_paradox "Precocity paradox")). Speedy growth of knowledge may occur at the cost of [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") (and the associated risk of [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories")). Only the metacognitive aspect of development is harder to optimize. The main metacognitive risk is the serialization of the thinking process. When the creative aspects of learning are sacrificed to favor focused processing, vast parallel functions of the brain subsystems may remain underutilized. In the long run, richly branched structures working in parallel will appear more useful than simple [mnemonic anchors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mnemonic_anchor "Mnemonic anchor"). When a child becomes an adult, we want to maximize the intelligence (as expressed in [problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_solving "Problem solving") skills). We will care less about the ability to acquire further knowledge if its [abstractness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Abstractness "Abstractness") is poorly crystallized.

Children struggle with formal schooling due to (1) volatile memory, (2) limited knowledge, and (3) poor metacognitive skills

The simple rule of thumb that underlies all the above optimizations is to rely on child's own control mechanisms that optimize the acquisition of skills and knowledge.

Children should learn naturally via play. Their memories are in a flow, and all forms of academic planning are futile or harmful. Many European countries aim to lower the age of the onset of formal instruction. Poland is a nice exception as it has recently raised the age of [compulsory schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling "Compulsory schooling") from 6 to 7, which is **relatively** ok as compared with the rest of the industrialized world.

The simplest remedy for learning difficulties in childhood is to rely on inborn knowledge acquisition mechanisms. Children learn best in play

## Unified theory of childhood amnesia

All the mentioned aspects of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") may have a single natural cause: [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") of the [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") of the brain. Immature brain is volatile because of high plasticity and fast re-organization of the [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network"). Neurogenesis is a measurable component of that plasticity. Semantic knowledge is helpful, because only [coherent](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") knowledge is subject to effective [stabilization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stabilization "Stabilization") that may help memories survive for months or years (even at the times of rampant [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization")). Finally, metacognitive skills are based on the same [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") and reside in its more [abstract](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Abstract_knowledge "Abstract knowledge") layers. For that reason, they take longer to develop.

For more see: [Conceptualization theory of childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization_theory_of_childhood_amnesia "Conceptualization theory of childhood amnesia")

## Data: memorizing alphabet

I used to think that [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") was primarily neurobiological. However, recently I have reviewed a [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") [collection](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Collection "Collection") that opened my eyes to the possibility of the overlap between **developmental amnesia** and **asemantic amnesia**. The collection was about learning the alphabet. It was memorized by a toddler at the age of 1 year and 11 months. This is exactly the case were [SuperMemo **should not** be used](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_does_not_work_for_kids "SuperMemo does not work for kids"). This is the type of learning that I consider futile and possibly harmful. Good news is the kid enjoyed playing with the letters, and that my advice may not apply when well compensated by good parenting. An intriguing news is that the collection showed a stunning span of differences in memory performance in reference to individual letters. In short, the excellent outcome of not so excellent practice was for me to obtain a solid proof of **asemantic amnesia** (i.e. amnesia induced by inadequate [semantic and/or mnemonic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") of prior knowledge).

Four letters in the collection: M, A, S, and H were memorized once and never scored a failing grade until letter fluency became natural with early reading. In the first 6 years, these letter were reviewed from 33 to 61 times. This obviously implies manual intervention in the algorithm when performance was shaky and longer [intervals](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interval "Interval") might result in [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting"). Those four letters may falsely indicate absence of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") and readiness for learning the alphabet. This could be a dangerous conclusion. As it often is the case, hasty generalization promote further harmful effort to accelerate development. The earliest recalled episode from this particular child's life seems to have occurred in the 3 year of life. Seemingly good recall must definitely come from regular review in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), or regular contact with the four letters. This again might indicate SuperMemo played its role.

The conclusions will change radically when we observe that the letter Y was last forgotten at the age of 7, at a time when the whole alphabet seemed perfectly mastered. The reason for that lapse was interference with the letter U that was also forgotten at the late age of 6. This kind of interference can result in serious embarrassment in adulthood when a well-educated and smart individual may suddenly confuse things kids know from the first grade. Naturally, [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") has nothing to do with amnesia. This is natural part of remembering and forgetting. Only a regular use of the alphabet in reading make this kind of lapse unheard of in literate adulthood.

In our case we have a child who "learned" the alphabet at the age of 1.9 and still has not actually learned it at the age of 7. The use of [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") was rather futile. The distribution of the actual "learning age", i.e. the time when a given letter stopped showing memory lapses is pretty even. From perfection of M.A.S.H. at 1.9 to interference for U&Y at 7. This strongly indicates that the [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework"), or perhaps a rudimentary mnemonic framework must have served a survival of early semantic memories in the period of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia"). I have no way of knowing, but some of those early memories might have been [anchored](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mnemonic_anchor "Mnemonic anchor") in the context of that specific [collection](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Collection "Collection") (which was pretty colorful and mnemonic). If this was the case, the level of [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") might not be high enough to make any positive contribution to early reading.

The claim that semantic learning is possible in early childhood may seem like the most obvious thing. After all, a two year old may show an excellent ability to communicate. However, that ability may always be based on a regular exposure to specific semantic memories, e.g. the word "milk" may be perpetuated by daily consumption of milk. It is hard to know if the same networks and synapses are involved in that memory at two and then at four. If a child shows a perfect streak of recall from the ages 2 to the age of 7 on a symbol that is highly asemantic and in no obvious way different than other symbols in the same class, the preservation of memory seems unlikely to be a matter of chance. A more likely explanation is that those early memories found a permanent place in a child's memory. Those memories may survive for life given sufficient review (as implied by the [two component model of memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_component_model_of_memory "Two component model of memory")).

If memory survival in the span of 5 years was to be a matter of chance, we might see cases of random distribution of forgetting for low-lapse letters of the alphabet. Instead, we can clearly see the memory looking for an early [mnemonic anchor](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mnemonic_anchor "Mnemonic anchor") and surviving as of then. Among 1 lapse letters, X and O suffered a lapse within the first two days, and only B scored the first lapse after a month in the 4th repetition.

Further more, multi-lapse items show clear streaks of remembering vs failing. If frequent use was to explain recall, those streaks would not appear (unless there were major changes in lifestyle explaining exposure differences).

Long-term semantic learning is possible in the period of childhood amnesia

The distribution of the accumulated total of memory lapses clearly indicates that if the alphabet included more letters, the problem would get progressively worse. We might still have a small set of well remembered letters, while the rest would cause continual problem indicating semantic unreadiness of the mind to store the symbols in the long-term memory. There is little or no correlation between letter frequency and the ability to remember. The reasons for effective memorization of M.A.S.H. can only be explained by the possibility of hitting the right [mnemonic anchors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mnemonic_anchor "Mnemonic anchor") at the right time. This is a matter of luck. However, I might answer such questions more definitively only if I receive more similar collections (again, I suggest parents should rather not use [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") with little children unless the kids truly enjoy it).

## Semantic protection of memories

It is pretty obvious that a [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") is essential for early learning. Mnemonic or cognitive skills are underdeveloped, and cannot account for early semantic memories. However, dramatic contrasts between different pieces of knowledge of the same sort in the span of five years are an indication that an extra layer of value must stem from [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning"). I believe that it is the [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") that makes it possible for memories to survive the period of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia"). In other words, once memories are well-established, they receive special protection for the negative side effects of brain development. The protection from [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") via [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") is certainly present in adulthood, but the adult brain is far more complex to make the impact of the [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") that well visible. Moreover, I argue, that a great deal of "adult brains" are "damaged" by harmful habits inherited from the process of schooling. The heavy reliance on mnemonic and metacognitive skills will often make learning possible in cases were a child might only learn asemantically and have otherwise its memory reject the asemantic content. The mechanism of protection for semantic memories must obviously involve the mapping from the semantic network to the neural networks in the cortex. The activation of those network in use or in sleep must be the key area to investigate.

Semantic learning leads to memories that are likely to receive special protection that is of paramount importance in early learning

In contrast, we should see the opposite happen in [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning"): rapid forgetting. If rapid forgetting is a minor issue in a given individual, it would most likely be the result of precocity or early schooling, in which there is a heavy emphasis on coercion and early accountability. This in turn provides early masking metacognitive skills that turn [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning") into a form of mnemonic learning with all the hallmarks of [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming").

A **free** young mind will quickly get rid of [asemantic knowledge](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning") via fast-track [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting")

All the above mechanisms could explain a paradox in which kids who receive no early instruction tend to [surpass their peers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Early_math_instruction_may_backfire "Early math instruction may backfire") in a matter of several years. This phenomenon might have its contribution to a wider paradox of development: the [precocity paradox](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Precocity_paradox "Precocity paradox").

## Kids are NOT great at learning languages

A widespread myth says *"kids are great at learning languages"*. I enjoy bringing it up because there is no kid on this planet that might compete with me in the speed of acquiring a new language. I doubt I would enjoy a new language without [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), and the comparison would never be fair. However, mnemonic capacity of children is usually very weak, and cognitive strategies are usually barely there. Children are considered superior to adults in acquiring languages for the following reasons:

- critical period in the development of the auditory cortex, which makes them indeed superior in phonetic adaptation
- vast resources of time
- heavy reliance on [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") that leaves asemantic adults well behind

There is a critical period in the development of the auditory cortex. As a result, early exposure to a statistical bias in the auditory input characterizing a given language will determine the specialization of the cortex. This is why kids growing up in a specific linguistic culture seem to easily learn their native language while later struggling to acquire new languages without an accent. Dr Patricia Kuhl is one of the world leading experts in the field. Unfortunately, by generalizing her findings a step too far, she also has a hand in perpetuating the myth of the young fantastic language learning machines. Kids are deficient on many platforms in terms of language learning. Without the reliance on immersion, [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning"), and a heavy investment of time, they are definitely less capable learners than the adults.

Kids can master their native language at 10,000 vocabulary level within 2-5 years. This usually happens in conditions of heavy exposure that amounts to several hours per day. Exposure to two languages of two parents can easily lead to fluent compartmentalization and bilingual fluency. 10,000 words is also what we consider a basic benchmark in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") for memorizing vocabulary of a language within one year at just 30-60 minute cost. This naturally does not include other linguistic skills such as grammar or pragmatics that are best acquired with [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") that comes naturally with immersion.

The natural child-like approach to language acquisition via [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") is often destroyed in adults by the exposure to [schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schooling "Schooling"). Many adults suffer from language learning anxiety. They get paralyzed when they are to speak in public. They suffer immense torture if this is to happen in a second language. Those conditioned reflexes and behaviors are produced in the process of schooling (quick association with the trauma of the performance in front of the class). Kids have no fears. They absorb languages by pragmatic use, generalization of rules, and usage statistics that affect neural processing. If we set kids in competition with those "injured" adults, they are indeed superior (see: [Hating SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Hating_SuperMemo "Hating SuperMemo")).

## Example: memorizing foreign words

In this text, I documented an extreme example to illustrate how bad kids are at learning new languages using adult methodology massively deployed at schools: [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning"). We have a case of a child with excellent command of the native language, and extreme difficulty in learning a second language. The same child can show high intelligence, excellent comprehension, and high verbal fluency in his own language (well above the average), and the ability to learn a new language at the level that has earned him a nickname *"dummy"* at school.

I had a conversation with a lovely teacher in one of the neighboring schools. I struggled to convey the message: **the brain needs to be ready to receive information**. When it is too young, too immature, unskilled, or the information is wrongly formulated, no amount of repetition will help. In the end, I had to resort to a empirical demonstration.

I investigated a case of a 7-year-old who kept failing English tests. A typical test would require kids to learn 10 new words as homework, and provide written replies to the teacher during the test on the next day. To most adults this would be a piece of cake. Most parents think it should be a piece of cake for their kids.

While most children in the class scored 8-10 correct answer on a monthly test, the problem kid usually struggled at 4-5. Teachers would brand the kid as intellectually immature and showing some signs of ADHD (with no actual psychiatric diagnosis). To my eye, the kid was smart, perfectly healthy, hyperactive, and lagging developmentally. Lagging behind is not always a bad thing (see: [Precocity paradox](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Precocity_paradox "Precocity paradox")). Before one of the tests, the kid spent 2 hours learning 10 words with his mom. She tried all possible tricks in the book, incl. vivid mnemonics. When all words had been reportedly memorized, I used the [final drill](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Final_drill "Final drill") feature in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") to test the kid.

The period between learning and testing was just an hour. A queue of 50 items was engineered for the kid. The queue was made up randomly of 10 English words for the test (Polish: English), 10 passive mirrors of those words (English: Polish), and 30 words the kid had learned previously. After 20 minutes of work, only 8 items remained in the queue. All these items were the words required for the test. 1 item had English: Polish form, and 7 items had Polish: English form. This means that after one hour break, and the 20 minutes learning process involving interference from 30 old items, the child reverted back to 30% recall (failing to answer 7 out of 10 items). Some of those problem resulted from inter-item interference and the resulting confusion (peas vs. beans). Around the 15th round of final drill, the **child started showing signs of distress**. This is not a typo. 2 hours of learning and 15 repetitions may not be enough to memorize a word in a foreign language for a brain that is not ready. This would be the high time to stop. The whole cramming session should not even be attempted on an immature child. However, I explained it was an important memory experiment and we need to continue until one of the participants drops dead: me or the kid. Around the 30th loop of final drill, the child developed a metacognitive strategy. It would commit a word to short-term memory, and retain it by sub-vocalization until the word's turn came up in the loop. In other words, the kid would tackle one item at a time, while ignoring the rest of the loop. Such metacognitive strategies humans develop over the course the entire creative life. Many of those strategies become automatic and fluent. In this case, the child clearly realized that some of the words are unmemorizable. When the last word scored a pass, the test was over. The word "raspberry" had to be reviewed 35 times. One hour later, a quick test showed a recall of 50%. This means that the investment of 3 hours and the employment of SuperMemo ([final drill](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Final_drill "Final drill"), i.e. not [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition")), produced just 5 new words in child's memory. The next day morning, the words were reviewed again with mom before the test and the final score was 5 (i.e. 50%). Just enough not to fail the test. All the efforts by the teacher, mom, and SuperMemo were a waste of time. The same kid immersed in English, or while playing computer games, or watching YouTube, would soak in this knowledge effortlessly in due time. If not this year, then some other time (depending on the intensity of exposure). Before the brain matures, and cognitive strategies develop, the adult world is only causing distress to children. As stress tends to accelerate learning, we drive towards a premature arrest in brain development.

Similar scenarios repeat daily in millions of homes. A typical parent gets depressed, while a typical teacher gets red hot with fury (perhaps masking the emotion in front of the child). A child who can read those emotions may be progressing towards the development of [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories") (e.g. in reference to *raspberries*).

For this case, the correct interpretation is:

- child's brain is lagging developmentally behind the rest of his class, which is nothing to worry about in a healthy kid
- the child did not acquire metacognitive skills needed to commit complex abstract string of symbols for preservation in long-term memory (the recall in final drill is semi-automatic based on short-term memory)

It is important that I had no success communicating in English with any other kid in the class. Some kids were head and shoulders above my investigated case. One was even born in the UK. Kids knew some words, but had no skill to build a rudimentary sentence. After a year of regular drills, many could not even understand my question: *"Do you speak English?"*. Parents would ordinarily blame teachers. Instead they should realize that the same kid immersed in an English-speaking environment might be acquiring the same language at lightening speed as long as it had specific exciting goals to achieve.

When I relate the story to teachers or parents I get a whole array of explanations. Teachers lean towards "wrong methodology", "no teaching skills in mom", "misuse of technology" (i.e. SuperMemo), etc. Some hinted "the child must simply be dumb". Schools are based on similar assumptions about memory. A child overwhelmed with the volume of material to memorize at grade four, may live for decades with a sense of inferiority ("I am just bad at memorizing", or "my brain volume is not enough"). These do not even need to be toxic memories. These are self-imposed limitations that affect self-esteem. Even a happy intellectual may carry similar thoughts inherited from school times. Brain errors or memory lapses, which are normal for all brains, incl. full-blown genius, are taken as personal negative cognitive characteristic. This a limitation that may last a lifetime (see: [How school can ruin a life](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_school_can_ruin_a_life "How school can ruin a life")).

## Memory empathy for a struggling kid

For all the doubters lacking "memory empathy", I suggest a final drill with a fictional word pair. Using meticulous calligraphy, how many final drill attempts would you need to correctly write the following word in fictional language Chiruhitai:

```
English: destabilization
```
```
Chiruhitai: циษา关ภา于तत्स中文अर्ध维基фу百д科
```

To an adult eye this may seem far harder than a *raspberry*. However, this word was engineered to go around the average [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") of the adult brain. Individual symbols were taken from different languages to minimize cultural dependence. The length of the word is to make it harder to overcome the difficulty with metacognitive skills present in the adult brain. In other words, this word may help you feel the exact way an immature brain can see foreign words. With no metacognitive strategy, and no [mnemonic anchors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mnemonic_anchor "Mnemonic anchor"), the word is simply unmemorizable (in reasonable time). In similar fashion, many adults may be incapable of memorizing poems. They lack cognitive tricks needed to accomplish the task. The same seemingly less capable individuals can later be trained to memorized a deck of cards with the use of mnemonic techniques.

## Asemantic amnesia

Most children can master speech. This implies they are also capable of mastering a second language. This acquisition can proceed pretty fast through immersion (e.g. after moving to another country). This acquisition may be even simultaneous with the first language in bilingual families. Many children can learn a second language via gaming (esp. interactive team play). Some may even obtain a passive command of the language by watching movies (with or without subtitles).

Unfortunately, most of language learning at school is asemantic and utterly futile. A great proportion of teens after 12 years of learning a language cannot hold a basic conversation. Without student's own initiative, learning a language at school is usually pretty ineffective. Even fantastic teachers may not be able to remedy the situation (see [my own case in learning English](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schools_are_useless_in_teaching_English! "Schools are useless in teaching English!")).

If we employ coercion to induce [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning"), we can illustrate the utter failure of memory to hold on to the learned content. Once a child passes the stage of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia"), the flat bottom-level [forgetting curve](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting_curve "Forgetting curve") can only be explained by **asemantic amnesia** (i.e. inability to remember without an established [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework")):

## Adult empathy

Adults struggle to empathize with young brains. It is no coincidence that early learning gravitates towards [asemantic content](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning"). Letters of the alphabet, days of the week, names of months, multiplication table, etc. These are all asemantic concepts that get quickly filtered as hardest. This gives them the aura of primacy and primitiveness. They may be labelled as *basic toolset*. In reality, what a young brain needs is a rich [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") that establishes the need for, and the meaning of those primitive concepts. Only then learning may become semantic and produce desired results. A vast majority of adults, including good teachers, do not understand the need for [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning"). This is the outcome of the conditioning by the [Prussian system](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Prussian_system "Prussian system").

It is very easy for an adult to get a glimpse into a child's brain. Imagine a child who is to learn the names of the days of the week. This child has no scheduled events such us the Sunday mass, or Saturday soccer. For this child, there is less difference between Tuesday and Thursday than there is a difference between Ganymede and Calisto. To learn the names of the days is like attaching labels to vacuum. Entirely futile and asemantic. The adult may reason *"these are vital terms the child will need in her life"*.

To remedy the deficit in **[educational empathy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Educational_empathy "Educational empathy")**, the adult can imagine the year split into 36 ten-day periods with each period assigned a specific name: *Primedec, Secdec, Winterdec, Febdec,* etc. How about learning that sequence just for a test? Only 36 names (just to compensate for adult metacognitive superiority). All adults, with no exceptions, rejects such a proposition. This is exactly how a child feels. A bunch of names to learn with no content and no purpose! This is classic [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning").

The inability to empathize with a young brain is the main source of design errors in education systems

## Why schools do not work?

The entire school system is driven by the asemantic illusion. Here are some discussed myths in reference to early learning:

- **Myth:** it makes sense to teach a 1-year-old the alphabet. **Fact:** it is possible but utterly futile (and possibly harmful)
- **Myth:** it makes sense to teach a 2-year-old to read the Constitution. **Fact:** it is possible, but requires top-equality tutoring/parenting
- **Myth:** kids can memorize vocabulary with [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition"). **Fact:** without [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework"), this can be utterly futile (and possibly harmful)
- **Myth:** the earlier we start teaching, the smarter the kid. **Fact:** the opposite is true. Teaching should be replaced with the reliance on the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"). Future education will be based on [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning")
- **Myth:** breaks in learning favor good learning. **Fact:** the need for breaks may indicate violations of the [fundamental law of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fundamental_law_of_learning "Fundamental law of learning"). The natural end to learning comes with an increase in [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") and/or [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") components of sleep propensity. Consequently, the only break that is necessary is the one that is followed by [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep")
