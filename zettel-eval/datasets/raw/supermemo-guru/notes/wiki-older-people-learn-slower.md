## Summary

People learn all their lives, however, with age, it is getting harder and harder to change convictions

## Grandmother question

*M.M. and many others asked:*

Why is my grandmother so stupid? You can explain some stuff to her a dozen times, and she keeps forgetting. Is it Alzheimer's?

## Conceptualization curve

In the process of [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization"), the brain gradually moves from high plasticity to high stability. While a young brain soaks in patterns like a sponge, it also forgets at frustratingly high rates. On the other hand, the old age is associated with wisdom, i.e. a great deal of [applicable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicable "Applicable") knowledge of high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). This stability has a side effect: old brains do not easily accept new [models](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Model "Model") of reality. They tend to stick with models that have been stabilized with decades of pragmatic use.

It is not only grandmothers who are affected. When you stop learning new things, you stop reframing your understanding of the world, and the stabilization may progress faster. Fast stabilization can deprive the brain of the chance to change with the times. This is one another place where the effect of rigorous schooling may show up. If the same mantras and models are repeated for years of [coercive learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercive_learning "Coercive learning"), not only is it hard to undo erroneous patterns. The [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") is negatively affected. When the brain senses low [learntropy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learntropy "Learntropy") in the environment, the motivation to learn is reduced. With less new learning there are fewer sticky tendrils on the knowledge tree. At the age of 40, an individual might be free to learn without constraints, but substantively novel learning does not happen. [Prior knowledge](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Prior_knowledge "Prior knowledge") suffices to survive a day at work and at home. There is less need for new knowledge, less craving, and, sadly, less zeal for life. A schooled individual who loses her love of learning may fail to recover even in conditions of freedom. She is thus deprived of one of the most precious sources of happiness in adulthood: the drive to explore the world.

[Coercive learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercive_learning "Coercive learning") (schooling) accelerates progression towards [stabilized](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stabilization "Stabilization") (slow-learning) brain at advanced age

## Iron myths

Older brains are tougher to convince, and more prone to harbor persistent myths. There are some iron myths that I do not mind busting to the same people over and over again, independent of their age.

### Colds are caused by cold

An 8-year-old approached me recently: *"It is very dangerous to play football in shorts and T-shirt in snow. You can get seriously sick, Sir"*. There is nothing wrong with kids being wrong, because they are fast learners, and [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") or correction will come sooner or later. However, the same kid warned me about swimming in cold water a few month earlier. I explained the link between colds and viruses in both cases. The correction did not happen despite our conversations. The culprit is not a stabilization of knowledge in the brain, but the stabilization of collective knowledge in the culture. As much as brains stabilize, myths that are part of culture are almost impossible to dislodge by means other than decades of chipping tiny cracks from all directions. Today, even smokers agree that smoking is bad for health. This agreement took decades to find a good seat. Evolution and climate change are also gaining on social understanding.

I always provide the example of colds and viruses because it is a myth I seem to be combating on a daily basis. Older people are particularly stubborn. If the myth was true, I should be catching colds pretty often. The opposite is true (see: [Formula for common cold prevention](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Formula_for_common_cold_prevention "Formula for common cold prevention")). The culture is so inveterate that the myth landed in a school book for 8 year old children! It seems schooling is not only harmful in its design. Occasionally, it is also harmful by promoting falsehoods. See: [Myth: We catch a cold from cold](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Myth:_We_catch_a_cold_from_cold "Myth: We catch a cold from cold")

Colds are (predominantly) caused by viruses

### School is good

Among parents and teachers, there is a hole set of school mythology that never seems to go away (see: [Mythology that keeps the archaic school system alive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mythology_that_keeps_the_archaic_school_system_alive "Mythology that keeps the archaic school system alive")). I can explain it to the same relatively young people again and again. I explain that it is not true that going to sleep earlier is the best way to get long refreshing sleep. Nearly all teenagers know this, and nearly all adults blast them for going to sleep late with the same never-advancing train of reasoning (see: [Myth: You can get more sleep by going to sleep earlier](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Myth:_You_can_get_more_sleep_by_going_to_sleep_earlier "Myth: You can get more sleep by going to sleep earlier")).

[Compulsory schooling must end](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling_must_end "Compulsory schooling must end")

### Repetitio est mater studiorum

I believe that arrogance is a good habit we naturally develop by **not going to school**. To be more precise, self-confidence is natural and comes from how the brain works to defend consistent models of reality (see: [Brain algorithms protect models of reality](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Brain_algorithms_protect_models_of_reality "Brain algorithms protect models of reality")). What is not natural, is the low self-esteem that we often get as one of [100 bad habits we learn at school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/50_bad_habits_we_learn_at_school "50 bad habits we learn at school"). I am very arrogant and self-confident. This is very healthy. That arrogance survived through years of schooling, and reached astronomical proportions during the three decades of [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning"). This is healthy for the brain and for the body. It helps to lead a happy life of no doubt and no anxieties. However, free people freely learn all necessary social skills. All naturally arrogant people learn pretty fast to act as a humble player because few things destroy relationships with well-schooled people as fast as telling them they are wrong.

When I try to explain that "school is bad", and [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning") hurts children, I often hear: *"It is all a matter of systematic review"*, and then those immortal words *"Repetition is important, you know"*. The arrogant me wants to scream that I have spent the last forty years living that claim for a few hours per day (see: [History of spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/History_of_spaced_repetition "History of spaced repetition")). However, the polite me, grunts patiently and says: *"You are absolutely right, **however**, repetition cannot be done on a material that does not want to stick to child's memory"* (translation: "for effective review you need the appropriate [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") " and, in addition, "you need to seal it with a signal that can only come from the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") system"). Perhaps I should be more blunt and say: **Forcing children to learn things they do not want to learn is criminal**. Not only do they not learn. They learn to hate learning.

Being blunt about [compulsory schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling "Compulsory schooling") is like telling a teacher she has wasted her life, or telling a parent she is an oppressor. They will not change. They will only hate me. Many probably do.

Mythbusting in person requires humble diplomatic skills

## Loving grannies

I love old people! I keep wondering why I have so much love for children and for the elderly. Could this be associated with the [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") curve? I love children for how receptive and plastic they are. I see them as the future of the planet. At the same time, I love the wisdom of old people and countless stories they can tell me about their lives.

In contrast, for all people of ages in between, I have some demands on kindness and contribution. As if only the love for the young and the old was unconditional. Could this be some universal way a free brain perceives other individuals? We love the kids unconditionally because they can't help being immature, and we love old people unconditionally because they can't help being unable to change? I am not the only one with similar feelings.

Love underlies tolerance. Granny's brain exhibits high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). We need to use love to develop tolerance and patience. It seems unavoidable: we need to explain to our granny the same things over and over again. She may or may not experience senile dementia. The problem will always be there even with those most open-minded grannies who never stop learning.

Grannies may appear forgetful even if they cognitive faculties are excellent

## Wisdom has costs

The whole annoyance with grannies comes from the plasticity-vs-stability trade-off. We are forgetful as children, and we struggle to learn new things as elderly.

Older brains tend to be more conservative, but wiser at the same time

## Reader warning

This texts explains why you should never trust my words. Due to my arrogance and age, my theories and models feel rock solid. If they are wrong, I may never know.

The only safe path to wisdom is to always consult multiple sources, compare contradictory models, and never stop learning
