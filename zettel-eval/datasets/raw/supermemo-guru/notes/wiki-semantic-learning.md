## Definition

**Semantic learning** is learning based on solid comprehension. **Semantic learning** builds knowledge that is well connected with the semantic network of prior knowledge. By definition, **semantic learning** leads to [coherent memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence"). Such memories are usually characterized by high [applicability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicability "Applicability") and high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). The effect of coherence on applicability is context dependent (e.g. coherent knowledge of history may not be applicable in the context of [problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_solving "Problem solving") in physics).

## Explanation

The easiness with which we remember is strongly related to how a piece knowledge is connected with the rest of the body of knowledge. To reference the system of semanatic connections, I use the term **framework**. In learning we will primarily encounter three classes of situations: (1) **semantic learning** in which the semantic framework is based on the meaning of knowledge, (2) **mnemonic learning** is **memorization** based on mnemonic frameworks erected artificially to support memory, and (3) **[asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning")** in which the framework of connections is weak or non-existent (colloquially we would call that form of learning **[cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming")**).

## Examples

In **[asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning")**, we may be requested to remember a number: 647 983 894. This is a very challenging job for an unprepared mind. The disassociation of the number from prior knowledge is magnified by the fact that we are not even providing a meaningful anchor. An association of the number with someone's phone might provide such an anchor. For example, *What is John's phone number? 647 983 894*

In **mnemonic learning**, we may use a peg list or a memory palace to (1) separate the number into chunks, e.g. 6 47 98 38 94, (2) convert the chunks into a pictorial story, and (3) memorize the story that can easily be decoded back as a number. It is not likely a phone number can be remembered without an assist from mnemonic techniques. It may occasionally be remembered via frequent use (e.g. one's own number), or via natural [mnemonic anchors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mnemonic_anchor "Mnemonic anchor") that develop over decades of learning (esp. if memorizing numbers is frequently needed).

In **semantic learning**, we associate new knowledge with prior knowledge. If the associations are simple, the knowledge is particularly easy to remember. For example: *What is the name of our galaxy? Milky way*. All **semantic learning** is mnemonic in nature as the semantic framework can make the best kind of a mnemonic framework.

## Evolution of learning in development

Little children can only learn using the natural semantic way. Things that are meaningful, simple, and [useful](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicability "Applicability") will be stored in memory. Those early connections may even survive the period of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") (e.g. recognition of a dog may precede the earliest recallable episode in life, e.g. an encounter with a vicious dog).

With the gradual buildup of knowledge, prior knowledge may provide rudimentary mnemonic framework that will help remember things with the use of prior knowledge without the need for a specific semantic connection. For example, we may remember that the Internet was born in 1969 because it was the same year that we went to the moon. The two events may seem unrelated, but the mind can automatically form the association.

The more we learn, the richer and more [coherent](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherent "Coherent") the semantic network of knowledge, the less the need to resort to mnemonic techniques for knowledge of high [applicability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicability "Applicability")

One of the great harms of schooling is that tests and deadlines promote [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming"). Children are coerced to employ **[asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning")** as an emergency tool. Overtime, incessant conditioning results in weakening the habit of **semantic learning**. A schooled adult may approach learning in the old bad-school way, i.e. [cram](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming") fast and voluminously. This has a disastrous impact on the quality of learning, comprehension, and the ability to reason about knowledge. This way, schooling undermines human intelligence!

Incessant conditioning that involves **[asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning")** is one of main reasons why **schooling undermines human intelligence**

## Adult empathy

Adults struggle to empathize with young brains. It is no coincidence that early learning gravitates towards [asemantic content](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning"). Letters of the alphabet, days of the week, names of months, multiplication table, etc. These are all asemantic concepts that get quickly filtered as hardest. This gives them the aura of primacy and primitiveness. They may be labelled as *basic toolset*. In reality, what a young brain needs is a rich [semantic framework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_framework "Semantic framework") that establishes the need for, and the meaning of those primitive concepts. Only then learning may become semantic and produce desired results. A vast majority of adults, including good teachers, do not understand the need for semantic learning. This is the outcome of the conditioning by the [Prussian system](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Prussian_system "Prussian system").

It is very easy for an adult to get a glimpse into a child's brain. Imagine a child who is to learn the names of the days of the week. This child has no scheduled events such us the Sunday mass, or Saturday soccer. For this child, there is less difference between Tuesday and Thursday than there is a difference between Ganymede and Calisto. To learn the names of the days is like attaching labels to vacuum. Entirely futile and asemantic. The adult may reason *"these are vital terms the child will need in her life"*.

To remedy the deficit in **[educational empathy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Educational_empathy "Educational empathy")**, the adult can imagine the year split into 36 ten-day periods with each period assigned a specific name: *Primedec, Secdec, Winterdec, Febdec,* etc. How about learning that sequence just for a test? Only 36 names (just to compensate for adult metacognitive superiority). All adults, with no exceptions, rejects such a proposition. This is exactly how a child feels. A bunch of names to learn with no content and no purpose! This is classic [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning").

The inability to empathize with a young brain is the main source of design errors in education systems

## Problem with SuperMemo

In [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), **semantic learning** may lead to a set of [items](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item") that may become [asemantic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic "Asemantic") through the process of [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting"). Those items will become [leeches](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Leech "Leech") when their connection with the semantic network of knowledge is lost or weakened. [Incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") is a natural form of prevention as it is easy to do a semantic review upon detecting weakened connections or encountering [leeches](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Leech "Leech")

Learn drive vs. School drive

> ***Figure:** This is how school destroys the love of learning. **[Learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive")** is the set of passions and interests that a child would like to pursue. **[School drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/School_drive "School drive")** is the set of rewards and penalties set up by the school system. **Learn drive** leads to simple, mnemonic, [coherent](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherent "Coherent"), [stable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stable "Stable") and [applicable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicable "Applicable") memories due to the fact that the quality of knowledge determines the degree of reward in the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") system. **School drive** leads to complex, short-term memories vulnerable to [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") due to the fact that schools serialize knowledge by [curriculum](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Curriculum "Curriculum") (not by the neural mechanism of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive")). [Competitive inhibition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/War_of_the_networks "War of the networks") between the **Learn drive** and the **School drive** circuits will lead to the weakening of neural connections. Strong **School drive** will weaken the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), destroy the passion for learning, and lead to [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness"). Powerful **Learn drive** will lead to [rebellion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Resistance "Resistance") that will protect intrinsic passions, but possibly will also lead to problems at school. Storing new knowledge under the influence of **Learn drive** is highly rewarding and carries no penalty (by definition of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive")). This will make the learn drive thrive leading to success in learning (and at school). In contrast, poor quality of knowledge induced by the pressures of the **School drive** will produce a weaker reward signal, and possibly a strong [incoherence penalty](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incoherence_penalty "Incoherence penalty"). The penalty will feed back to produce [reactance](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Resistance "Resistance") against the [school drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/School_drive "School drive"), which will in turn require further coercive correction from the school system, which will in turn reduce the quality of knowledge further. Those feedback loops may lead to the dominance of one of the forces: the learn drive or the school drive. Thriving [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") increases [rebellion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Resistance "Resistance") that increases defenses against the school drive. Similarly, increased penalization at school increases [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness") that weakens the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") and results in submission to the system. Sadly, in most cases, the control system settles in the middle of those two extremes (see: the [old soup problem](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Old_soup_problem "Old soup problem")). Most children [hate school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Why_kids_hate_school%3F "Why kids hate school), lose their love of learning, and still submit to the enslavement. Their best chance for recovery is the freedom of college, or better yet, the freedom of adulthood.
