### Biphasic human

In this chapter, I will show why [biphasic sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Biphasic_sleep "Biphasic sleep") is the best sleep pattern for high productivity and for the brain health.

Most researchers agree that human adult [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle") is [biphasic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Biphasic_sleep "Biphasic sleep"). In addition to sleep, one of the outward expressions of the circadian cycle are the changes in core body temperature.

![Temperature changes in the course of the day in degrees centigrade (courtesy of: Prof. Luiz Menna-Barreto, State University of Campinas, Brazil)](https://supermemo.guru/images/8/80/Temp_changes_during_the_day.jpg)

[SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") and [SleepChart](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SleepChart "SleepChart") provide an excellent tool to verify the claim of the biphasic nature of human sleep-wake cycles. I have collected data from monophasic and biphasic sleepers that illustrate our biphasic nature.

### Monophasic sleep with biphasic learning

The height of the two alertness peaks may differ in a monophasic sleeper, who will also show the same depression in [recall](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Recall "Recall") around the 8th hour of the subjective waking day. However, characteristically, a monophasic sleeper may not get the same performance boost in the evening as [biphasic sleepers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Biphasic_sleep "Biphasic sleep") due to the effects of the [[wiki-two-components-of-sleep|homeostatic sleep drive component]]. Even a few minute nap can result in a major boost in alertness. This has already been noticed by a prominent napping expert Dr David Dinges in his comprehensive surveys comparing habitual nappers with non-nappers (Dinges 1989 [^1]).

To illustrate the difference between biphasic and monophasic sleepers, see an analogous [recall](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Recall "Recall") graph in which a monophasic 15-year-old non-napper shows the best performance in the morning hours with a sharp dip at the 8th hour of wakefulness coinciding with a subjective decline in cognitive function:

![Grades of a monophasic sleeper in relation to the circadian phase](https://supermemo.guru/images/thumb/2/21/Grades_vs_circadian_phase_%28monophasic_sleeper%29.jpg/800px-Grades_vs_circadian_phase_%28monophasic_sleeper%29.jpg)

Grades of a monophasic sleeper in relation to the circadian phase

After a temporary dip, there is a sharp recovery, and a gradual decline in performance in the second half of the day. That decline is strongly accelerated by a homeostatic mechanism. The yellow line shows the estimated [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") component of alertness. In this case, the [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") benefits are muffled by the [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") decline in alertness, which is not shown in the graph. This is why the hypothetical [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") alertness and the actual alertness match only in the first half of the day.

### Biphasic circadian graph

There is a biphasic twist to the [two-process model of sleep regulation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two-process_model_of_sleep_regulation "Two-process model of sleep regulation"). In [free running sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_running_sleep "Free running sleep"), where sleep is a true expression of sleep propensity, it is possible to visualize both the [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") and the [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") components of sleep in a circadian graph:

![Circadian graph of a biphasic sleeper](https://supermemo.guru/images/thumb/1/19/Circadian_graph_%28biphasic_sleeper%29.jpg/800px-Circadian_graph_%28biphasic_sleeper%29.jpg)

Circadian graph of a biphasic sleeper

> ***Figure:** **Circadian graph** in a biphasic sleeper. All sleep blocks are plotted in reference to the predicted optimum waking hour. The red line shows the average length of sleep blocks initiated at a given [circadian hour](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_phase "Circadian phase") (time from arising). The blue line shows the frequency of sleep initiated at that [circadian hour](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_hour "Circadian hour"). The optimum non-shifting length of the waking day is determined by the crossing of the green and red lines. In this case it is 20 hours (this value usually drops after block [consolidation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Consolidation "Consolidation") and is then more accurate). The most frequently used length of the day is also 20 hours indicating regular adherence to the optimum. Optimum [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") time from waking is likely indicated by siesta frequency that peaks at 9 hours. Usual siesta length is 1.2 hour and is nearly independent of the [circadian hour](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_hour "Circadian hour"). Maximum unconsolidated nighttime sleep occurs when initiated 17 hours from waking. The optimum unconsolidated length is less than 4 hours*

**[SleepChart](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SleepChart "SleepChart") can express homeostatic and circadian sleepiness with its charting tools!** It shows the biphasic nature of human performance

In a habitual napper, the [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") biphasic nature is paradoxically expressed by the two-peak sleep propensity curve instead of the [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") curve. The reason for this role reversal is the physiological difference between the two circadian peaks in sleep propensity. In a habitual napper, sleep is initiated as easily at [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") time as it is initiated at night. However, the length of sleep at [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") time is very short (usually 15-80 min).

In the presented graph, the blue line corresponds with the ability to initiate sleep at any given [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") time. On the horizontal axis, it aligns well with the alertness graphs displayed in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") (as shown in earlier paragraphs). It aligns well with both the learning data, as well as with the two-process sleep model implemented in [SleepChart](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SleepChart "SleepChart").

The red line corresponds with the ability to maintain sleep. It reveals what is not visible in the alertness graph shown earlier: [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") naps cannot last long and will always be subject to an early natural termination (low red line under the first blue peak). In contrast, the period of [subjective night](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Subjective_night "Subjective night") is the only time of day when sleep can and should last longest (usually no less than 4-5 hours). The red peak is also the reason why [polyphasic sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Science_of_polyphasic_sleep "Science of polyphasic sleep") adepts crave for "core sleep", wake up groggy, and need heavy alarm artillery to wake up in this critical [subjective night](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Subjective_night "Subjective night") period.

David Dinges, in his surveys noticed, that napping more than once within a day was extremely rare. Most nappers took naps lasting 15-120 min. Naps will be shorter if they are taken before the [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") peak. If they are taken after the peak, they will usually last longer, and may even integrate with the night sleep in cases of particularly large delay, or where there is a sleep deprivation, [[wiki-how-do-we-fall-asleep-3f|REM-sleep deficit]], or any other form of "sleep debt".

Dinges noticed that both appetitive (habitual) and replacement (compensatory) nappers tended to time their naps 7-8 hours from waking (see: [[wiki-best-time-for-napping|Best timing of naps]]). Even though napping habits may differ, the [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") timing of the [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") trough seems to be pretty similar across the population (Dinges 1992 [^2])

It is important to note again that the evening boost in alertness is magnified by a nap but shows up also in non-nappers and can easily be deconvoluted in the two-processes model into its [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") and circadian components (as shown in the next two examples).

### Biphasic performance in sleep deprivation

Mid-day slump is as prominent in conditions of severe as well as mild [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation"). This [graph](http://jama.ama-assn.org/content/295/2/163/F1.large.gif) shows a mid-day alertness slump in a 26 hour [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") study (Czeisler et al. 2006 [^3]). The timing of the slump (hours 10-12 of waking period) indicates that the preceding sleep episode was positioned suboptimally (hence the need to interrupt sleep for the study). Natural awaking would probably take place 1-2 hours after a forced awakening in lab conditions. The graph also shows that [sleep inertia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_inertia "Sleep inertia") caused by forced awakening from Stage 2 [NREM](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/NREM "NREM") or [REM](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/REM "REM") sleep causes a much greater cognitive decline than 26 hours of [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation").

### Summary: Napping is good!

From the above charts we can conclude that human circadian pattern is definitely biphasic. Here are the implications:

- Humans are biphasic in nature and show a [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_sleep_propensity "Circadian sleep propensity") boost in learning in subjective evening hours
- Non-nappers show a mid-day dip in performance and might also benefit from a [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta")
- In [free running sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_running_sleep "Free running sleep"), healthy humans tend to fall into [biphasic sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Biphasic_sleep "Biphasic sleep") pattern
- Siesta is a well-tried old practice in many cultures around the world. It is a healthy practice

**[Biphasic sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Biphasic_sleep "Biphasic sleep") is the healthiest and most natural form of sleep**
