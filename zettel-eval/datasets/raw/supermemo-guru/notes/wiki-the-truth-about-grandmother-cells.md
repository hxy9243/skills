## Disagreement about grandmother cells

A **[grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")** is a neuron that responds selectively to an activation of a high-level concept corresponding with an idea in the mind. Neurons of this type have been found in the brains of animals from simple snails to monkeys. Today they pop up regularly in artificial neural networks.

You will hear of grandmother cells often when you read my texts. However, the idea is not without controversy.

The concept of a grandmother cell is often considered a joke in neural computing community. Those who are privy to artificial neural networks and parallel distributed processing (PDP) have a good connectionist model of how the brain might work. The list of those who support distributed representations reads like who is who of my MAPs (most admired people) in the field (2018). Even one of the contributors to the [Neurostatistical Model of Memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurostatistical_Model_of_Memory "Neurostatistical Model of Memory"), Dr [Murakowski](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Murakowski "Murakowski") is a connectionist.

In contrast, I have imagined the idea of a [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") as early as in the 1980s. I built all my thinking about the brain around this concept. For over 30 years, all things I learned about the brain agreed entirely with my thinking. No wonder, then, that I developed a strong mental [model](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Model "Model") that cannot be easily shaken. I am convinced all the list of stars of neuroscience is wrong. A [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") is as sure a thing for me as a memory connection between two concept neurons, and the memory properties, [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") and [retrievability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retrievability "Retrievability"), that make it possible to remember one's grandmother for life.

This is why I never bothered to compile an argument for the existence of grandmother cells. I just took it for granted and it shows in all my writings from as early as my [Master's Thesis](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Master%27s_Thesis "Master's Thesis") (1990).

It is highly likely that I might have been influenced in the early 1980s by Polish books on neurophysiology that might have been soaked in the spirit of [Jurek Konorski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jerzy_Konorski) who was one of the early independent proponents of the concept of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"), which he called *gnostic cells*. If Konorski's name had been adopted, we would have not had grandmother jokes in neuroscience today. However, I like the catchy and mnemonic name that makes for a good meme in the name of promoting the science of the brain

## I disagree with an icon of neuroscience

Terrence Sejnowski is one of my MAPs (most admired people). I have read dozens of his papers from as early as in the 1990s. I listened to dozens of his lectures. I find his messages very inspiring and I hardly ever disagree. I know Terry is a connectionist and I do not mind, but I was very surprised to read in, [This idea must die](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/This_idea_must_die "This idea must die"), that he believes that the grandmother cell idea should die for the good of neuroscience. Terry's contribution to science is monumental. What surprised me was his eagerness to close the debate on a concept that, in my eyes, only gathers more and more biological support. Its computational feasibility was obvious to me from the day I first thought of it in the 1980s.

Sejnowski knows a world more about neural networks than myself. It is really pretty presumptive for me to disagree. However, he got two pages to frame his reasoning, and he did not convince me. All his reasoning is correct. However, he basically spoke about the limitations of the experiment, in which an alleged Halle Berry neuron has been identified.

## Have we found Halle Berry cell?

I would summarize Sejnowski's text as *"the famous Quiroga experiment with a Halle Berry cell does not prove grandmother cell hypothesis"*. Terry is right.

We have not really found Halle Berry cells. Hitting the right cell with an electrode verges on the impossible. Halle Berry cell might simply fire in response to some Halle characteristics, and be a transit route to neocortical encoding. The same route may be taken over by Bill Clinton a few days later. Just because a cell fires doesn't mean it represents the correct concept. It would be nice to evoke the image of Halle by sending impulses to some specific cells.

Sejnowski seems to have missed the most compelling argument against the Halle Berry cell. The Halle cell was found in the hippocampus which should indeed be connectionist. The hippocampus is not designated as a place of storage, but as a place of capture and association (see: [Hippocampal concept neurons in semantic and episodic memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Hippocampal_concept_neurons_in_semantic_and_episodic_memory "Hippocampal concept neurons in semantic and episodic memory")). No [concept cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cell "Concept cell") could survive in a structure that keeps registering and erasing memories on a massive scale. Storing long-term concepts is not the job of the hippocampus. Halle Berry and Jennifer Aniston cells were probably only transit routes towards a wider concept connectivity. All that was needed in the experiment was to find a cell that responds to a characteristic of Halle Berry that is absent in Jennifer Aniston and is also absent in the remaining pictures used in the test.

The overlap can be seen in cells that can fire 300 times stronger in response to Oprah than in response to Whoopi. This is an indicator of the connectionist aspect of the hippocampus. At the same time, this finding says little of the feasibility of a [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") in the neocortex.

## Should we kill models in science?

In a call to abolish the grandmother cell hypothesis, Sejnowski would best use his two-page text for presenting a compelling argument for the connectionist point of view. He didn't. What is worse, I have never seen any compelling line of reasoning in the matter. Even worse, I am sure such line does not exist without violations to the Occam's razor principle. It is computationally conceivable to contrive a workable model of the connectionist brain. However, why would we even go there if the present simple model of memory is neat, easy to analyze, easy to implement outside the brain, etc. I leave the connectionist job to passionate theoreticians in their quest to see if this is possible to improve upon nature using connectionist tools. Whoever is wrong, science should always progress by a clash of models: good and bad.

I appreciate the contribution of ANNs and PDP to the field of neuroscience, but I dare claim that the brain is a bit more sophisticated. For one, why do computational neuroscientists so badly neglect the concept of [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability") that perfectly resolves the problem of catastrophic forgetting. Computational neuroscientists ignore biological neuroscience at their own peril. Closing the debate would be a disaster for the field. We need to reconcile the best evidence. From my point of view, there is a lot of inspiration coming from connectionist models.

## Language, cognition and neuroscience

[Jeffrey S. Bowers](http://www.bristol.ac.uk/expsych/people/jeffrey-s-bowers) from the University of Bristol is a cognitive psychologist. He has been the most passionate defender of the [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") hypothesis for some two decades now. In 2009, he presented the best compilation of evidence yet: *" [On the biological plausibility of grandmother cells: Implications for neural network theories in psychology and neuroscience](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19159155) "*.

In the preface to *Language, Cognition and Neuroscience: Vol 32, No 3* (2017), Bowers asked key figures in related fields to opine on the plausibility of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"). The invited authors included one of my MAPs: Edmund T Rolls. Whatever I write here about the hippocampus or memory systems, I might have largely learned from Rolls two decades ago.

In [his paper](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/304917808_Cortical_coding), Rolls provided a summary of research about the ways neurons seem to code information in the cortex. Speaking of the cortical code, Rolls noticed:

> The advantages of the code include high capacity, generalisation, graceful degradation, and rapid readout of the information by biologically plausible dot-product decoding. None of these are properties of local or *"grandmother-cell"* representations

This line of reasoning could spell the death to the grandmother cell hypothesis. However, all the allegedly missing properties of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") have been identified incorrectly. Rolls's claims are not true!

Rolls has presented a rich account of his own research on neuronal firing in the cortex. I have no reservations to that account and can only praise the effort. However, Rolls's analysis in the context of grandmother cells is an unintended red herring. I would summarized it as follows: " **Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence** ".

When Rolls attempted to summarize his thinking about the implausibility of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"), he provided the best summary in just a few sentences ([source](http://wrap.warwick.ac.uk/80516)):

> A local representation is one in which all the information that a particular stimulus or event occurred is provided by the activity of one of the neurons. In a famous example, a single neuron might be active only if one's grandmother was being seen, and this is sometimes called grandmother cell encoding. (The term was coined by Jerry Lettvin in about 1969 — see Charles Gross (2002).) An implication is that most neurons in the brain regions where objects or events are represented would fire only very rarely (Barlow, 1972; Barlow, 1995). A problem with this type of encoding is that a new neuron would be needed for every object or event that has to be represented. Another disadvantage is that this type of coding does not generalize easily to similar inputs, so that similarities between perceptions or memories would not be apparent. Another disadvantage is that the system is rather sensitive to brain damage: if a single neuron is lost, the representation may be lost. Another disadvantage of local encoding is that the storage capacity in a memory system in the brain (the number of stimuli that can be stored and recalled) may not be especially high (in the order of the number of synapses onto each neuron) (Rolls, 2016)

Below, I will dismantle all the above claims one by one. In addition to the usual evidence that may be derived from cognitive science, I also toss in two contributions from the field of [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition"), which show that **(1) [two-component model of long-term memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two-component_model_of_long-term_memory "Two-component model of long-term memory") implies the existence of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"), and (2) [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") can prove sufficient cortical capacity to represent all imaginable concepts with the use of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")**.

Neither me nor Bowers have ever stuck an electrode in a living brain. Perhaps this is actually a good thing. We do not drown in a chaos of firing cells that can only be described statistically. The reasoning presented below is derived from high-level observations and measurements about how the brain works. Like myself, Bowers is interested in learning languages. He can see how atomic lexical units land in memory and how [memory interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_interference "Memory interference") can ruin that effort. This is an eye opener to any cognitive scientist.

Before I praise Jeffrey though, I should note that he also seems to be the author of this sentence ([2016](https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding)):

> I show that there are no current examples of neuroscience motivating new and effective teaching methods, and argue that neuroscience is unlikely to improve teaching in the future

For contrast see: [Pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning") or [Spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition")

## Google's "cat node" is a grandmother cell

In one of his lectures, Sejnowski demoed a "cat node" emerging in a Google network used to analyze images. That "cat node" is exactly a correspondent of a [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"). [Jeff Dean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeff_Dean) recounts the story of the cat neuron and human face neurons in [this interview](https://youtube.com/watch) (starting at 26:19). See also: [Building high-level features using large scale unsupervised learning](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/6639343).

Even in Sejnowski's own critique, he says: *"each of the hidden units coded different combinations of features of the input units, such as fragments of eyes, noses or head shapes"*.

Fragments of eyes constitute separate [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept"). If these concepts are represented by singular units, it is the confirmation of the grandmother cell thesis. Perhaps Sejnowski uses a stricter definition, in which one's grandmother qualifies grandmother cells, but grandmother's glasses do not make such a sufficient condition. This distinction would be artificial. Granny and glasses differ only in the level of abstraction and complexity. They make part of the same concept universe in the human mind.

To recognize faces, we need only one cell per face as the face is a concept. All viewing angles are nicely processed with feature extraction nets and combined using the same ultimate pattern recognition system. For one person, Halle Berry may be composed of "black eyes", "curly hair", "beautiful", plus 10 other attributes integrated at entry. For someone else it will be "chocolate", "talented", "sensual", "warm", "like my mom", plus 15 other attributes. All those sub-concepts may be associated with face-selective cells.

For clarity, when I speak of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"), I mean any cells that represent a meaningful concept. A grandmother is definitely a high-level concept, but there are thousands of lower-level concepts, including glasses. A grandmother cell may fire at any time whenever it receives signals from other units. It will bring memories of one's grandmother if the cell is lucky to break through to the level of consciousness. We cannot use electrodes to fire the cell and produce visons of a granny, nor can we measure the firing and try to prove it happens only in the definite presence of a granny. Neuroscience isn't there yet (2018).

## Sentiment neuron

In 2017, in the process of developing ChatGPT, OpenAI trained a massive recurrent network on 82 million Amazon reviews. Upon training, they discovered multiple [concept cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cell "Concept cell") in the net. A single neuron was branded as *" [sentiment neuron](https://rakeshchada.github.io/Sentiment-Neuron.html) "* as its activation perfectly reflected the sentiment of the reviewer: from negative to positive (see the [original 2017 paper](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1704.01444)).

The discovery of the *"sentiment neuron"* by OpenAI offers a glimpse into a natural [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") process within artificial neural networks, mirroring mechanisms that underpin cognition in biological brains. The sentiment neuron, identified in a large language model, activates specifically in response to emotionally charged inputs, effectively encoding a high-level abstraction of sentiment. Such crystallization of meaning into a single neuron echoes the idea of **grandmother cells**. In artificial neural nets, in unsupervised learning, this emergent behavior suggests that as layers process and distill input data, certain nodes can evolve to represent distinct, interpretable features, much like [concept maps](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_map "Concept map") forming in a trained model. The sentiment neuron’s specificity challenges the distributed representation paradigm, hinting that localized encoding might play a universal role in artificial and human neural architectures. This aligns with evidence of sparse coding in the cortex, where individual neurons can indeed signal complex, singular ideas.

## Donald Trump neuron

Recent years bring more and more findings in the field of [mechanistic interpretability](https://www.darioamodei.com/post/the-urgency-of-interpretability). In particular, the case of [Golden Gate Claude](https://www.anthropic.com/news/golden-gate-claude) illustrates how a tiny set of neurons can dramatically change the [personality](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Great_personalities_do_not_come_from_genes "Great personalities do not come from genes").

Focused exclusively on mechanistic interpretability at Anthropic, [Chris Olah](https://x.com/ch402) has observed the emergence of dozens of [concept neurons](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_neuron "Concept neuron"), features, circuits, and hierarchies in artificial neural networks, such as Gabor filters, and detectors for color, curvature, boundaries, halos, and textures. As befits a [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network"), a concept neuron for cars is affected by signals from features activated by the appearance of wheels, windows or headlights. This is a direct equivalent of [conceptual computation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptual_computation "Conceptual computation") in the brain.

Olah noted the almost inevitable appearance of a [Donald Trump](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") neuron in the models he studied (see: [Chris Olah at Lex Fridman podcast](https://youtube.com/watch) (5:20)).

Mechanistic interpretability is a new source of evidence on the emergence and role of concept neurons

## Synchronous firing is a source of bias

Sejnowski, who worked on concerted firing (e.g. phasic sleep spindle oscillations), may see the brain as a connectionist machine. He may focus on timing and synchrony. He may focus on complex mechanisms involved in [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") in sleep (see: [memory optimization in sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_optimization_in_sleep "Memory optimization in sleep")).

Sejnowski can see Princes Leia waves and see ANN computing in action. But this is connectionist optimization and integration, not the actual act of storing new memories for long-term survival. There is little wavelike in encoding a binary memory.

My perspective is based on simple question-answer associations, like those in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). Massive firing is certainly involved in [thought](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem), [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep"), [memory optimization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_optimization "Memory optimization"), [memory consolidation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_consolidation "Memory consolidation"), etc. Synchronous activity will impact [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability") of a single synapse. However, the only sensible model of a simple binary memory surviving a lifetime can be implemented in a simple connection in a simple neuronal constellation.

I found an ancient quote from Terry:

> To categorize is human, to distribute, divine.  
> *Terrence Sejnowski, 1988*

Could this be that Sejnowski has been biased for years with his [models](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Model "Model") as I have been biased by mine? 1988 is the same year in which I hypothesized how the brain might be storing simple memories like those imprinted with [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") (see: [Two components of memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_components_of_memory "Two components of memory")).

## Inspiration in zoology

I think that people involved in ANNs do not pay sufficient attention to basic biology. I have always loved zoology and I never ignore inspiration coming from bugs and slugs. We can literally see [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") with our own eyes. ## Repeat learning, repeat activation

For memories to develop high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), the same activation pathways must be used. The same cells and the same synaptic connections must be involved. In the connectionist model, all synaptic weights are in the flow. All new patterns require recalculations at the high risk of [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference"). This is expensive energetically, complex computationally, and risky in terms of [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") via [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference"). By definition, connectionist interference is the opposite of [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), while stability is the key to human wisdom. Perhaps my life dedicated to building stable memories makes me more sensitive to that weakness of the connectionist model?

A clever way of investigating neuronal ensembles involved in learning has been devised with the help of transgenic mice that express tetanus toxin. See: [Irreplaceability of Neuronal Ensembles after Memory Allocation](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports/fulltext/S2211-1247\(15\)00321-6)

## Memory stability underlies computability

The study of neural networks and the entire field of [computational neuroscience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_neuroscience) neglect the existence of [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability"), which is a vital component of long-term memory. [Stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") is necessary for long-term [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") and [human intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Human_intelligence "Human intelligence"). With a good understanding of the [two component model of long-term memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_component_model_of_long-term_memory "Two component model of long-term memory"), we can clearly see the inevitability of the existence of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"). This fact is obscured for those who study neural nets using short-term memory principles.

Connectionism is great for computability at the initial storage stage, but localist approach is favored for [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). The brain needs both. Computational neuroscientists study the efficient brain of the moment. However, they tend to underappreciate the changes needed to reprogram that structure for lifelong [problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_solving "Problem solving") (see: [Conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization")). Once we separate a function through a [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") process and whittle it down to a few cells and a few synapses, we can sear it all in protein, perhaps for life.

In other words, the brain needs to first (1) find the right connections, then (2) consolidate them at a low cost, and (3) toss away the garbage.

While capturing a new memory, the brain needs to quickly compute its optimum connectionist representation. While using stable memories years later, the brain can focus all its energy on problem solving.

In short, for problem solving, we do not want to burden the brain with connectionist computation at the time when it needs [fast thinking](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fast_thinking "Fast thinking"), and simplicity in creatively establishing new associations between concepts.

In 2005, we showed how [memory complexity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_complexity "Memory complexity") affects [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability"). For example, for two independent memory traces under perfectly symmetric [review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Review "Review") conditions we have:

> S=S <sub>a</sub> \*S <sub>b</sub> /(S <sub>a</sub> +S <sub>b</sub>)
> 
> where:
> 
> - S - [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") of the composite memory trace
> - S <sub>a</sub> and S <sub>b</sub> - stabilities of memory subtraces a and b

The inefficiency of composite memories can be extended to demonstrate the inefficiency of the connectionist model. If the same concept needs to be represented by more than one neuron, its vulnerability to [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") will be greater. It's [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") will be lower. If a set of synapses is used to store a single memory, there is little advantage of localist model over the connectionist model early in the process. However, the difference between memory stabilities will keep increasing with time (see: [details](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Complexity_of_memories_in_spaced_repetition "Complexity of memories in spaced repetition")).

The inefficiency goes a step further if two independent memories share synapses in the connectionist model. In such a case, the [two component model of memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_component_model_of_memory "Two component model of memory") would imply that a review of one memory impacts the review of the other memory. Neither [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") nor [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") could be optimized independently. While learning about fishes, we might boost the [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") of memories associated with architecture. That would be nice. However, while forgetting the name of a fish, we might accidentally forget the meaning of the Laffer curve. Optimization of memory is a vital component of human intelligence. In a connectionist model that optimization would be very difficult.

[Two component model of long-term memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_component_model_of_long-term_memory "Two component model of long-term memory") provides strong novel evidence for the existence of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")

## Wiring problem

As the brain is composed of a myriad of structures and substructures, we can be pretty certain that at the very low level, small connectionist systems exist. The hippocampus might be a prime example of a large connectionist system. When it is just about to pass its maximum capacity, it goes to [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep"). When it runs out from its working memory capacity, it adds new neurons via neurogenesis (see: [Neurogenesis assists pattern separation in the hippocampus](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurogenesis_assists_pattern_separation_in_the_hippocampus "Neurogenesis assists pattern separation in the hippocampus")). However, in the long run, it always relies on the neocortex for the ultimate storage. This is where it lays ground for [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell").

Sejnowski speaks of hidden layers in neural networks that can provide distributed coding. They can encode any function, which may be impossible to figure it out by just looking at individual cells. This distributed nature of concepts would be the cause of costly complexity.

If we imagine [associating ideas](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Associationism "Associationism") carried by small connectionist concept assemblies, the wiring needed for the association would be a nightmare (comparable with rich CA3 wiring in the hippocampus or a Hopfield network). One might hypothesize that complex wiring in a connectionist unit would be the exact reason why only input pattern associations form, and the conceptual [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") is impaired. [Crystallization of concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Crystallization_of_knowledge "Crystallization of knowledge") has its contribution to [creative thinking](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity").

To associatively wire separate connectionist assemblies, several cells in particular states would correspond with one concept, and several other cells in specific states would correspond with another concept in the other net. How do we wire a new association without pre-existing routes? A single connection would suffice in the localist approach. The direct connection does not need to exist physically at the time when the association is made (e.g. in the hippocampus). All the new association needs is to find a route in the brain, and the rest can be wired later (e.g. in [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep")).

For an example of complex wiring, imagine that we take two nets as pattern recognizers and make one recognize a grandmother, and the other recognize dumplings. It would obviously be easier to have specific outputs from those to nets to form an association (a [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") and a dumpling cell). In the connectionist model, we would need complex wiring that might entangle distributed representations of the grandmother with those of the dumplings. In the light of recent developments that show the role of dendritic spine morphology in the course of learning, synaptic reweighting appears to be particularly costly. We should rather speak of synaptic rewiring. This is the cost of long-term memories stored in the neocortex that, in reality, is well compensated by synaptic [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") that in turn reduces [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") with other stored engrams.

Separation of information solves many wiring problems. [Bowers](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23273798.2016.1267782) studied a form of interference named **superposition catastrophe**: *it is difficult to co-activate multiple things at the same time over the same set of processing units*. Distributed systems are great at pattern recognition, but they are not so good at sequential learning. For example, instead of a face, we may need to learn to recognize Face A, and then Face B, and then to co-activate both. [Grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") are a natural answer to similar wiring problems.

## Generalization

Artificial neural networks are supposedly great at [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"). However, this is a dumb variety of generalization. In basic implementations, it is based on similarities between input patterns. It is prone to overfitting. We tend to marvel over neural networks as they seem to outstrip programmable serial computers. Neural nets seem to have a mind of their own. They need no programming. They can figure out things on their own.

If they can categorize thousands of images and recognize cats or faces, they must be good generalizers. However, human brain can go much further. Its implementation of generalization is smart and selective. In neural networks, there is a trade-off between sparseness and interference, which is a reflection of the ageless [plasticity-stability dilemma](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Plasticity-stability_dilemma "Plasticity-stability dilemma"). Human brain seems to have found an optimum balance. On one hand it can keep learning incessantly for a century. On the other it can store stable memories from early childhood, and build high intelligence through smart generalization based on competitive learning and forgetting, which leads to higher [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") for winner memories.

Conceptual generalization of a grandmother is fast and easy. A young concept of a grandmother may requires granny glasses on input. However, that particular input can be eliminated from the recognition rule by active suppression or passive forgetting. The input may be suppressed if we recognize the grandmother without glasses. It can also be forgotten if we often recognize grandmother without paying attention to her glasses. If glasses play a role in recognizing grandmother, they can be generalized independently. Improving glasses recognition will affect the recognition of the grandmother.

Paradoxically, while [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") (losing information), conceptual generalization leads to stable memories and serves an error correction function (without the need for redundancy). When we meet granny, our recognition is improved if we forget that she had a pimple on her nose three months ago.

While generalizing, the brain trims down the cost of representation and speeds up deterministic computation. A concept map responsible for one's grandmother or Halle Berry, does not need to be just one cell, or one cortical column. However, it is more efficient to have simple representations even if redundancy may have many benefits. Knowledge that can live in memory for decades must have a clear place in the brain free from [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference"). Minimum neurons, and minimum synapses are a clear and cheap answer to the demands of such a memory.

Without [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability"), generalization in neural networks is not convergent (for infinite datasets). New inputs keep reweighting the synapses, and the concept may reach a generalization plateau. In localist representations, the generalization can reach the state of perfection: a concept neuron corresponding with an generalized idea, and its inputs corresponding with the attributes of the concept. All we need to recognize our grandmother is to obtain a small subset of well-defined attributes. The longer we live, the more we learn, the closer we get to perfection. Granny recognition is instant and cheap.

Competitive learning and [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") serve [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"). This process is convergent with the help of (1) [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability") and (2) [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")

## Cell death

The problem of expensive cell death is often mentioned by the connectionists. While ANNs can show significant fault tolerance with redundant code, the concept of [atomic memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Atomic_memory "Atomic memory") is inherently vulnerable. Cell's death is tantamount to the loss of memory. Redundancy in learning is possible (see: [Knowledge darwinism](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_darwinism "Knowledge darwinism")), however, [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") favors [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability").

The problem may in part be resolved with pseudopattern training, but it may equally well be up to the longevity of cells carrying a set long-term memory. As much DNA is subject to damage and repair with minimization of the impact on the carried information. Similar mechanisms might be involved in survival of long-term memories even though I am not aware of any reason for which that would be necessary. All memories are subject to forgetting via interference. This process that would usually be exponential is hardly ever perfectly so. Cell death might still have some contribution. The repair process might be important only for memories of [stabilities](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") on the order of [permastore](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Permastore "Permastore"). Even then, we can quickly reconstruct the concept of a grandmother using its associated concepts. For a well-loved person, the number of associated concepts may go from dozens to hundreds. All things we know about granny can serve the reconstruction. In [Neurostatistical Model of Memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurostatistical_Model_of_Memory "Neurostatistical Model of Memory"), we actually hypothesize that this type of [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") may have a monumental contribution to [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability"). In other words, long-term recall is not only chemical, but also structural.

## How many concepts can a brain store?

It is a frequent error of connectionists to claim that the brain isn't big enough to hold all the concept neurons we might need in the course of a lifetime. Some of that error comes from the belief that we may need many concept neurons for our grandmother, so that we could react to her picture presented at different angles. Some of the error comes from the happy and true realization than a small neural network has a huge expressive power. It seems only natural to capitalize on such a gift (if one fails to notice the problem of [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference")).

A frequent argument is that with *N* neurons we may have N grandmothers and 2 <sup><i>N</i></sup> connectionist grandmothers. This reasoning fails to appreciate that we can associate the grandmother with a similarly humongous number of permutations of granny attributes: with glasses, in blue glasses, with nose cut with broken red glasses, with hair glued to blood flowing from a cut from the broken silver glasses bridge, etc.

If we could estimate the number of concepts the brain needs to hold, we would be in a better position to estimate the physical capacity of the cortex to keep all those concept separate.

[SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") is the best tool to estimate the number of concepts that might live in a human brain. It shows the cost of encoding and crystallizing a single concept. It shows the power of [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") in [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"). It makes it easy to observe [knowledge darwinism](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_darwinism "Knowledge darwinism"). It precisely measures the speed of learning. It can register the learning progress over decades.

In the 1980s, I predicted we will never see a human being capable of storing a million [items](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item") in her brain. The speed of learning is limited by memory storage optimization process. We are less concerned with running out of storage, and more concerned about using it effectively. Our top priority is **[intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence")**.

My prediction on a million memory items would refer to a human being who would have devoted her entire life to learning and documenting her progress (e.g. in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo")). This would imply that the documented knowledge would not be much less than the total knowledge. Items may correspond with concepts or inter-concept associations. In other words, the number of concepts would be less than the number of items. The best of the best experts, might be storing 100-300 thousands facts and rules in their memory. Similarly "low' numbers were suggested by Herbert Simon years ago. There are about 100 million cortical microcolumns in the neocortex. There is room plenty enough. For details see: [How much knowledge can human brain hold](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_much_knowledge_can_human_brain_hold "How much knowledge can human brain hold").

With [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), we can show that the brain can easily harbor all [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") we will ever need

## Deconvolution of information streams

Karl Friston using his free energy predictions claims that "the brain is a generative model of its environment" and that "causal regularities in the environment should be recapitulated in its neuroanatomy". We see clear topographical delineations. We see the preservation of topography. We see separate streams of information. We see that at many levels of the brain structure.

Portions of the brain specialize: visual, auditory, etc. This provides for simpler wiring. We do not need to analyze sound to distinguish between blobs in two colors. The same is true if we go down the hierarchy. We may have a visual area dedicated to recognizing faces (in humans as much as in monkeys ). It makes sense to do edge processing, texture processing, etc. in specialized areas too. The lower we go, specialization will be less of inborn wiring, and more of training in the course of development. That efficient separation of information goes down to the very bottom level.

We can go even further by extending the timeline to the course of evolution. When concepts become vital for survival they can be separated into ganglia with privileged wiring, e.g. from cranial nerves. This way a memory may progress from a learned casual reflex, to a well generalized survival strategy, to a strategy genetically imprinted in the preplanned brain wiring.

## We are both distributed and localist

The ultimate truth about connectionism is that we have both worlds live inside the brain and there is no need for a compromise between the camps. Take any neural ensemble and you will see that there are concept neurons, and there are intermediary processors. The only difference between a concept neuron and any other neuron is that concept neurons carry an easy to guess function, while the function of rank-and-file processors is elusive to human interpretation. Computationally and semantically, there is no actual difference.

Any neural network with a single output can be seen as a distributed processor with a localist output leading to a concept cell. A [multilayer perceptron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perceptron) would be a classic example. The input may receive retinal signals, the output may have a form of a visual concept. The processing is parallel and distributed, and the outcome is consolidated in a single axon of a single cell.

Despite receiving a great deal of [conceptualized input](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cells_in_the_medial_temporal_lobe "Concept cells in the medial temporal lobe"), the hippocampus might be strongly connectionist. It is a temporary integrator of information (Damasio's "convergence zone"). It needs to be fast and reliable, but that makes it useful in the short-term only. Sparse coding and speed would be an advantage. As we progress deeper into the neocortex, we see denser representations and lower density of NDMA receptors. This is the place where we should likely find a myriad of concept cells. The number of cortical columns might be in the exact correspondence to the potential extent of the conceptual world of the human mind. [My rough estimations of the lifetime capacity of human memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_much_knowledge_can_human_brain_hold%3F "How much knowledge can human brain hold) fit well in that picture.

## Connectionism has its roots in ignorance

Connectionist models have been built out of our ignorance of how the brain works. In my [Master's Thesis](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Master%27s_Thesis "Master's Thesis") (1990), I mentioned Lashley's experiments that failed to locate the place where the brain stores memory engrams. Brain science of the 1980s was hazy. In addition, in pre-web time, it was hard to get up-to-date literature. However, inspired by Pavlov, I did not experience a shadow of a doubt about [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"). My key inspiration was [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), which can build stable memories for life. In my thinking, there was no room for connectionist [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference").

In a simple mode, a neural network has some input, some output, and some layers. Researchers wondered how the brain might work and looked for algorithms that would tinker with synaptic weight to convert an input pattern into a desired output. The brain is a bit smarter. It does something similar, however, once it finds regularities, such as well-defined [concept cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cell "Concept cell"), it picks them for preservation (see: [Conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization")). By eliminating interfering connections, it can reliably reproduce a desired function without the need to recalculate weights upon receiving new input. A concept neuron fits its stable place in the brain that turns its attention to finding other regularities in the environment.

## Connectionists bred concept cells in the lab

Few people inspired my thinking about [memory optimization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neural_optimization_in_sleep "Neural optimization in sleep") in sleep as much as Sejnowski, Buzsaki, McClelland, McNaughton, O'Reilly, and French. I tried to decode their papers word-for-word to be sure I truly grasp their thinking.

In one of his papers, French noticed:

> Only rarely does new learning in natural cognitive systems completely disrupt or erase previously learned information; that is, natural cognitive systems do not, in general, forget ‘catastrophically’. Unfortunately, though, catastrophic forgetting does occur under certain circumstances in distributed connectionist networks. The very features that give these networks their remarkable abilities to generalize, to function in the presence of degraded input, and so on, are found to be the root cause of catastrophic forgetting. The challenge in this field is to discover how to keep the advantages of distributed connectionist networks while avoiding the problem of catastrophic forgetting

In the quest to develop networks free from catastrophic interference, scientists working on neural networks tried dozens of mechanisms. One of the architectures called ALCOVE quickly evolved in what French called *"for all intents and purposes, a localist network"*. In another system called CALM, some nodes received a special status of protected nodes. They were nothing else than an equivalent of grandmother cells in a smarter variant of a connectionist system. In other word, even connectionist evolve concept cells in their labs.

Today, the most striking example of winner-takes-all approach to neural computing is [competitive learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competitive_learning). In particular, Kohonen networks allow of neural clusters that are capable of feature extraction. Those networks were inspired by the way visual cortex works. The winning neuron in a self-organizing network is nothing else than a [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell").

Nature has already implemented concept neurons in the most primitive nervous systems. It seems it is the connectionist approach that is phylogenetically younger. Slugs and bugs do not have a hippocampus.

Today, the concept of [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability") keeps creeping in the artificial intelligence systems under various names and guises. I have always claimed that artificial intelligence needs the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") -and- [retrievability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retrievability "Retrievability"), and [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep") (see: [Artificial intelligence needs to sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Artificial_intelligence_needs_to_sleep "Artificial intelligence needs to sleep")).

In the early 1990s, French proposed a separation of internal network representations during learning. In 1995, McClelland, McNaughton, & O’Reilly published a highly influential paper that proposed a [dual network architecture](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dual_network_theory "Dual network theory"): [*Why there are complementary learning systems in the hippocampus and neocortex: insights from the successes and failures of connectionist models of learning and memory*](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/7624455). In the brain, the roles of the learning and memory networks would be taken by the hippocampus and the neocortex.

Due to the involvement of the hippocampus, the thinking expressed in papers by French and others is highly connectionist. It never bothered me. However, if we are bashing stars today, I need to stress that in the context of this text, there is tiny flaw in their [dual network theory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dual_network_theory "Dual network theory"). They keep speaking of distributed overlapping representations in the neocortex. Such representations would be an outcome of a neural optimization in which the hippocampus teaches the neocortex about the patterns it has stored in the course of a waking day. However, the concept of teaching with minimum [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") would still evoke *plasticity-stability dilemma*. If we involve [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability"), we no longer need to worry about the overlap. Gradually, the memories may become less distributed, more conceptualized, and more stable. At the end of this long optimization process, we can arrive at concept neurons that will provide stable memories that can last for decades without interference.

The general premise of the dual network theory is correct. However, it should not frame cortical wiring in complex and expensive connectionist terms. The teaching process is supposed to happen every night. I see it as a storage optimization that leads to cheap and efficient memories. There is a reason why we lose brain cells in development and this is considered a good thing. In a connectionist system, we do not want to lose synapses to keep the function of the system intact. In cortical networks, we keep deleting synapses and cells that interfere with fast thinking and with stable memories.

When studying Einstein's brain, we were all surprised with the scarcity of neurons. Instead, the genius mind stocked up on glial cells. This increased the efficiency of processing (sparse network), and the endurance of the system (supplies).

I largely agree with Terry's line of reasoning. He justly questions the Halle Berry experiment. However, his text does not provide a compelling connectionist argument. Here I will only comment on a few minor threads:

> According to the grandmother cell hypothesis, you perceive your grandmother when the cell is active, so it should not fire to any other stimulus

Not exactly. You can think of dumplings and recall your granny. This should fire the concept cell as well.

> There are many neurons in the brain, but not enough for each object and name that you know

This is my field, and I know we have a huge supply of cells capable of coding for objects we want to remember. Encoding quality memories is the bottleneck. There are neurons plenty. We are limited by the brain's effort to maximize is computing capacity. For this it employs [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") that loses information, and a great deal of other forms of [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting").

> The same set of neurons can recognize many different objects by differentially weighting their outputs

This is true and almost certainly a prevalent way of encoding early, when storing memories. Over time, however, it makes more sense to [generalize](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"), and devote specialist assemblies or cells to specific concepts.

> The network can generalize by correctly classifying new inputs from outside the training set

Once we have a clear concept of our own granny, we do not really need to generalize much further. One cell would be the acme of generalization. We can extend our understanding of granny by concept mapping, i.e. adding new connections, e.g. to granny attributes, historic contexts, emotional codes, etc.

> Much more powerful versions of early neural network models, with over 12 layers of hidden units in a hierarchy like that in our visual cortex and using deep learning to adjust billions of synaptic weights are now able to recognize tens of thousands of objects in images

It would be interesting to know the energetic cost, and computational efficiency of such nets. Moreover, human brain can do similar feats, and then build interesting concept maps around individual images. I wonder how would the Google project tackle associating a face with a set of facts, e.g. the face of Ray Kurzweil with his life story reassembled into a [concept map](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_map "Concept map") from the semantics fished from Wikipedia. For the human brain, it is all a matter of simple connections: Ray cell, and a set of [concept cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cell "Concept cell") organized into a constellation. If I am not mistaken, Google works on an idea called *Neural Graph Machines* that combines neural networks with graph-based label propagation algorithm. This kind of system could build knowledge about Ray, incl. facial recognition. The use of graphs to represent semantic connections would be similar to the use of localist concept maps in the neocortex.

In the end, Terry provides the best answers himself. He wonders how many neurons should be involved in the facial recognition system in the visual cortex:

> There are sound theoretical arguments for minimizing the numbers of neurons in the representation of an object. First, sparse coding would be more energy efficient. Second, learning a new object in the same population of neurons leads to interference with the others being represented in the population. An effective and efficient representation would be sparsely distributed

I agree wholeheartedly. If we take sparseness to an extreme, we can obtain concept cells, and then, paradoxically, we can pack them dense in the cortex.

We need grandmother cells for efficient brain computing.

## Churchland opposes scientific modelling

Patricia Churchland is Sejnowski's colleague, coauthor, and fellow connectionist. They both have roots in the most reputable Francis Crick circles. She and her husband's names show up in my [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") database almost as often as Sejnowski. Her credentials are spotless. The kind of work I do, studying the brain with outward behavioral measurements, would probably earn me a badge of a "folk psychologist".

Churchland is a neuropsychologist, neuroscientist, and a radical neurophilosopher. In a separate chapter, in [This idea must die](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/This_idea_must_die "This idea must die"), she went a step further than Sejnowski and called for the end to brain modules. This is the same connectionist spirit from Sejnowski's chapter, taken to a radical extreme.

To call for the death of brain modules is tantamount to a call to the end of the modelling of the brain functionality. It is as if the only brain Churchland would accept is one giant neural network seen as a black box. The only way to understand such a box would be to use analytical tools of neural computing. In her words, any significant "neuronal business" is based on "spatially distributed networks". This holistic approach is the Everest of connectionism.

Churchland's proposition is so radical that I suspect that book editors must have goaded the authors to be as radical as possible, but no further than is needed to be taken seriously.

Having studied brain modules, submodules, and subcomponents of sub-sub-sub-modules for four decades now, I am a staunch localizationist. I have a suspicion that there could be another underlying cause for Churchland's stand. As a connectionist thinker, she may consider studying modules a waste of time. Distribution of neurons in the LGN is a kind of new religious phrenology that sucks oxygen from bona fide neural research. Ironically, it is the study of brain modules with the putative or hypothetical or half-verified function that provides best evidence that shapes the thinking of those who see the brain as a localist collection of concept neurons, ensembles, and concept maps.

Interestingly, to support her reasoning, Churchland uses examples from neuroscience. In the process, she keeps tossing names of "modules", and how their function is hard to pin down, or misleading. If reading braille is an example of functional substitution in the cortex, speaking of "visual cortex" sets all the necessary context to understand the exception. If we are to abolish the visual cortex as a module because of its occasionally unusual characteristics, we only take a big step into the dark abyss of neuro-ignorance.

I take Churchland's text with optimism. If Sejnowski is as serious about the death of the [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") as Churchland is about the death of the visual cortex, we can take the whole [This idea must die](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/This_idea_must_die "This idea must die") book with a grain of salt. It makes for a boatload of inspiration.

## Grandmother cell mechanism

### Robert French concession

In the light of new evidence, one of the towering figures of neuroscience came to grips with the possibility that [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") might be plausible. Robert French work on catastrophic interference inspired lots of my thinking about [REM sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/REM_sleep "REM sleep"). However, he remains skeptical about the grandmother cell theory. In *" [Much ado about nothing](http://leadserv.u-bourgogne.fr/fr/publications/001230-grandmother-cells-much-ado-about-nothing) "*, Robert French wrote:

> We do not dispute the idea that there may, indeed, be neurons in the brain that are very narrowly tuned (i.e. grandmother cells). Not only are there numerous experimental reports of such neurons, but in addition, the hierarchical processing of information in the brain makes the presence of such cells a logical possibility. However, the causally efficacious nature of such neurons at the functional or behaviour level remains to be demonstrated

It is baffling to hear French speak of *causal efficacious nature* of grandmother cells if his own diagram in the quoted paper shows how concept cells are perfectly positioned to take part in the thinking process (without the need to involve effectors). For comparison see my own model in: [How to solve any problem?](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem).

As much as the existence of concept neurons is a logical possibility, I posit that the their value in the thought process is also a logical possibility. For me, the body of evidence is so solid that I cannot think about the brain in terms other than a [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network"). I will try to show a tiny flaw in French's reasoning.

### It is hard to prove the existence of grandmother cells

French noticed that we do not have a solid proof yet:

> We have found no reported cases of the loss of recognition of single, highly familiar objects, which also argues for a lack of causal efficacy of grandmother-cell neurons

I am not sure if proof positive is possible with today's tools. Waves of cells fire in a conscious brain all the time, and only a few concepts hit the attention window at any given time. How could we possibly fire a cell and evoke an image by making sure at the same time the fired unit sends signals that navigate their way to consciousness. A set of new tools would be needed. If we only correlate a cell firing with the input, we can always find a connectionist explanation. However, it is getting harder by the day (e.g. see [Quiroga's morphing experiment](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cells_in_the_medial_temporal_lobe "Concept cells in the medial temporal lobe")).

At the moment, we need to consume research which shows that killing specific cells or synapses kills specific memories. We know it from rats who are unable to report if they forgot or even ever remembered their grandmother.

We need take the whole body of evidence and then the verdict is clear. Spherical shape of the earth was pretty obvious to many people since the times long before Erastothenes, however, only a human eye peek from space in first space flights, two millennia later, provided evidence as clear as the one French would like to see for [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell").

### Cases of dramatic loss of memory

French insists:

> We should be able to find cases of patients who have lost their capacity to recognise one, or a few individuals

We can anecdotally report dozens of similar cases, some short lived, other long lasting. Some via [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting"), other via injury.

With the whole hierarchy of cells leading to a [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"), establishing a few cell, first in the working memory, and then as a substitute in the neocortex should be fast and easy. Recovery might be hard to notice. I explain that on the example of zebra below. When we forget something dramatically obvious, e.g. a name of a movie start, it is hard to say if it is a result of establishing a new diversion route in the mind, or something more sinister like an actual loss of cells. However, the mere act of trying to recall the name will have a reconstructing power.

I love George Foreman. He was the one who made me take on boxing in my teen years. His heavyweight championship at 45 (1994), made me think *"I got many years of youth ahead"*. With his hero status in my mind, I was totally baffled during one of my conversations when I could not recall his name. This was tantamount to forgetting my own name. I do not recall the date and how I recovered from that mishap. I forgot the name of John Nash a few times in moments when I needed his name in a conversation. Nash might be developing into some kind of [toxic memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory") in my case. Next time I talk of Nash, I might forget his name in sheer panic. Lots of people will admit to having similar lapses of [active recall](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Active_recall "Active recall"). Lapses in passive recognition are however very rare. How do we even recognize catastrophic loss of memories. They could be painless and hard to observe. DNA can mutate, cells can die, and what we mostly observe in birth is healthy babies. The losses are not easy to see

### Forgetting zebras

French asked about the proof for the loss of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") using an example of the recognition of zebras:

> There should be reported cases of people who had lost their capacity to recognise just “zebra” or “apple”

Here I present a scenario which shows that such a loss would be almost impossible to notice. Note that some components of that scenario are hypothetical and some of the hypotheses are mine. The purpose of this presentation is to illustrate the plausibility of the loss of grandmother cells that would be entirely impossible to notice.

#### Scenario: learning about zebras

- when we meet a zebra for the first time, our senses send messages to the **sensory cortex**, which engages in **pattern recognition**
- we recognize familiar components of a zebra: stripes, horse-like body, black lips
- recognized patterns will form an **activation map** in **sensory cortices**. This will be our zebra sensory map
- **[grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")** for individual concepts light up in the **neocortex**: stripes, lips, tail, hooves, etc.
- the constellation of activations from the neocortex is sent as an **input pattern** to the hippocampus
- the image of that zebra activation map is captured in the **working memory** in the **hippocampus**, perhaps in a connectionist manner
- we instantly set up a temporary **concept neuron** in the **hippocampus** to fire in response to the image of zebra. This concept neuron will for now respond to a few characteristics we have captured in the first encounter. The concept neuron may fire in response to only some characteristics, or it may require them all on input
- the pathways from the retina to the **hippocampus** are peppered with [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") as shown in this [diagram](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4757852/figure/F1) from Sejnowski's publication
- perhaps the zebra has been mauled by a lion, and it is all in blood and is not using one of its legs. We recognize blood too and register a problem with a leg
- the concept neuron in the hippocampus will spark setting up a similar concept neuron in the **neocortex**, possibly only during the first night of **[sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep")**
- setting up a single representative for a concept is essential for **[generalizations](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization")** and for **[reasoning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem)**. This winner-takes-all phenomenon can be see in **competitive learning**, e.g. in Kohonen networks. The zebra will be represented by a **[concept map](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_map "Concept map")**, of which the **grandmother cell** will play a central role in **integrating** the information about the zebra
- when we first think of zebras, the constellation of concepts will light up in **associative cortex** (input), in the hippocampus (**working memory**), and back up in the neocortex at **hippocampal output**. The dominant activation corresponding with a concept cell for a zebra will be an **attractor** for the arrival of further information
- **competitive learning** will update the **winner input** to make it more likely to win in the future
- in **sleep**, the brain will **integrate** the information about the zebra and [**optimize** its storage](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neural_optimization_in_sleep "Neural optimization in sleep"). This is the time when the hippocampus might be free to generate **teaching input** for the neocortex. All **relevant synapses** will be strengthened, and those that **interfere** with the concept will be suppressed, and possibly deleted in the long run
- new **dendritic spines** will sprout to make sure the new understanding of zebra grows in **[coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence")**
- on the second encounter with a zebra, we may see a healthy individual. This will **suppress** the "missing leg" **association**. The input from the "blood" concept will receive a new status. It will be a likely co-occurrence that plays no role in **recognition**. When we later see a bloody zebra, the blood concept will light up in the **sensory cortex**. It will be sent to the **hippocampus** as part of an **episodic memory**, however, it will have little or no contribution in lighting up the **neocortical grandmother cell** responsible for thinking about zebras (some hints on pattern completion can be found in [Quiroga experiments 2014](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cells_in_the_medial_temporal_lobe "Concept cells in the medial temporal lobe"))
- if we meet zebras in highly unusual circumstances with a great deal of information missing, the **hippocampus** may allocate a new **concept cell** in **working memory**, but may be unable to reactivate the **concept cell in the neocortex**
- in **sleep**, the hippocampus can use that information to **train the neocortex**, and possibly find the commonalities between a **fuzzy concept** of a zebra in **working memory** and the concept cell in the **neocortex**. This is the time when we can enhance our knowledge of zebras. Incidentally, my understanding of this hippocampal training process I owe to Robert French himself. His papers from two decades ago inspired my thinking
- as **working memory** ensures the **hippocampus** has its **input patterns** registered, **sleep** is a good time to look for **neocortical patterns** that provide the closes match with concepts stored in the **hippocampus**. I see that as waves of excitation at lower **activation thresholds**. This cannot be done in the waking mind due to a possible conceptual chaos. New activations can be sent back to the hippocampus for comparison, and then go back to the neocortex in a loop that brings better and better focus on a fuzzy image of coarse coding
- sleep might also be the time of massive **[forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting")**. As new connections are laid, **ineffectual connectivity** is lost or actively erased. Effective neurons may clear inactive **dendritic filopodia** while attracting new **dendritic spines**. [Protein](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/AMPA "AMPA") buildup may help strengthen [stable connections](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). New routes may cause random forgetting by **[interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference")**, which would primarily target weaker pathways. Access to some memories will be lost by re-routing
- **[axonal-dendritic overlap](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Axonal-dendritic_overlap "Axonal-dendritic overlap")** would favor learning things in **[semantic proximity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_distance "Semantic distance")**. This would also determine the areas of best [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity"), and the areas of most change in [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep"). At the same time, the overlaps would be deathly for **[incongruent knowledge](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence").** This is why a connectionist will find this text boring, while I am unlikely to change my mind about [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")
- in the morning, we wake up with a new **insight**. We understand zebras that one bit better. When the **thalamus** opens the gates of **consciousness**, all dream-like activations evaporate almost instantly. The brain returns to its more **[focused](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle")** thinking mode, suppresses rampant nighttime creativity, and permits the flood of **sensory information** to shape new learning
- with each encounter we update the concept of zebra. On one hand, we add new **associations** for the "knowledge about zebras" bank, on the other we keep **[forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting")** lesser characteristic of zebras. This leads to **[generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization")**, and the crystallization of the concept of a zebra
- we may have more concept neurons for the zebra. For example, in addition to the **[grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")**, the **visual cortex** may hold a tip-of-perceptron cell that fires up each time we visually recognize a zebra. That visual concept would map on the **hippocampus** to allow of activating the [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"). Damage to the **visual concept cell** for zebras would only reduce the speed and accuracy of recognizing zebras. It would not affect our reasoning about zebras. Such concepts cells would ideally reinforce their [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") in rich **feedforward and feedback loops**. Each rewarding thought about zebras originated in the [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") would reinforce the pathways shaped via **competitive learning** in the visual recognition hierarchy. Concepts cells would promote their own existence and shape **white matter connectivity** tracts. All those changes have roots in the [pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning")
- when we meet a lion, the **hippocampus** might send some signals to the **amygdala**. Lions may receive high **valuations** as they pose a major threat. In turn, the amygdala will make sure the episode receives higher priority. All connections established while watching lions may be more durable. Those loops make part of my hypothetical mechanism behind the **[knowledge valuation network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_valuation_network "Knowledge valuation network")**
- when we meet a zebra and a lion, we can make an **association** that may lead to a conclusion: activating a concept of "chase" that will make the brain predict a possible chase in which the lion will be a pursuer
- in sleep, **valuations** established by the **amygdala** may play a major role. This is why so many dreams may have nightmarish undertones when we get woken up from their deepest stage. As the brain is sending a great deal of extra signaling associated with the concept map linked to lions, the dream may involve a lion in a totally absurd context. This is why we sleep to make sure we do not act out our dreams. This is why a great deal of the **hippocampal teaching** and **[generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization")** cannot be executed in waking

#### Scenario: losing a grandmother cell

Imagine we meet a new animal, e.g. [thunderbird](http://harrypotter.wikia.com/wiki/Thunderbird) in "Fantastic beasts" ([video](https://youtu.be/QJr0N2BUfTk)). We can instantly set up a concept cell with a few associations, e.g. 6 wings, 2 legs, long tail, eagle beak, feathers, etc. We can begin reasoning. We can hypothesize we are dealing with a bird or a mammal. We can guess the locations of lungs and kidneys. We can continue watching the movie without a sense of being handicapped in any way. It is not a problem we see a thunderbird for the first time. We can continue watching the movie fully entertained.

This is exactly the perception I would expect from someone watching a zebra having lost a concept cell or two. We can instantly set up a new concept cell for the zebra in the hippocampus. The experience of zebra would actually be much richer than that of the thunderbird. This is because it is associated with dozens of images, and video clips, and stories, and episodes from one's life (e.g. visit to a zoo or Africa). We can quickly reconstruct a zebra from associated sub-concepts such as stripes, horse-like body, fly-swatting tail, savannah, or fear of lions. All those subconcepts would form an entangled concept map of which the lost [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") might have been the most prominent. However, the zebra concept cells is surrounded with a great deal of redundancy: dozens of conceptual associations. Connectionists point to the wonders of pattern completion in neural networks. Conceptual networks posses the exactly same quality. Representations differ, but redundancy plays the same role.

Having lost the [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell"), we would not have lost the ability to recognize zebras. We would be able to reason freely about zebras. We would remember a great deal about zebras. However, we might have lost some of the connections. This would reduce the [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") of the memory, and our ability to be smart about zebras. If someone told you that a zebra got stripes because it is a cross-breed of black or white horses, you would usually have a good laugh. However, this laugh may come from the good connectivity of the zebra concept with other aspects of knowledge about zebras and biology in general. Concepts cells might be more about fast thinking and creative association than about processing zebra images, including the recognition stage.

If we go further, lose more cells, and degrade our understanding of zebras to the level of a thunderbird, we might not even notice!

Alzheimer's is a different story, access routes get ravaged even though the concept cells might be surviving. In Alzheimer's, losses to memory can be dramatic, and yet, frequently, they do not seem to make much impression on the patient. In early stages, they are even hard to notice for friends and family. Could we see Reagan's lapses of memory as a serious injury to select concept cells? I bet we rather saw a normal thing that can happen to anyone. If we do indeed lose the main concept cell associated with zebras, the only measurable effect would be the lost access to some of the memories that did not have redundant links with other zebra memories. For example, we might lose the access to the name "zebra". I see it happen in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") all the time. Nobody's memory is free from this vulnerability. Even a dozen of repetitions cannot make a memory bullet proof. The surest anti-forgetting tool is redundancy, esp. if established efficiently via [knowledge darwinism](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_darwinism "Knowledge darwinism").

Incidentally, when trying to recall the thunderbird, I gave it 4 wings and 6 legs. It has easily established itself as a concept in my mind. This concept is coarsely coded, misrepresented, and I was not bothered by the fact. As a form of exercise, I was able to paint the bird, and compare it with the real thing. The difference became obvious only when recall met reality. In a well designed memory system, with this level of coarseness, we would only need 8-20 concepts to be associated with the concept of a thunderbird. Given some painting talent and some extra time, I might reconstruct the whole thunderbird to a degree that it might fool half of the audience as the real McCoy.

Loss of a single grandmother cell would remain unnoticed. It is possible that we lose or forget quite a number of grandmothers each day

## Summary: Grandmother cells make humans intelligent

I have mentioned a number of reasons why [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") are essential for the efficient functioning of the neocortex. We cannot model the brain with neural nets that are algebraic, sensitive to catastrophic forgetting, unable of convergent [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"), unable to physically rewire, etc. The brain is doing a better job than that. Here is a short summary that hierarchically explains how overlapping benefits add up to the acme of neural processing in the form of **human intelligence**:

- **simplicity**: knowledge distributed in concept neurons is easy to manage for life
- **cost**: the cost of re-weighting synapses is significant. It is ok to re-compute hundreds of synaptic weights upon a stimulus in an ANN. In real life, rewiring needs to be more methodical and judicious. Connectionist approach is used mostly to capture the chaos of the incoming information. The cost of this process is high, and one of its side effects is the need to sleep
- **stability**: concept neurons make it possible to encode knowledge of high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). Memories can be stored for decades without interfering with other memories
- **speed**: concept neurons and stable connections provide for fast and efficient computing based on deterministic routing
- **compactness**: instead of expensive sparseness, we can have non-overlapping representation that can, in theory, be densely packed in the cortex
- **reliability**: knowledge of high stability is resistant to interference. It makes it possible to build large and [coherent](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") semantic structures that underlie wisdom
- **generalization**: modelling is essential for intelligence. Connectionist systems are good generalizers in short-term only. A major change of input and they can forget catastrophically. Connectionist systems generalize by expensive re-computation. Biological system generalize by smart [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting"), and [optimized wiring](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_optimization "Memory optimization")
- **association**: in theory, an association between concept neurons can be built with a single connection. Connectionist approach requires synaptic reweighting
- **creativity**: creative powers of the human brain are based on an association of ideas. To form a creative breakthrough, in the simplest case, requires the firing of two [concept neurons](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") in an overlapping time frame
- **intelligence** combines (1) [stable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), well [generalized](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"), and [coherent](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") knowledge, (2) [fast thinking](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fast_thinking "Fast thinking"), and (3) [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity"). Fast thinking is facilitated in simple stable deterministic networks that elicit unambiguous firing

The joke of the [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") turns out to be the key ingredient of human intelligence:

Human intelligence is based on the deployment of a vast [network of concept maps](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem)

- [Two component model of long-term memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_component_model_of_long-term_memory "Two component model of long-term memory") provides strong novel evidence for the existence of [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell")
- With [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), we can show that the brain can easily harbor all [grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") we will ever need
- Loss of a single [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") would likely remain unnoticed. It is possible that we lose or forget quite a number of grandmothers each day

A [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") is the atomic constituent of a concept map in the brain
- Mechanistic interpretability in artificial intelligence provides new light on the emergence and the role of concept neurons in [conceptual computation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptual_computation "Conceptual computation")
