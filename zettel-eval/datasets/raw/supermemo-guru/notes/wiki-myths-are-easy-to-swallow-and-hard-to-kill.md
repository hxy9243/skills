## Humans with awful memory

Children and the elderly have awfully bad memories. They are both vulnerable to human mythology. We are bombarded with fake news and false beliefs from morning to evening. Some of those false beliefs we absorb. Everyone is susceptible. No amount of knowledge or genius can prevent one from consuming a myth and accepting it as a scientific truth. Toddlers and grannies are easiest victims. However, they become victims for entirely different reasons. While a toddler stands a great chance to recover, a granny will probably die with a set of entrenched myths stored deep in her brain. This says little about granny's cognitive health. This is mostly a testimony to the course of the development of the human brain over a typical lifespan. Healthy brains differ vastly depending on the stage in their development. Despite another popular myth, brain development continues in octogenarians too. It may be less exuberant, but the remolding of human cognition never ends.

## Old dogs do not learn

Old men are no less vulnerable than old ladies, however, I intentionally use the label of "granny" because I instantly recall my own grandmother. She kept saying *"if you keep running around without a T-shirt in winter, you will catch pneumonia"*. I would say, *"Don't worry. [Infections are caused by bugs](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Myth:_We_catch_a_cold_from_cold "Myth: We catch a cold from cold"). You cannot catch a cold from fresh air while playing football".* Granny would accept my explanation, nod her head, consume the scientific model and the next day, she would repeat the same message all over again. As if her brain was impervious to a correction. Grannies are often cognitive champions. They sport great knowledge and great memory. However, they still often somehow seem unable to learn simple things.

## Models of memory: origin of myths

[Two component model of memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_component_model_of_memory "Two component model of memory") provides a simple explanation to that seeming paradox of a healthy brain resisting new knowledge. When we learn new things, we encode memories with low **[stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability")**. New memories are vulnerable to [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting"). Exposure to the same knowledge, esp. after a break, enhances the [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). If the false belief of " [catching a cold from cold](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Myth:_We_catch_a_cold_from_cold "Myth: We catch a cold from cold") " comes back each time anyone catches a cold, after a few decades, the myth becomes entrenched and very stable. Stabilities of some memories at older ages can grow to exorbitant values. It seems no amount of hammering can break those memories. If a granny learns about the discovery of cold viruses (published in the 1950s), she may keep her mind open, and encode the new information. Her brain has a capacity to break the old model, build a new one, and store is as a set of new memories. However, those new memories have low [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"). Only their high [retrievability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retrievability "Retrievability") makes a granny think correctly about possible causes of a cold. For a short while, granny seems to have made a breakthrough until... the new memories fade. Depending on the age, the old model's stability, and the time till the next exposure (to information about viruses or the cold virus itself) granny can easily forget the new memories and the old model re-emerges easily. At times, the speed of [fast thinking](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fast_thinking "Fast thinking") takes over and high stability memories win over the new high retrievability memories. This explains why a fast brain isn't always a superior brain. If the old railway network wins the battle, granny's goose is cooked. She will have likely forgotten the new explanation and have fallen back on her old knowledge. It can literally take minutes for the damage to be done. Granny's brain is old. But it does not mean it is cognitively impaired, it is old in old stable knowledge. Grannies make great experts in old knowledge. No wonder that in seeking wisdom, we often turn to the elderly. When exploring that wisdom, naturally, we need to be cautious and highly selective.

## Naïve toddlers

As for toddlers, their brains grow so fast that establishing new memories is very hard (see: [Childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia")). This is particularly visible for "intentional" memories. This is why early schooling is a grave error. However, a fraction of memories, esp. in subsystems essential for survival, keeps establishing new routes that may last a lifetime. There is nothing wrong with children watching cartoons with immortal characters that die seventeen times per episode. For toddlers, wrong models are harmless as long as there is [no limit on exploration](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/On_freedom_of_education_and_freedom_of_information "On freedom of education and freedom of information"). Moreover, wrong models can accelerate [crystallization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Crystallization "Crystallization") of correct models through [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"). Myths and false models are a form of cognitive training. They can improve IQ. They can improve immunity to fake news. On the other hand, we can easily do damage by accelerated academic training, [religious indoctrination](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Ban_on_homeschooling "Ban on homeschooling"), [coercive learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion_in_learning "Coercion in learning"), or just by yelling at the kid. Few things accelerate development as effectively as stress. Cortisol and other factors in the stress response are [excellent brain sculptors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_baby_brain_does_not_work "How baby brain does not work"). However, the brain that gets sculpted early leaves little material for the sculptor to work with in adolescence. If you train a child with a rod and make it sing and memorize verses, it will work. A child may even remember a verse or a song for life. However, such early training can also destroy child's prospect for long-term development. Stunted brain growth will be complemented by the hate of learning. [Free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") maximizes crystallization of new models, and the development of cognitive immunity to falsehood and deception.

Even milder forms of [coercive learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercive_learning "Coercive learning") can lead to [toxic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memories "Toxic memories"). A kid drilled early and heavily in multiplication table may develop a lifelong anxiety about multiplication or math in general. Even if there is no anxiety, *[parasitic memories](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Parasitic_memories "Parasitic memories")*, as their name implies, can sap cognitive processing for years to come.

## Myth vulnerability in adulthood

As for all ages in between a toddler and a granny, our susceptibility to myth depends on prior knowledge and prior training. Schools condition students to accept higher authority. Whatever is dished out in textbooks is supposed to be all true. Allegedly, textbooks need no verification (for which there is no time at school anyway due to the high speed of [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming")). [Free learners](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") get burnt with falsehoods so often that their inner skeptic is red hot. I keep learning stupid things with [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") because the universe of knowledge is so vast that it is impossible to verify all things we learn. However, each time I get burnt, I strengthen my skeptic habits. Unless I read from a "shaky consensus" of Wikipedia, I always check the source and the author. Check [me](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Piotr_Wozniak "Piotr Wozniak")!

## Myth remedies

Myths are hard to kill. Understanding the above myth memory mechanics helps the battle with falsehoods. The above text should also make everyone more relaxed about consuming myths. It will happen to everyone and there is no need to commit seppuku upon falling a victim.

The trick to recovery from a myth poisoning is to tackle the corrective knowledge with utmost priority. You do not need to be a granny to fall victim to a granny mechanism, i.e. (1) learn a correction to an entrenched myth and (2) forget the correction after a sufficiently longer interval. Time favors the old myth, which starts the battle with a higher [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") of memory. My best, but not hermetic, solution to killing myths is to urgently use [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). With [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition"), we can assist memory in overriding the old false model. At times it can be very hard. Moreover, as much as it is a remedy, [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") can also help entrench wrong knowledge that may only feel so much more obvious, and so much more correct. **Memory stability feels like correctness!** In other words, building stable memories carries a risk of feeling omniscient. Even a die hard skeptic is vulnerable.

For natural reasons, I believe my own mind is myth free. That's the part of the subversive power of myths: they reside in the brain without causing pain. The pain shows up only when myths emerge in real life applications. However, I harbor some **true** memories of well-entrenched stability that illustrate the problem. In my own case, Polish names of plants and animals are an example of early memories that keep interfering with English names. I learned those Polish names in early childhood. When I see a "harvest mouse", I instantly recall its Polish name. I need to dig deep and use precious milliseconds to find the English equivalent.

## Anti-myth formula

This is my formula to combat false beliefs:

- **toddlers and preschoolers**: do not worry about myths. They will get corrected in due time!
- **adults**: rich lifelong learning is a formula for vast and reliable knowledge (see: [Knowledge in creative problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_in_creative_problem_solving "Knowledge in creative problem solving"))
- **nonagenarians**: use [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") or just enjoy living: your wisdom is highly appreciated!

[Free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") is the best remedy for false beliefs. Best learning is learning that is rich, wide, free, and lifelong

Old and new knowledge in creative problem solving

> ***Figure:** Creative problem solving requires (1) vast expert knowledge of high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), and (2) rich new knowledge of high [retrievability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retrievability "Retrievability"). Vast stable knowledge makes it easy to solve algorithmic problems. Those problems can be solved at low energy expenditure with the help of [fast thinking](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fast_thinking "Fast thinking"). Problems that require "thinking out of the box" rely on [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity"), i.e. association of remote ideas. Creativity and [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") are powered by "hungry knowledge", i.e. fresh knowledge that can easily be molded and [generalized](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") via [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting"). This plasticity provides for good pattern matching in new learning and in creative problem solving*
