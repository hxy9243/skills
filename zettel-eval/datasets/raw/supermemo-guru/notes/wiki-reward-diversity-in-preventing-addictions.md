## Paradox of addictions

While fearing addiction, we often take measures that increase addiction. An interesting example is [gaming disorder](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Gaming_disorder "Gaming disorder") in children. Gaming addiction is allegedly caused by [variable reward](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Variable_reward "Variable reward"), however, inconsistent parenting can produce a more dangerous variability at a higher level. In the end, it may turn out that a parent is more guilty than a game developer. In addition, [compulsory schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling "Compulsory schooling") takes away the last vestiges of freedom from the young generation. This is how we trade [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence") for addictions. [Compulsory schooling must end](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling_must_end "Compulsory schooling must end")

The negative impact of [variable reward](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Variable_reward "Variable reward") can be minimized when there are rich sources of diverse rewards in life

## Amplified variable reward

Bruce Alexander's Rat Park experiments provided the first clue that a caged rat and a (relatively) free rat, have pretty different personalities. The propensity to addiction increases with the degree of reward deprivation. Stress potentiates the strength of the reinforcing reward. Anti-depressants reduce gambling. There are many indicators that the injury to the reward system may reduce the number of reward routes, and the existing routes may easily be depleted. This may be the mechanism of depression.

Vulnerable reward-deprived individuals are more prone to respond to **variable reward**. In a healthy brain, when the amplitude of reward and penalty signals is low, the network looks for an average or for a trailing average (for highly fluctuating signals). In a healthy brain, spending some time in the casino is enough to detect the negative expected payoff. In a healthy brain, some time with computer games is enough to turn on executive self-control, make a higher-level assessment, and look for remedies against the net loss. In addition, multiple sources of reward result in more consistent resultant reward signal: the simple pleasure of living. That consistency helps to drown fluctuations caused by occasional penalties.

This is not the case when the response to the reward is amplified, esp. when it comes from a singular source. It may begin with staking too much in a casino. However, the main societal problem arises from the fact that by the age of 15, a great proportion of the schooled population is psychologically injured. With rampant [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness") and loss of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), the effects of variable reward become amplified. Neurophysiologically, the loss of freedom at school is on average less severe but not much different than a loss of freedom for a caged rat. For a [bullied kid](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bullying "Bullying"), a rat cage might actually feel like a safe harbor.

The sensitized brain will look for all clues to detect patterns that determine the source of reward or the source of penalty. Instead of a simple reward averaging, and a simple behavioral strategy (e.g. engage or quit), with the increase in the signal valence, the brain increases its effort in feature extraction. This is supposed to help associate clues and behaviors with reward. This is why a distressed gambler is a "better" gambler.

When the rewards and penalties increase in magnitude and do not come too close together, the brain tries to find out patterns. This may lead to irrational thoughts like *"Friday is my lucky day"*, or *"I was nice for God today so he should help me win"*. A cool mind will just not experience the roller-coaster and stay cold to the experience.

For a modern kid, beyond child abuse, [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness") is primarily born at school. When a child is constantly forced to go against its own instincts in learning and in behavior, the brain decouples decisions from reward. In other words, through conditioning, the child stops responding to natural rewarding stimuli, e.g. as those generated by the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") (i.e. the instinctive force that is subject to the highest degree of suppression at school). If the suppression has a form of the [war of the networks](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/War_of_the_networks "War of the networks"), we arrive at a child that is apathetic and devoid of higher goals. If prime sources of reward are cut off at the neural level, all available forms or reward become more attractive in proportion to the deprivation. This is why the abused child is more likely to be addicted to drugs or alcohol. This is why schools have a major contribution to addictions. If someone goes to Facebook to feel better about herself, we know it is a dangerous indicator of a potential for addiction.

If [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness") leads to depression, we may use anti-depressants to prop up depleted reward routes. However, that's only the first assistive step. In the long run, recovery of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") and other natural reward pathways is a long process that requires establishing new connections in the brain. That recovery will also improve the neurally efficient response to **variable reward**.

[Diversity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Diversity "Diversity") of reward is an important defense against the dangers of **[variable reward](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Variable_reward "Variable reward")**. Reward diversity is preventive in depression

## Parents do more harm than videogames

When a baby cries in the night, a mom with a heart in the right place will spring up to her feet and soothe. In the ideal case, [co-sleeping](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Co-sleeping "Co-sleeping") would only require snuggling up to the bundle. However, modern parenting "experts" keep coming up with new "inventions" such as "cry it out method", or allegedly behavioristically-inspired "take your time" method. In that latter case, when the baby cries, the parent is supposed not to reward the "tantrum". Instead, she might soothe the baby every 3 times, or perhaps every 5 times, on a variable declining schedule. This is not a way to condition a child to "behave properly". It is a method to make the kid go loco on variable reward until [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness") sets in. That's a formula for abuse we know from Ceausescu orphanages. A cry is an astronomically important biological signal that there is a problem with the system. The only right course of action is maximum assistance.

A baby cry should never be subject to training. The signal must remain unadulterated

Rewards may be used throughout development. When they are supposed to control physiology, they can be harmful. If a child receives a candy for sitting down on a potty, chances are it will seek potty more often. If that reward is intermittent, potty might become a favorite sitting place. Child physiology should better be left alone, and all forms of "rational" control are better deferred. Instead of an occasional wet mishap at school, we may end up with adults who can control their bladder at Got Talent level only to end up on dialysis or on a kidney transplant ward.

The main risk for developing a [gaming disorder](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Gaming_disorder "Gaming disorder") stems largely from the assault on child's freedoms. In an attempt to control gaming time, parents introduce unclear rules, spark fights, impose restrictions, improvise, confiscate devices, impose penalty chores, etc. The more irrational and unpredictable their behavior, the more the reward of access to gaming become variable reward. In addition, school plays a similar role and adds to the sense of loss of control. This is exactly how variable reward drives gambling at the level above the game itself. The reward is the access to the game, not the tricks that a game developer might have programmed in to keep kids addicted. A healthy brain will saturate. The joy and attraction cannot last for ever. There are other attractions in life. There are other games. There is fatigue. There are many balancing forces that terminate gaming without the fall into addiction (see: [Videogames and TV tend to saturate](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Videogames_and_TV_tend_to_saturate "Videogames and TV tend to saturate")).

Amplified by parental fears, harmful myths about the [gaming disorder](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Gaming_disorder "Gaming disorder") spread fast and virally. For a good example, see the myths mass-produced by Dr [Manfred Spitzer](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Manfred_Spitzer "Manfred Spitzer") which I describe here: [The morbid myth of Digital Dementia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/The_morbid_myth_of_Digital_Dementia "The morbid myth of Digital Dementia"). The net result is that parents live in a state of anxiety about the future of their kids. This makes them jittery and irrational. They permit and then deny, and then permit again. They come up with new ideas (e.g. *"let's lend that Xbox to the neighbor"*). This is exactly the approach recommended by Nir Eyal to keep someone mad about a product. In the end, well intentioned parents may do more harm than the games themselves (see: [Dangers of imposing screen time limits on children](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dangers_of_imposing_screen_time_limits_on_children "Dangers of imposing screen time limits on children")).

Variable reward in game addiction is often generated by a parent, not by the games

My mom tried to control my bedtime in youth. I recall reading books with a torch under the coverlet. It was the time my attraction to books reached its peak. I was a voracious reader. When reading books became compulsory in high school, and when the choice of the books was controlled at the ministerial level, I developed a hate for Polish literature (see: [I do not read books](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/I_do_not_read_books "I do not read books")). **Healthy kids are [programmed to resist control](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Education_counteracts_evolution "Education counteracts evolution")**. This simple observation should be familiar to any minimally observant parent of a child with a reasonably preserved autonomy. Sadly, the observation has little impact on the design of education systems around the world

There is a simple remedy to the negative effects of parental indecision and vacillation beyond consistency that only partly mitigates the loss of freedom. That remedy is **unconditional love**. Unconditional love is hard, esp. if the brain is bombarded by the stressors of modern society from all directions. Parents are naturally equipped with all necessary instincts needed to love unconditionally. At times, adoptive parents may prove superior to natural parents. One of the strongest predictors is how much the child is wanted in the family in the first place. In families obsessed with the arrival of a new family member, unconditional love comes easy and this starts a cycle of positive feedback of love and development that amplifies love. When a kid is unwanted or unexpected, bad start may become a self-sustaining string of emotional upheavals. A rational remedy might be to keep negative emotions inside? When love is hard to come by, giving a kid room to grow might restart the engine of positive feedback. Usually, these are short term gains. Families seem to have their own [behavioral systems](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Behavioral_system "Behavioral system") pretty well set and those behavioral system determine the outcome.

Unconditional love is one of the best remedies against variability that leads to addictions

Parents put a lid on computer games or on the use of YouTube because they fear addiction. Variable reward of electronic media is alleged to be the hook. Instead, parents should focus on the root cause of the drive towards addictive behaviors. If the valence of the signals is high, it is not without reason. Perhaps the kid needs more freedom or more attachment. The sad thing is that once the brain gets molded by lovelessness and helplessness, freedom is not a remedy. It may actually be used for more gaming, gambling, drugs or alcohol. If this is the case, we can say that freedom came too late. The patient has already been damaged.

Untainted sense of freedom is more important than the avoidance of addictive agents

## Learning as sustainable reward

Learning is the most stable and sustainable type of reward! Good learning is a formula for good life. Learning is helpful in depression, however, its efficiency is limited because depression suppresses the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"). As a result, learning is inhibited in depression. This leads to a vicious cycle of a depressed mind: less learning, more depression, even less learning, etc.

An interesting question emerges: is [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") rewarding? With a string of unpredictable successes and failures, there is something variably rewarding in repetitions. For that to be the case, knowledge must be [well formulated](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/20_rules "20 rules"). Otherwise we may end up with [hating SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Hating_SuperMemo "Hating SuperMemo"). However, if the formulation is right, does the [pleasure of knowing](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_knowing "Pleasure of knowing") beat the [boredom](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Boredom "Boredom") of repetition devoid of novelty? Are good grades rewarding? Having used [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") for 20 years, I find it harder to testify. In [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), [repetitions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Repetition "Repetition") are interleaved with the learning of new things, which is highly rewarding (see: [Pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning")). The pleasure of incremental reading is continuous and sustainable. Pure [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") is more like variable reward, and only when done in the right way. Failure is usually a form of penalty, but failure due to interference can be particularly painful (e.g. answering "fish hook" instead of "gaff"). Repetitions at the very late hour or in a wrong [circadian frame](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_frame "Circadian frame") can be pretty unpleasant and can condition the brain to dislike SuperMemo (I know a user who likes late repetition "sweetened" with beer, which I am not sure is a good idea). Discovering useless knowledge may be unpleasant but it is instantly remedied by a pleasurable **Delete**. Pleasure comes from many source: [pleasure of knowing](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_knowing "Pleasure of knowing") (esp. in cases of high [applicability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicability "Applicability")), satisfaction with very long-intervals that make [items](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item") "disappear" from view, re-discovering forgotten knowledge (in here failure can be pleasant too), or even the sense of "duty done" (which may not be too beneficial for learning per se). Bad item formulation may be a case of an unhappy encounter that turns happy upon reformulation. In that last case, we have a formula for success. Users who are vigilant to reformulate tend to last. Those who are too lazy to improve, tend to sense that their knowledge gets stale. When leeches start building up, pleasure is gone, efficiency drops, users may give up SuperMemo for long stretches of time or drop out altogether.

Well-managed [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") is a form of enjoyable [variable reward](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Variable_reward "Variable reward")

Spacing will play some role in the pleasure of learning, however, [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") is a perfect example of a never-ending stream of high reward that will keep the student going on even better than if the reward was to be intermittent or variable.

Nir Eyal shows how variable reward can be used in marketing. Over the decades dozens of people advised how to make SuperMemo more addictive, how to use gamification, or how to capitalize on variable reward. My stance is stubborn. [Incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") is its own best reward. Its power comes from the productive quest that keeps eliminating chaff in the search for golden nuggets of knowledge. [Spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") associated with incremental reading is doubly efficient because of (1) [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence"), (2) use of [cloze](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze "Cloze"), and due to (3) [knowledge darwinism](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_darwinism "Knowledge darwinism").

Those who do not enjoy [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") must figure out why. If learning is not its own reward, it is not occuring beyond a short-term effect.

Gamification would turn SuperMemo into a school: fake rewards for fake learning

Products that intentionally employ variable reward are like a capricious girlfriend that plays hard to get. This is not my area of interest. In SuperMemo, nobody controls your drive. It is driven by the quest for knowledge. In Eyal's terminology, I see myself as a Facilitator in the Manipulation Matrix: I love to work on improving [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") because I love to use it myself.

In [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), we have a hunt for valuable knowledge. Penalties come from texts that are hard to understand, however, a whole array of weapons can be deployed to minimize the penalty: ignore or delete, schedule for later, reduce priority, etc. At the same time, rewards are maximized by extracts, setting high priority, generating stable knowledge, pleasure of [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") for highly [applicable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicable "Applicable") knowledge, [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence"), etc. With rewards in a dominant position, penalties do not add much to the variability of the [learntropy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learntropy "Learntropy") signal. As a result, learntropy becomes a trailing average of sustainable pleasure that is proportional to skills, prior knowledge, bank of value in the collection, and the brain state. The best brain state for [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") is high creativity mixed up on demand with high attention (see: [Natural creativity cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle")). The main driving force of incremental reading is the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"). As such incremental reading is mildly addictive, and entirely harmless. The addiction is self-limiting as learning is good on its own and limited by available windows of high alertness. In a decade, the pleasure is likely to increase, the cost is likely to drop, and the benefits will keep accruing as good interest. That's the opposite of maladaptive addiction.

Well-managed [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") is a source of sustainable high reward

## Hook oversold

The idea of variable reward is becoming a popular meme. As such it is often oversold. It seems to be a panacea whose flaws are swept under the carpet.

If variable reward is used to reward employees, it is bound to result in some frustration, and some focus redirected from creative pursuits in the quest for rewards. In a salesforce, variable reward might work for the bottom line (at least for a while). However, if a sales person is motivated by her own rewards instead of the service provided, there are casualties on the way: the quality of the service, customer satisfaction, employee satisfaction, and, ultimately, the quality of life in society. In that sense, I might call variable reward a capricious reward.

If social media companies try to use the "capricious reward", it is a very short-sighted strategy. Facebook, Twitter or Instagram may play with variable reward, but in the long run this backfires. Neural networks can struggle with decoding the reward and produce an obsession, but there is the executive control that makes sure there is a good summary of the impact of social media on one's life. The higher level assessment will tell the user of Facebook: *"You are wasting your life here"*. Self-control comes to rescue and people delete their accounts in droves just because it became fashionable and rational. Due to this high level of instability, Facebook may once be destroyed by a surprise competitor in a matter of months. All past discussions and uploads will be forgotten or erased with few regrets.

Abuse of [variable reward](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Variable_reward "Variable reward") can backfire

Instead of playing with capricious reward, we should focus on creativity, problem solving, and inherent value. Everyone would benefit.

I was asked many times to join Twitter, and I keep getting contradictory assessment. Too many people give up after a while. Naturally, [Donald Trump](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Donald_Trump "Donald Trump") will not. It does not really matter to what degree [variable reward](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Variable_reward "Variable reward") participates in Trump's Twitter obsession. As long as it is a great weapon to accomplish his goals, his being immersed in the technology is entirely justified. [Kamala Harris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kamala_Harris) 's call to close Trump's account would be a serious violation of free speech, and a case for her tendency for "prosecutorial over-reach". I do not use Twitter and I know Trump's tweeting only by receiving a filtered selection from the media. Interesting, but not the best investment of one's time

Someone compared [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") to Twitter. Both tend to deal with fragmentary pieces of knowledge. However, that is one of the very few characteristics that the two technologies have in common. [Incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") is based on knowledge structure, concept maps (in the collection and/or in user's memory), and [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") that ensures lasting [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") of [applicable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicable "Applicable") knowledge that underlies [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence"). There is no twitterization of knowledge in SuperMemo. While Twitter is immensely popular, incremental reading is just at the beginning of the adoption road (see: [Why is incremental reading not popular?](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Why_is_incremental_reading_not_popular%3F "Why is incremental reading not popular)). It is not popular primarily because it is not easy for a bystander to see the knowledge infrastructure that builds at the backroom of the effort behind classifying all pieces of knowledge into a complex processing network. Incremental reading keeps growing mostly because of its ability to generate genuine value and sustainable reward for its users

## Omnipresence of variable reward

[Barefoot](https://www.nirandfar.com/why-you-should-run-your-business) marketing guru Nir Eyal got a hammer and he can see nails everywhere. I have the same problem, I see [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") as a universal remedy to human problem for the same reason. Eyal extends the influence of variable reward a bit too far. Here is a quote ([source](http://www.nirandfar.com/2012/03/want-to-hook-your-users-drive-them-crazy.html)):

> There are variable rewards we seek for personal gratification. For example, from birth, things that stimulate our senses mesmerize us. Babies put everything in their mouths for the same reason there are flashing neon lights in Las Vegas. We love novel sensory stimulation

Bad odors stimulate senses, but they do not mesmerize. Babies put things in their mouth because of the inborn program. They need no reward to continue. Flashing lights have more to do with salience than reward. They may be pretty annoying too. We are attracted to novel stimuli even if they are not so pleasant, however, we love new [applicable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicable "Applicable") knowledge even more, and best learning can be largely free from reward variability.

Eyal adds:

> We are the most relentlessly curious species on the planet, having made more sense of our surroundings than any other animal. But it is this same impulse, to search endlessly, never satisfied, that creates habitual behavior from many new technologies

[Learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") reward is somewhat similar to [variable reward](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Variable_reward "Variable reward"). The reward is proportional to discovery, it is immediate in the wake of discovery, while discovery does not need to be immediate in learning. Penalization is moderate, and comes primarily from [decoding failure penalty](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Decoding_failure_penalty "Decoding failure penalty") and scarcity of discovery. In today's rich access to knowledge sources on the web, even that scarcity factor is likely to be replaced with a degree of anxiety caused by information overload. All that variability can best be expressed as the [learntropy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learntropy "Learntropy") of the input signal. It does not take much training for a free mind to enjoy rich benefits of positive learntropy. A healthy mind is invariably curious, and this is a thing to celebrate. The addictive power of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") serves a good purpose, and should always be cherished and maximized.

Variable reward is potent but it is not as omnipresent as it is painted by some marketing gurus or by scaremongering critics of digital media (e.g. [Manfred Spitzer](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Manfred_Spitzer "Manfred Spitzer")). Most of all, it is far less potent when handled by healthy brains with good reward computational power. If variable reward works in digital products, it is also a verdict on the health of the product's audience. If they get hooked and behave irrationally, the true reason is the brain's health, not the nature of the reward signal.

My life seems to be driven by habit developed around rules. The rules evolved to maximize productivity. Productivity is supposed to serve the goals, but the goals have already been internalized and do not occupy much of the processor's power. The rules were introduced incrementally, in baby steps, which made it easy to run a productive life without much struggle with self-discipline. In this kind of lifestyle, I seem to be impervious to variable reward that is said to attack users of digital media. My day is organized by [Plan](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Plan "Plan"). My learning is shaped by [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"). My work is prioritized by a set of tools in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). My exposure to variability is then dramatically reduced. I may not be able to fully sense the power of variable reward, but I can testify that digital life can be devoid of major woes. Productive digital life is fun

## Variable reward in gaming

There are many factors that make [videogames](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Videogames "Videogames") attractive. Variable reward is just one of many. [Learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") is a healthy force that makes kids delve into games. Very often, they will do scientific simulations and still call those games. This language does not help in combating the opposition from the adult world. My own case of gaming is interesting as it shows an entirely different force at play: competitiveness. Variable reward did not play a role, or its role was minimal. I loved games only to prove my superiority above the opposition. At the same time, my case shows that the "addiction" can easily be broken assuming a healthy brain and a bit of maturity. I admit that my own experience may add a bit of bias in my thinking, however, I have spoken to too many kids to be worried about [gaming disorder](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Gaming_disorder "Gaming disorder") or [digital dementia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Digital_dementia "Digital dementia") beyond most pathological cases with root cause located well beyond the world of gaming.

I understand the attraction of computer games because I was drawn into gaming at least twice in 1986. This was the year I got my first computer, the famous ZX Spectrum. One of the games spread illegally on cassette tapes was Harrier Attack in which the player could bomb the Falklands and score points. The game was fun on its own, however, it was not variable reward that worked in this case. It was the social factor. The most powerful force behind Harrier was a competition between several of my colleagues studying computer science.

On May 2, 1986, me and a group of friends played rounds on Harrier in the late evening to find the best player. Losers were dropping out and going home. Their main excuse was that the hour was late, and they were sleepy. More ambitious losers where doing pushups upon each lost round. Among the players, there was [Krzysztof Biedalak](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Krzysztof_Biedalak "Krzysztof Biedalak") ([SuperMemo World](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_World "SuperMemo World") 's CEO).

Me and [Biedalak](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Biedalak "Biedalak") are very different. Contrasted with my childish approach to life, he is an acme of stability and seriousness. In our circles, he is often the only adult in the room. This makes me wonder how I managed to drag him into bombing Falklands. As Biedalak was in the more ambitious group, he was sweating on the floor a lot while combating sleepiness of a conscientious early riser. My secret weapon was that I did at lot of Harrier before the contest. I was the only one who owned a computer, and practiced the game a lot for the sake of a win. I did not admit my practice to my opponents. In addition, at midnight, for a night owl like myself, the game seemed even more attractive. On that night I pulverized my friends. This was a reward I cherished more than the variability of scores on the way.

This quest to beat the competition might have slowed down [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") in its early years. I did not tell many people about my secret [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") technology. Around 1985-1986, I loved to excel in English class without admitting to having had a secret weapon for magic "vocabulary retention". I did not feel guilty about keeping things secret, because I was convinced that some serious research must have long uncovered [optimum intervals](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Optimum_interval "Optimum interval") in learning. Me and Biedalak were in a competition for grades. This is how our friendship started. The same was the story of [Tomek Szynalski](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Tomek_Szynalski "Tomek Szynalski") (of Antimoon.com) who competed with his friends in high school. [Alfie Kohn](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Alfie_Kohn "Alfie Kohn") might not be too convinced, but those cases of competition were pretty healthy (see: [The case for competition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/The_case_for_competition "The case for competition")). They lead to more knowledge, to friendships, and later to partnerships (cf. Steve Wozniak or [Bill Gates](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bill_Gates "Bill Gates")). It is important that all the competitiveness came from within (i.e. it was not pre-arranged by adults). Incidentally, when learning English, Szynalski made a good start with the help of gaming. He also safely outgrew the habit.

Three month after the Harrier competition, I was drawn into a game again. This time it was about beating my late brother. The game was Manic Miner. Again I used my secret weapon of gaining advantage by practicing ahead of time. On Aug 24, 1986, I proudly beat my brother's 12 hours effort in just 3 hours. However, this time the win was of mixed reward. On one hand, it felt great to prove my superiority. On the other, the summer vacation was coming to the end, and my realization of missed opportunities started gnawing at me. Due to night-time gaming, I lost a lot of sleep. My programming projects were behind schedule. My English was progressing too slowly, etc. This is when a rational brain kicked in and I decided to quit games for ever.

Today, my gaming skills are ridiculously bad. Each time I get invited to a game, with research on my mind, I quit fast. I am just all thumbs. The window of opportunity has closed. I will probably never have fun with games. The story is a bit similar to my parting with literature. I appreciate the benefits, but cannot find time and sufficient motivation to practice and to return. The activation threshold is too high.

All my knowledge of gaming today comes from observing children. I see them drawn in, getting blasted by parents, getting addicted, failing at school, learning a lot from games, changing games often, getting bored often, quitting, and sometimes even vowing not to return. All stories seem to fall into the same pattern. In healthy cases, kid saturate and quit at some point (or just play intermittently, e.g. with their own kids, years later). In morbid cases, under a pressure from parents and school, the addiction gets worse, and may even land a child in a reform school

There are many rewards that draw children into gaming. The best lure away from the habit are competing rewards of a productive life
