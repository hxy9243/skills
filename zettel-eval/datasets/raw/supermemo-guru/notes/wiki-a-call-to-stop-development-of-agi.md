## My notes

### Intelligence as its own guardrail

The article begins by explaining the obvious, then moves on to making claims without explaining:

1. *"Releasing AGI into the world would be a terrible mistake"*
2. *"Trying to achieve AGI is a dangerous act"*

Only then we hear: *"widely available AGI would, sooner or later, have no guardrails"*. Isn't that analogous to saying, *"humans have no guardrails"* (beyond those imposed by society)?

The best guardrail against humans and AIs is a vast [brain-like super-intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurocracy "Neurocracy") that helps individual actors act intelligently in synergy and responds to rogue actors. It would be analogous to today's states, except it would encompass the entire known [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence") and be based on [neurocracy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurocracy "Neurocracy") rather than democracy or other systems.

Intelligence itself is the best guardrail against itself

### Humans are weaker

ChatGPT quickly proved superiority of its intelligence over mine (esp. that I was hobbled by time contracts). Its first reservation is powerful. It is true.

Equating “humans have no guardrails” with “AGI would have no guardrails” is an imprecise analogy. Humans are embedded in slow, messy social institutions, biological constraints, and physical scarcity; they aren’t trivially copied, massively speed-scaled, or instantaneously self-replicated. AGI’s digital nature and potential for rapid self-improvement/change make the kinds of failure modes very different in kind (not just degree). The article stresses exactly those digital advantages (copying, scaling, self-exfiltration)

My reply is *"So what?"*. The evolution towards super-intelligence will be faster, less predictable from human perspective, and more dangerous from human perspective. By we also need to factor in self-correcting nature of intelligence. Corrections to the trajectory will also be faster and increasingly more accurate. This is a convergent process whose ultimate outcome is largely inevitable. I predict it will be positive (see: [AI is good](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/AI_is_good "AI is good")).

### Circular control

The reservation about circular control is actually weak and must have been derived from human sources.

Circular / paradoxical control claim: “We should build super-intelligence to control super-intelligence.” That’s tempting, but it’s circular. To use an AGI as an external guardrail you must already have sufficient confidence that the AGI you build is aligned and robust — which is the very problem you were worried the community can’t solve. In practice this is the classic “control problem”: building the thing you need in order to guarantee you can safely build it

The same problem could be voiced at the start of human evolution in reference to the emergence of benevolent intelligence. It is a non-issue.

### Neurocracy

[Neurocracy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurocracy "Neurocracy") is my own term. It has not been well-defined yet beyond explaining it is a form of a [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network"). ChatGPT simply does not understand the concept, and the fault is largely mine:

**Understates the political and value-plurality problem**. A single “neurocracy” inevitably embodies specific value choices. Human values are plural and contested across cultures and institutions; any global brain would need a mechanism to reconcile or choose between incompatible values. That creates normative and political conflicts that are as important as the technical alignment problem

The entire point of neurocracy is the harmonious synergy in conditions of value plurality. We can see how it is the case in the brain, which is not bothered by contradicting optimization signals from its various subsystems.

### Inevitability

Here we have a little misunderstanding:

**Treating AGI inevitability as a reason to stop other action is risky logic**. You write as if superintelligence’s arrival is inevitable and therefore governance debates are futile. In reality: timelines are uncertain and contested among experts; that uncertainty increases the importance of preparing governance and technical mitigations now rather than abandoning them. (If it’s soon, we need urgent work; if it’s far, we have time to iterate on safer frameworks.)

I do not intend to call for inaction. I say that opposition is largely futile and might be dangerous if it leads to polarization: humans vs. AI. As this text proves, I support the conversation. I just want it to be intelligent (hence the call for [neurocratic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neurocracy "Neurocracy") super-intelligence). I don't mind the opposition. On my part, I am just a bit busier with speeding ahead as opposed to engaging into slowing down those who counteract the progression of AI.
