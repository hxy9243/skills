The origin of false memories is best understood by deconstructing the three key properties of memory: (1) reconstructive power, (2) [generalizing power](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"), and (3) creative power.

The following properties of memory determine human intelligence, and may also contribute to forming false memories:

- **reconstructive power** of memory makes it possible to fill in missing pieces and enable pattern recognition in conditions of incomplete information. For example, an elephant trunk may suffice to recognize the entire elephant
- **[generalizing power](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization")** of memory makes it possible to reject secondary details to increase the applicability of memory to multiple contexts. This way, the same pattern can be recognized and used in a wider range of contexts. For example, discounting for the size of ears, makes it possible to recognize African and Asian elephants alike. In the same manner, tusks may be eliminated as a necessary condition for recognizing elephants
- **creative power** of memory makes it possible to add new details to memory for the purpose of problem solving. For example, imagining an elephant falling off a cliff might help one visualize theory of gravity or theory of relativity
- **[forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting")**, in addition to forming memories in the first place, forgetting is the key property of memory that keeps memories in constant flow that maximizes the adaptation to environment. Forgetting in particularly important for the generalizing power of the human mind

Reconstructive, generalizing and creative powers of memory give origins to false memories through the following mechanisms:

- **pattern recognition**: in the process of adding missing pieces, a false completion may occur. This process can form a new false memory
- **[generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization")**: in the process of generalization, a false match may become possible. This match can lead to a new memory
- **creative elaboration**: in story telling, creative mind can perpetually add new details to old memories. If this process continues for long enough, the original episode may become unrecognizable. A new memory of the episode forms, and it may get [consolidated](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Consolidation "Consolidation") over time
- **re-association**: two true memories can be falsely connected by a new creative association. This process requires a substantial degree of forgetting
- **misinformation** (false input): new memories can form as a result of a false input, e.g. suggestion, fake news, memory therapy, police pressure, etc. (see [Ted talk by Elizabeth Loftus](https://www.youtube.com/watch))
- **blending**: fusing distinct memories is the most distortive process that requires a high degree of forgetting and the involvement of one or more other false memory processes mentioned above. For example, two different episodes may become a single memory episode. This can, for example, make it possible to link a wrong perpetrator to an actual crime that one has witnessed

Due to a huge [memory turnover](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") in early childhood, memories acquired in youth are least reliable. Recall from the ages 2-7 is peppered with false episodes and distorted memories. They may feel real to the owner of memories, but many memories have often been planted, acquired later, or re-molded through the distortive processes mentioned above.

Once a false memory is effectively planted, it may integrate with the rest of episodic memories and enter the process of [consolidation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Consolidation "Consolidation") (which includes [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"), increase in [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), creative elaboration, etc.). After a longer while, planted memories can feel real. A highly intelligent and reliable adult may swear on his life that a false memory is actually a recall of a real event from his or her life.

The above mechanisms are often used to question the reliability of a witness. This is why it is essential to remember that the probability of misappropriating a memory of high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") is extremely low. For example, traumatic events experienced in adulthood and stabilized promptly after the fact are unlikely to be a subject of false memory formation.

Stable memories can only become unstable by prolonged disuse (e.g. decades). Stable memories can also be dislodged via [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference"), e.g. brutal and prolonged police interrogation. Otherwise, **probability of supplanting stable memories is very low**.

Credibility of the testimony of a witnesses who claims early and stable memories should not be questioned on the grounds of false memory implantation.

False memories cannot be investigated with a lie detector or future brain scans. They are integrated with a person's knowledge and do not physically differ from memories that are more accurate.

False memories can be used to prevent [war of the networks](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/War_of_the_networks "War of the networks"). For example, Elizabeth Loftus succeeded in planting false memories related to dietary preferences. Her techniques can be used in therapy.
