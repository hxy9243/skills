## Increase in sleep problems

[Passive schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Passive_schooling "Passive schooling") is harmful for [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence"). It should be replaced with [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning"). For that reason, [compulsory schooling must end](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Compulsory_schooling_must_end "Compulsory schooling must end").

[Sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") is a new and incrementally increasing problem that adds to the [problem of schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_of_schooling "Problem of schooling").

The omnipresence of electronic devices emitting blue light in the evening results in a new balance of [zeitgebers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Zeitgeber "Zeitgeber") that change the positioning of the human [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle") in reference to the hours of sunlight. Children naturally wake up later today than they would a generation ago. This is particularly noticeable at teen years, and may progress towards [DSPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/DSPS "DSPS"), which can throw teen biology into chaos and result in serious consequences for health.

Due to this generational [phase shift](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Phase_shift "Phase shift") in sleep cycles, [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") at school increases, and it may not be enough to start school later. Social awareness of the problem needs to increase. We are all alarmed with the epidemic of suicides among the youth, and yet few people seem to trace the problem back to an important contributor to the mental health issues: sleep disorders.

## Double whammy

Most children go to school sleep deprived. At the neural level, this has two prime consequences: (1) it accelerates the conditioning towards the [hate of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Hate_of_learning "Hate of learning") (i.e. the loss of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive")), and (2) it accelerates [memory interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_interference "Memory interference") that can undo the prior learning effort.

### Amplified hate of learning

[Pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning") is essential for success in learning. It underlies future [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence") of today's students. Those facts are encapsulated in the [Fundamental law of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Fundamental_law_of_learning "Fundamental law of learning") that should be a guiding light for all parents and teachers. Children need to be free to make educational decisions.

The [hate of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Hate_of_learning "Hate of learning") develops as a result of conditioning. The brain associates [decoding failure penalty](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Decoding_failure_penalty "Decoding failure penalty") with salient attributes of schooling (at first) and the learning effort in general (later). In other words, the child is exposed to persistent conditioning that shows that school and learning are unpleasant and should be avoided.

In lay terms, the neural effects of [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") can be explained with a [Swiss cheese model of homeostatic fatigue](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Swiss_cheese_model_of_homeostatic_fatigue "Swiss cheese model of homeostatic fatigue"). After a longer period of cognitive effort, small patches of the cortex may lose their ability to process information or even experience microsleep. This can be visualized as a neural network with its portions functionally disabled: like a Swiss cheese network (see: [Local sleep in portions of the awake cortex](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Local_sleep_in_portions_of_the_awake_cortex "Local sleep in portions of the awake cortex")). This type of network will gradually lose its functionality, which may, at random, increase a [semantic distance](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_distance "Semantic distance") in learning and increase chances for [neural penalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Decoding_failure_penalty "Decoding failure penalty"). We all know that learning can be particularly unpleasant when we are tired. When we experience [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation"), [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") fatigue occurs much faster. A brain woken up abruptly, e.g. with an [alarm clock](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Alarm_clock "Alarm clock"), may never reach its natural cognitive potential. In substantial [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation"), the Swiss cheese memory is a problem throughout the waking day.

[Sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") speeds up the development of the [hate of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Hate_of_learning "Hate of learning") associated with [schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schooling "Schooling")

### New learning can ruin prior learning

The Swiss cheese phenomenon may also undo the prior learning efforts through [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference"). If the brain is forced to undergo [active recall](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Active_recall "Active recall") in the [Swiss cheese state](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Swiss_cheese_model "Swiss cheese model"), it may inadvertently produce false associations. If the recall provides a wrong result (e.g. wrong answer in [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition")), that result may become associated with the question. The new false association may receive priority in terms of [memory stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_stability "Memory stability"). As such it will get perpetuated. In other words, the student will **undo good learning** by forming false memories. [Interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") is an omnipresent phenomenon in a healthy brain, and underlies the cognitive power of natural [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") in shaping problem solving capabilities (see: [Abstract knowledge](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Abstract_knowledge "Abstract knowledge")). However, in a Swiss cheese state, the error rate in neural computing increases exponentially leading to accelerated forgetting. The effects of learning will always balance good learning progress (new memories), with bad interference regress (wrong memories displacing good memories). With the progression of [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") fatigue, this balance is gradually shifted towards bad learning. At some degree of fatigue, learning is counterproductive, i.e. it does more damage than good. The brain naturally prevents bad learning by making it unpleasant.

In [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation"), learning can undo prior learning via magnified [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference")

## Long-term neural injury

Paradoxically, Swiss cheese state may provide creative disruption to resolve stalled creativity (see: [How to solve any problem](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem "How to solve any problem")), however, that process should be entirely spontaneous. Involuntary active learning in the condition of fatigue should be discouraged due to (1) [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") that ruins prior learning, and (2) long-term effects that lead to neurodegenerative disorders (see: [Bad learning contributes to Alzheimer's](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bad_learning_contributes_to_Alzheimer%27s "Bad learning contributes to Alzheimer's")).

Learning in the condition of [sleep deprivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") today, may contribute to Alzheimer's disease decades later

## Let kids sleep

The conclusions should be obvious to all parents, teachers and educators. We should never forcefully wake up children for school.

#### Free sleep is a basic child's right
