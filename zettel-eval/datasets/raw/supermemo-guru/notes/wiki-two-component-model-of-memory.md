## Outline of the model

The **two component model of long-term memory** asserts that two independent variables are sufficient to describe the status of [unitary memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Atomic_memory "Atomic memory") in a healthy human brain:

- [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") (S) - this variable determines how long a memory can last if undisturbed and if not retrieved
- [retrievability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retrievability "Retrievability") (R) - this variable determines the probability of retrieving a memory at a given time

## Biological function

We believe the model is an expression of an evolutionary need to prevent catastrophic [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference") in neocortical networks. [Stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") of memory will reduce changes to synaptic weights and prevent [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") of memories marked as highly useful. On the other hand, [spacing effect](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spacing_effect "Spacing effect") protects memories from achieving high [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") on frequent exposure (see: [Structural and molecular mechanisms of the spacing effect](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Structural_and_molecular_mechanisms_of_the_spacing_effect "Structural and molecular mechanisms of the spacing effect")).

Two component model of memory resolves the plasticity-stability dilemma

## Origin

In literature on memory research, an ill-defined term of *memory strength* will often stand for either stability (S) or retrievability (R). This results in a great deal of confusion that slows down the progress of memory research. In [1990](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Optimization_of_learning "Optimization of learning") and [1995](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/ANE1995 "ANE1995"), we have proven theoretically that the two variables: [S](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") and [R](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retrievability "Retrievability") are sufficient to describe the status of memory in [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition").

Independent research by [Bjork](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Robert_Bjork "Robert Bjork"), formulated as *New Theory of Disuse* (1992), uses analogous terms of *storage strength* and *retrieval strength*. See also: [Jost's Law](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Jost%27s_Law "Jost's Law").

For a historic take on the development of the model see: [Two components of memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_components_of_memory "Two components of memory")

## Significance

The model can be used in [the analysis of molecular and structural properties of long-term memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Modelling_molecular_and_structural_long-term_memory "Modelling molecular and structural long-term memory").

The ultimate practical proof for the correctness of the two component model comes in the implementation and practical outcomes of [Algorithm SM-17](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Algorithm_SM-17 "Algorithm SM-17") in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). The model and the algorithm make it possible to quantitatively study memory. For example see: [How much knowledge can human brain hold](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_much_knowledge_can_human_brain_hold "How much knowledge can human brain hold")?

The model provides evidence in support of the [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") theory. See: [The truth about grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/The_truth_about_grandmother_cells "The truth about grandmother cells")
