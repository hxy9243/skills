## Optimizing the timing of brainwork

Twice a day, we peak in intellectual performance. We can improve our productivity by understanding the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle")

### Circadian graph and brainwork

Charting the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle") makes it possible to find the best windows of time for brainwork. The [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") graph below was generated with [SleepChart](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SleepChart "SleepChart") using a log of [free running sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_running_sleep "Free running sleep"). In the graph, two yellow bands indicate the optimum time for brainwork. The exact timing may differ for each individual. However, the blocks can be easily determined:

1. **Morning block**: soon after waking, after morning coffee, or after breakfast. The best brain slot may last 2-4 hours. If you are sleepy in the morning see: [Natural creativity cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle")
2. **Evening block**: soon after [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta"). The second best brain slot may last 2-4 hours as well. If you do not nap, you may not fully benefit from that block (see: [Power nap](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Power_nap "Power nap")). If you are sleepy during this slot see: [Best time for napping](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Best_time_for_napping "Best time for napping")

### Best brainwork time

The optimum timing of brainwork requires both high [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") alertness and high [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") alertness. There are two quality alertness blocks during the day: the first occurs after awakening and the second after the [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") period. Both are marked as yellow blocks in the graph (above). For best learning and best creative results, use these yellow blocks for brainwork. [Caffeine](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Factors_that_affect_sleep) can only be used to enhance alertness early in this optimum window. Later use will affect sleep (the half-life of caffeine is about six hours). The optimum timing of exercise may vary depending on your [exercise](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Factors_that_affect_sleep) goals and the optimum timing of zeitgebers (e.g. early morning for [DSPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/DSPS "DSPS") people and evening for [ASPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Advanced_Sleep_Phase_Syndrome_\(ASPS\) "Advanced Sleep Phase Syndrome (ASPS)") people). In this example, the stress block is followed by the exercise block to counterbalance the hormonal and neural effects of [stress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Factors_that_affect_sleep) before the [siesta](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Siesta "Siesta") (see: [Stress valves](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stress_valve "Stress valve")). Unmarked white areas can be used for the lunch (before siesta) and fun time unrelated to work in the evening at a time when the ascending [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") sleepiness makes creative work ineffective. That white evening protective zone should be free from stress, [alcohol](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Factors_that_affect_sleep), [caffeine](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Factors_that_affect_sleep), etc. Recommended activities might include fun games, relaxation, TV, reading, family-time, DIY, housework, etc. For inveterate workaholics, less challenging and stress-free jobs might also work ok.

**The best test for a well designed day is that all activities [should be fun](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning")!**

Brainwork is fun only if your brain is ready. Sleep is fun if you are ready. Rest and entertainment feel right only after a productive day. Even a bit of [stress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stress_resilience "Stress resilience") can be fun if it is properly dosed and timed. You do not need to be an adrenaline junkie to enjoy your stress and exercise slots. There is little exaggeration in saying that **a good understanding of the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle") is the key to a happy and productive day!** (see: [Formula for happy life](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Formula_for_happy_life "Formula for happy life")).

Happy and productive life is best achieved by adhering to the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle")

### Natural creativity cycle

In addition to the variables of the [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle"), the [natural creativity cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle") employs the fact that the neocortex operates in several modes determined by local fatigue, [creative meandering](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity"), and [neural optimization in sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neural_optimization_in_sleep "Neural optimization in sleep"). During the day, natural transitions from high focus to distraction, to high creativity, and to rest will occur in proportion to the engagement of individual areas of the cortex. It will depend on the [concept maps](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_map "Concept map") involved in the [mental computation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mental_computation "Mental computation"), and the overall neural activation. At night, the brain will proceed with natural [memory optimization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_optimization "Memory optimization") by sequentially switching between [NREM](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/NREM "NREM") and [REM](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/REM "REM") sleep modes.

All brainwork should be based on self-regulation and natural transitions to high [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") and/or rest. All forms of artificial control will increase the fatigue and reduce the productivity

For details see: [Natural creativity cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Natural_creativity_cycle "Natural creativity cycle")

### Balanced 24 hour cycle

The slanting green line separates the graph into the areas of phase advanced (right) and phase delays (left). The line is determined by points in the graph where the waking time (horizontal axis) added to the sleep time (left vertical axis) equals to 24.0 hours. The place where the green breakeven line crosses the red sleep length line determines the optimum balanced sleep cycle of 24 hours. In the presented example, 17.35 hours of waking, added to the expected 6.65 hours of sleep time complete a balanced full 24 hours sleep-wake cycle. The greater the angle between the green and red lines, the harder it is to balance sleep and fit it into the 24h cycle of the rotating earth. In the example, adding waking hours does not shorten sleep much enough to make the balance easy. This implies that a religious adherence to a 17.35 day may be necessary to balance the cycle. However, this shortened waking day may increase [sleep latency](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_latency "Sleep latency") and increase the probability of premature awakening, which can also tip the balance towards the phase delay. The vertical aqua line shows where the expected sleep time added to the waking time equals to 24 hours (crossover with the green line representing a perfect 24-hour day). In [DSPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/DSPS "DSPS") or [ASPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Advanced_Sleep_Phase_Syndrome_\(ASPS\) "Advanced Sleep Phase Syndrome (ASPS)") that 24h balance may be hard to accomplish. For example, without medical intervention, only a large protective zone in the evening, early nap (or no nap), and intense morning exercise can help balance the day in [DSPS](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/DSPS "DSPS").

**Important!** This graph is based on data that is true solely for a free running sleep condition. If you use an alarm clock to regulate the timing of your sleep, these measurements and recommendations may not apply! In addition, the timing and amplitude of changes differ vastly between individuals!

## Sleeping against your natural rhythm

If you sleep against your natural rhythm you will often experience tiredness or drowsiness that can be resolved by adjusting the sleeping hours. In healthy individuals, the daytime alertness is primarily determined by:

1. [circadian phase and homeostatic sleepiness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Two_components_of_sleep "Two components of sleep")
2. total sleep time the night before
3. [amount of slow-wave sleep the night before](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_optimization_in_sleep)
4. regular adherence to the sleep-wake schedule in preceding days
5. [sleep deficits](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_deficit "Sleep deficit") accumulated in the preceding days (e.g. [[wiki-how-do-we-fall-asleep-3f|REM deficit]], SWA deficit, etc.)

All those factors are closely associated with the sleep phase. [Free running sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Formula_for_good_sleep:_free_running_sleep "Formula for good sleep: free running sleep") provides the best way to maximize alertness throughout a waking day. Free running sleep is likely to shift the minimum temperature point from the early morning closer towards the middle of the [subjective night](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Subjective_night "Subjective night"). You should notice [increased sleepiness before going to sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Insomnia "Insomnia") and no [sleep inertia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep_inertia "Sleep inertia") upon awakening! If you cannot free-run your sleep, it is very important to understand the relationship between your [homeostatic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeostatic "Homeostatic") and [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") sleep drives as compiled in the table below. In the course of the day, you should move in sync between the yellow areas of the table, i.e. [[wiki-how-do-we-fall-asleep-3f|from perfect alertness to maximum sleepiness, and then back to perfect alertness]]. The gray areas illustrate when your sleep falls out of sync:

|  | High circadian sleepiness | Low circadian sleepiness |
| --- | --- | --- |
| High homeostatic sleepiness | **Peak of the night**: You are very drowsy and fall into refreshing sleep with latency of less than five minutes | **Insomnia**: You are tossing and turning in bed. You are very tired but you cannot fall asleep. Your temperature, blood pressure and pulse are raised. Your thoughts are racing  **Solution**: Wait for the arrival of the [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") phase. Delay going to sleep by 3-6 hours |
| Low homeostatic sleepiness | **Hypersomnia**: You are drowsy throughout the day despite long sleep hours. Napping does not help. You show minimum energy levels. Your muscles are weak and atonic  **Solution**: Adjust your sleep phase to your [circadian](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian "Circadian") (e.g. try to go to sleep 3-6 hours later) | **Peak of the day**: You are alert, energetic, and full of new ideas |
