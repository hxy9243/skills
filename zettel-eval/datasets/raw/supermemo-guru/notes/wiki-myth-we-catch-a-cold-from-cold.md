## No virus, no infection

For centuries people have been convinced that cold causes colds and influenza. In the 1950s, it has been shown nicely that colds are caused by viruses and have very little to do with the temperature (even though they are still scientists out there who try to prove otherwise). People in Antarctica do not catch a cold because there is no virus around.

See: [Formula for common cold prevention](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Formula_for_common_cold_prevention "Formula for common cold prevention")

## Origins of the myth

Here are the main factors that contribute to the spreading of the myth:

- colds are seasonal and many viruses spread best in colder or rainier seasons due to longer stays indoors
- many viruses survive longer in lower temperatures
- reducing body temperature will temporarily reduce immune defenses, which may reveal latent infections
- the delays in the chain of causality make people believe that cold is the main cause of the infection
- excessive exposure to cold is as harmful as excessive exercise
- lower temperature in the nose helps the virus, while winter swimmers can keep their mucosa warmer in cold weather
- fever is a great defense in viral infections and it is hard to maintain it when exposed to a cold

To take the above facts and claim that cold can cause a cold is like saying *cars can cause a cold* because car exhaust can also reduce the immunity ever so slightly. In addition, heavy exposure to carbon monoxide can bring an individual to a verge of death when, in recovery, even natural commensals can cause a serious respiratory infection.

Analogously, we might say that *exercise makes you weak* because a good workout may temporarily weaken the muscles.

The myth is pervasive and even super-smart people may believe it. Even a doctoral degree in medical sciences may not prevent the belief.

## Personal experiences

This is the typical scenario that makes people swear they got sick from cold:

1. they get infected with the virus
2. in a day or two, as the virus proliferates, their immune system begins the battle
3. one of the chief weapons against viruses is fever, and a cascade of immune processes associated with fever
4. in adverse conditions, when the body is unable to raise the temperature to the desired level, shivering begins
5. shivering make people believe they got exposed to uniquely low temperatures for too long
6. in a day or two, symptoms of infection follow and are immediately associated with shivering *"Oh! I remember that time I got frozen stiff on a bus stop!"*

Here is another scenario:

1. you eat frozen ice cream
2. low temperature irritates or injures the mucous membrane in the throat, which can feel like an onset of an infection
3. new unrelated infection can easily be associated with "tingling in the throat"
4. latent infections are also likely to show up first in the injured place

Until people understand the above sequences, they will never kill the myth of "catching cold from cold". The name "cold" does not help either. It is culturally pervasive: colds are called "cold" in many languages.

Correlation with cold weather has its own contribution too. In cold weather, central heating adds more to the risk than the weather itself. Crowding indoor in small spaces in winter is another contributor to seasonality. It would then be safer to believe in a myth that "central heating causes colds and influenza".

## The myth is extremely harmful!

The biggest problem with the myth is that **cold can actually be the best form of prevention against colds and flu**. Runny noses flush the virus. Hot bodies kill the virus (cold improves thermoregulation). Regular exposure to cold turns the immune system into a fast reacting fire-fighting team.

I combat the myth with passion because exposure to cold has been my own [prime weapon against infections](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Formula_for_common_cold_prevention "Formula for common cold prevention"). Moreover, **the myth prevents a [healthy lifestyle in children](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Daycare_infections "Daycare infections")**. A healthy body and a healthy brain should be intolerant of heat. Overheating is the enemy of the brain cells and of the brainwork. Millions of loving grannies keep their grandkids protected in warm hats in summer. This is bad for societal IQ.

**Cold helps combating colds!**

**Caution:** exposure to cold is like exposure to other forms of acute stress or exercise. Micro-injuries result in adaptations. Phasing in the program too fast may backfire. Progression must be incremental. 30 minute ice swimming session without preparation can end up as bad as running a marathon without preparation. Good transitions take months. Adaptations are like body building: 4 grueling hours in the gym on Day #1 will not result in bigger muscles. They will result in injury.

## Coronavirus update (2020)

### Explosion of knowledge of virology

2019 coronavirus outbreak has one fantastically positive side effect. The media is saturated with detailed instructions about how to prevent the infection. The public learns about the value of washing hands, not touching one's face, avoiding crowded spaces, keeping a distance, elbow bumps, etc. After weeks of bombardment in the news, I am yet to hear a single case of someone saying "dress up warmly" to prevent the infection. The outbreak turns out extremely educational. The question remains if the public returns to the old myth when the name coronavirus disappears from the news and the old term *"catching a cold"* comes back? The old myth will have to get undermined to a degree. The young generation will have the idea of viral transmission well ingrained in their memory.

### Climate puzzle

For a longer while, politicians and the media speculated that the arrival of summer might slow down the epidemic. Supposedly, the heat and the UV radiation are great enemies of the virus. However, once the pandemic found new strengths in California, Arizona, Florida, or Spain, those hopes were considered dashed. To this day (Aug 1, 2020), very little attention is paid to the fact that the key to seasonality lays in human habits. Winter colds and summer heats might be equally bad due to keeping people indoors. Paradoxically, and shamefully, in early April, Polish government banned access to parks, woods, and beaches, while these are the places were people should spend a great deal of time to safely add to their immune resistance. Polish minister of health, Lucas Szumowski, infamously said: *"Don't go for a jog. This is not the time to work on your physical condition"*. I proudly exercised my civil disobedience and regularly sneaked my way to the woods despite a rich net of police patrols.
