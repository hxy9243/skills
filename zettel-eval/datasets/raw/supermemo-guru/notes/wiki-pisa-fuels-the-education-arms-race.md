## Best brains and the future of education

[Fareed Zakaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fareed_Zakaria) is one of the smartest journalists I know. I love his [GPS program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fareed_Zakaria_GPS). If I ever missed one, it must have been due to the [Daylight Saving Time](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Daylight_Saving_Time "Daylight Saving Time") that disrupts the digital life of the planet twice per year (fortunatly, DST is [soon to be ditched by the EU](https://www.dw.com/en/eu-to-stop-changing-the-clocks-in-2019/a-45495680) at long last).

On September 2, 2018, Zakaria invited one of the prime experts on education: [Andreas Schleicher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreas_Schleicher). Zakaria's introduction was enticing ([transcript](http://transcripts.cnn.com/TRANSCRIPTS/1809/02/fzgps.01.html)):

> Andreas Schleicher has been schooling himself on education for decades. He works for the OECD and is the mastermind behind the [PISA test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programme_for_International_Student_Assessment) which compares school systems around the world. He has written a fascinating book about the lessons he's learned. It's called "World Class: Building a 21st Century School System"

With those credential on the table you might think you would hear a breakthrough solution for modern schooling. Instead, we got served a formula for an **educational disaster**!

That disaster is based on erroneous ideas of global optimization based on central control. The same idea that was originally espoused by [Bill Gates](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bill_Gates "Bill Gates").

Here is the putative algorithm:

1. run a set of education systems
2. measure the outcomes
3. compare the systems
4. let worse systems learn from better systems by copying the solutions
5. go back to Step 1

This optimization has been used for nearly two centuries now and, despite improved optimization criteria (incl. PISA), it progressively leads to worsening societal outcomes. It isn't much different from trying to apply a similar algorithm to designing a perfect car using user satisfaction ratings. Perfect design would work in one place only: [the perfect nation state of North Korea](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/North_Korea "North Korea"). How about a perfect computer application that would satisfy everyone?

Pisa tests can measure a small part of what we expect from education. It won't reflect future mental health or adult productivity. Improvements in schooling and testing fail to translate to happier and cohesive populations. The reasons is that global optimization of education is not possible. Quality measurements are inadequate. **We cannot effectively measure the outcomes, and we cannot provide a single formula for all individuals**! The test incentivizes heavy learning, and penalizes independence, [passion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_passions "Childhood passions"), [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity"), and [problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_solving "Problem solving"). The experts, incl. Schleicher, will tell you that testing for problem solving is their prime goal, however, they keep neglecting the elephant in the room: [coercion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion "Coercion") cannot lead to better learning, let alone better creativity or problem solving. Today, the fastest avenue towards high Pisa scores is [direct instruction](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Direct_instruction "Direct instruction"), and direct instruction entails [coercion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion_in_learning "Coercion in learning").

## Problem solving is based on knowledge

We need to remember that problem solving is knowledge-based and for that reason it is always domain-specific. As a result, it is also culture-specific. [India is a technology power house](https://www.weforum.org/agenda/2017/09/indias-rising-sectors). Dismal performance by India in Pisa tests, resulted in a boycott that was explained by cultural disconnect that disadvantaged Indian students. Even the change of words from " *avocado* " to " *mango* " can impact the results.

A great problem solver in the area of pest control may be powerless in the field of architecture. The transfer of knowledge and skills is usually minimal across domains that are that far apart. [Free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") helps develop a rich transferrable problem solving toolset. Tech-tools like [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") are a good metaphor as they are universal and can be employed in any domain.

Willy-nilly, Pisa needs to ground its tests in domain-specific knowledge, or it will just become another version of an IQ test. That reliance of knowledge will always favor those who are universally and broadly learned. Maximization of broad learning results in undermining [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") and [problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_solving "Problem solving") skills. Education should aim at balancing the two. For more see: [Knowledge in creative problem solving](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_in_creative_problem_solving "Knowledge in creative problem solving").

It is a bit like a balance between exercise and learning. A bit of jogging is good for the brain and will boost learning. Maximization of jogging performance would make learning hardly possible. Maximization of learning would easily steal precious time from jogging. To measure and optimize education, we would always need to take human opus vitae (life's work) into the equation. This would introduce a lag of decades. Pisa can provide rich suggestions, but it cannot be an optimization criterion.

The paradox is that you [cannot teach "problem solving"](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-013-9243-1). You can only let kids free, and let them solve problems. This is why all teaching will be domain-specific, all tests will be domain-specific, and both will favor enslavement as opposed to freedom. Instead of schooling, we should ask kids to solve problems. Reward them if need be: XPrize for kids, Nobel Prize for kids, grants for kids, etc. We should then watch the fruits grow.

I can easily solve problems in the domain of [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") (hence the nick *[SuperMemo Guru](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_Guru "SuperMemo Guru")*). I might be strong at sleep, memory, or creativity. However, I am totally useless in pest control, architecture, rocket science, or brain surgery. I cannot even drive a car. If I scored well in Pisa, it would not be due to my problem solving capacity. It would only be due to the fact that my field, [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), requires a great deal of learning for the sake of testing the learning tools. We need great problem solvers in many domains, and the best measure of problem solving capacity is the fruits: problems solved

Paul Allen sponsors work on an artificial intelligence system that shall be able to pass 8th grade science exams. This should be a clear warning. If machines can do it, it cannot make the hard core of problem solving. That hard core is reserved for humans for another 15-30 years. Ray Kurzweil promised me that I will live long enough to see it. I believe his prophesy.

## Conclusions drawn from Pisa

The truth is in the eye of the beholder. To a [teacher](https://learningspy.co.uk/featured/pisa-2015-tentative-thoughts-successful-schools) Pisa provides an instant conclusion *" [School works](https://theeconomyofmeaning.com/2018/01/10/this-new-meta-analysis-is-making-the-news-direct-instruction)!"*. To a person who understand the brain, it is also obvious: *"Pisa measures wrong things!"*. To [my eye](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Piotr_Wozniak "Piotr Wozniak"), Pisa says clearly: *"Schooling does not work!"*.

Of all the correlates, my biased eye will always fish for those that indicate creativity, and creative problem solving. As I know that freedom and rebellion are good for creativity, those aspects of schooling will also be important.

Who wins in Pisa? Those who cram a lot, spend a lot of time at school, show discipline, stick to the [curriculum](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Curriculum "Curriculum"), listen to the teacher, get extra compulsory science classes, follow direct instruction, start instruction in the [kindergarten](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Daycare "Daycare"), etc.

Like [Follow Through](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Follow_Through_\(project\)), Pisa might be helpful in optimization for the disadvantaged, helpful for honing basic skills, but it won't help advance higher cognitive function, critical thinking, or problem solving.

Pisa is a bit like studying the correlation between (1) reading a book and (2) knowing its contents. The outcome is obvious: readers know their books (as long as memory lasts). The trick for success is to employ the right book at the right time. How can we measure that book-selecting skill?

What parameters are losers for Pisa? Lax discipline (for me this includes freedom), truancy (i.e. craving for freedom), being late (e.g. due to getting good sleep), enquiry-based education, grade retention, etc. Being late for school was my trademark. I would never let school interfere with my sleep! Of good things, at least I agree with Pisa correlates that [homework](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homework "Homework") assignments are largely harmful.

Preferences fostered by Pisa seem to perfectly align against my own attitude towards school at the age of 15. I would contribute to dismal scores for Poland. The optimization would work to eliminate pests like myself.

Students who design their own experiments contribute poorly to Pisa score. Those who dutifully listen to the teacher do great. In real life, it is the independent tinkerer who will change the world 2-4 decades from now.

## Pisa reading test

Pisa reading test is an exception of sorts. Reading is beneficial for everyone, and a good reading test should be possible. Such a test is not a good optimization tool beyond helping those who read badly. Again [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") is the ultimate formula. All we need is to provide conditions for kids to learn to read. As of that point, passion for reading is all they need. The more they read, the better they get. Their knowledge will keep growing and so will reading skills. Schools are more likely to do harm. Few things can discourage reading more effectively than a compulsory reading list. This is exactly why [I do not read fiction](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Why_I_do_not_read_fiction "Why I do not read fiction") today. That passion was slaughtered for me at high school.

As for the reading test, I always used to score well. However, I know reading is domain specific too. I love all texts on neuroscience or artificial intelligence. However, I struggle with fiction. Compulsory reading lists at school made me develop a [fiction-reading-phobia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Toxic_memory "Toxic memory"). For [this book](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Problem_of_schooling "Problem of schooling"), I engaged in a bit of an investigation and was shocked to find 3 words I did not know on the first page of a book for kids! After decades of reading, and whole [Advanced English](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Advanced_English "Advanced English") memorized, this was quite a shock. No wonder my return to reading fiction might be hard. If the book was used as the basis of the test, I might trip. Does it mean I am any worse in my own field?

In case you die to know, the words I shamefully did not know were: *zeotrope, doubloon,* and *cutlass*. A well-read child might do better.

## The myth of (national) common core

Zakaria likes the concept of the [common core](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Common_core "Common core") and national standards. However, he failed to mention the fact that compulsory [curriculum](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Curriculum "Curriculum") leads to [coercive learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercive_learning "Coercive learning"), which annihilates [problem solving capacity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem). Schleicher responded with an indication that a "clear vision" is needed, and suggested that "different paths" may lead to the same goals. This means that [common core](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Common_core "Common core") can be an inspiration with no coercion involved.

Singapore is cited as a good example of high quality national standards. Singapore made recent news with topping Pisa tests. I took some of Pisa tests and see they really make sense. They measure a set of skills and knowledge we need to foster in kids. However, they do not measure everything. Topping Pisa is not the ultimate statement of superiority for a school system or a nation.

Singapore is great. Its students are great. However, Singapore comes short on creativity and problem solving. The workforce is less innovative.

There is a whole industry of private tutoring in Singapore. Parents spend inordinate amounts of money for [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming") and tutoring. I like the idea of one-on-one tutors. However, they need to be dosed in the right proportion. If the child's schedule is pre-programmed and jam-packed with tutoring with little room to maneuver, or choose, [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") will suffer. The [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") will suffer. The conformity will set in. Singapore should learn from Finland to pay less attention to the clock. Getting kids up early and running them on a perfectly timed schedule is bad for their physiology and bad for their brains.

One of the side effects is that Singapore is affected by the myopia epidemic. In Pisa-driven optimization, we would make that epidemic worse. Pisa does not factor in the time outdoors. Bad sight does not affect the scores much. Bad postures does not show on the test either. Bad health induced by [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming") often hits the population in their thirties or even later.

For that reason, great scores in Pisa may not translate to a remedy to Singapore's Nobel Prize winning drought. If Singapore wins a Nobel Prize, it will rather come from an American lab.

Ramasamy Sinnakaruppan of Singapore Education Academy writes in *" [Why Singapore’s education system needs an overhaul](https://www.todayonline.com/daily-focus/education/why-spores-education-system-needs-overhaul) "*:

> Students from Singapore and East Asian countries have consistently come out tops in the Programme for International Student Assessment (Pisa) and Trends in International Math and Science Study (Timss) rankings for decades, and yet companies from these countries barely feature in Forbes’ annual ranking of, say, the Top 25 most innovative companies in the world. Interestingly, the United States and European countries feature prominently in the latter ranking, but not the former

In the interview with Fareed Zakaria, Schleicher surprised me with a praise of his native Germany:

> You know, your school system today is your economy tomorrow. I think most countries have understood it. My own country, Germany, used to be where the United States was in the year 2000. It's now at the high end of the performance spectrum

If I was to bet on the future, it is the US that is likely to win the battle for better economy, not Germany. Both countries will get a lot from immigration and will struggle to accommodate that advantage. However, innovation is based on creativity and problem solving. This is why a German [ban on homeschooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Ban_on_homeschooling "Ban on homeschooling") is a throwback to the aftermath of the Weimar Republic. It is so bad that I branded Germany an [outcast](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/European_Outcasts "European Outcasts") (with apology to all my super-smart and super-nice German friends). On the other hand, homeschooling and unschooling in the US are exploding. Some of that is motivated by religious convictions, but the trend is clear and the impact on the freedom of learning will definitely give the US an advantage. This will not show up on the Pisa radar. By design, the execution of Pisa depends on the involvement of schools.

If US is dismal in the area of education, it is because of the dismal state of its schools. [Free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") is actually masking the extent of the problem.

In a nice graph, in his [TED talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch), Schleicher shows how Korea advanced and how it dramatically increased the number of high school graduates. What's great at the ascending part of the curve may become a curse later on (see: [Korean educational bankruptcy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Korean_educational_bankruptcy "Korean educational bankruptcy")). Once we reach 80-90% of literacy, we should rather cherish a growth in [homeschoolers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Homeschooling "Homeschooling") or [unschoolers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Unschooling "Unschooling"). We should then look for parameters that would show increase in creativity and problem solving. Schleicher agrees with that position and proposes Pisa as a better measurement. Pisa is definitely better than raw attendance or graduation numbers. But it got the same issue. All we need from 15-year-olds is to perform fine (i.e. just ok) on Pisa. We do not want to rush to fantastically high scores because that drive would be bound to stifle overall development.

Memorizing Quran or Bible verses will be penalized by Pisa, but so will free problem solving that takes away from basic [curriculum](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Curriculum "Curriculum").

## Voluntary participation in compulsory testing

Pisa is enormous in scope. It makes it fantastically interesting. Half a million students took part in 2015. This is a big army. Some kids did not take it seriously. This undermines the credibility, but still seems worth it.

Pisa testing costs some $250 million worldwide (my own estimate). Cost of Pisa is $25 million in the [US alone](https://www.educationnext.org/the-international-pisa-test). i.e. as much as it takes to educate 30 kids.

Half a million souls had to devote a day in life for the project. In all ethical research, all subjects should first sign a consent form. If students are tested against their will, this can bias the results. If they sign a consent form, this introduces another form of bias. The rebels would not sign. That would instantly filter the best and the worst in a hard to predict manner. Pisa is voluntary, but the [decisions are made at the school level](https://nces.ed.gov/surveys/pisa/faq.asp) and few things at school are voluntary:

> Each country/education system is responsible for recruiting sampled schools. They begin with the original sample and only use the replacement schools if an original school refuses to participate

It is instantly visible that the growing mass of homeschoolers and unschoolers will never register in the Pisa ranking tables. The US will always find it hard to compete with Germany where [homeschooling is banned](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Ban_on_homeschooling "Ban on homeschooling")! In Germany, all talent is forced to attend school.

> Although participation is [voluntary](https://nces.ed.gov/surveys/pisa/ifp_schools.asp), it is important that every selected school and student participate to ensure the completeness and accuracy of results. Each school and student selected represents many others; thus, the validity of the results depends on a high participation rate. Moreover, in order for schools to receive a school-level PISA report, a minimum of 85 percent of sampled students in the school must participate

Low participation would skew the sample by bad representation. High participation would skew the results but affecting the minds of the participants.

If my school told me to come for a test, I would probably stand in the leading ranks of quitters, unless someone managed to pique my curiosity or threaten with penalties. No wonder then that some students just [do not give a fig](https://www.odt.co.nz/news/national/non-serious-students-cost-nz-international-test-study).

> Unlike school sampling, there is no substitution of sampled students allowed. Schools inform students of their selection to participate. Student participation must be at least 80 percent for a country's education system's data to be reported by the OECD

There is one unique research value of Pisa that I love. According to Schleicher:

> PISA isn’t a high stakes test; students are aware that it’s not about them as individuals, but about painting a more general picture

Low stakes of Pisa for an individual are one of the most important aspect of the test. Most of the testing at school, for college admissions, or for a certificate are based on [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming") and short-term memory. Instead of providing a measure of student's abilities, it measures the ability to cram a great deal of data in a short period of time before an exam. This is the opposite of what we want to achieve. Long-term and [coherent](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") knowledge is the goal.

In addition to a smart design, a good test should also carry low stakes and be administered at unpredictable times. It should measure the actual status quo of knowledge and skills. If up to a third of students provide careless answers, the tests will also provide some clues on the degree of enthusiasm for knowledge and learning.

## Dangerous Pisa optimizations by example

Optimization by Pisa is dangerous. It can be easily illustrated by example. I will use Jobs and Wozniak.

Of well-know personalities who changed the world, Steve Job would be an interesting example. At 15, he would do poorly on a Pisa tests. If the population had more Jobses, it would reflect badly on a national score. The measure that would improve Jobs's performance might tighten up the discipline in learning subject such as biology. Some Pisa problem require some vestigial knowledge of biology, but even that is missing in most "normal" teens, incl. those gifted with creative genius. Tighter screw on biology would undercut Jobs's future creativity prospects. I know Jobs's knowledge of biology was dismal because he admitted that he never knew the term *pancreas* until he got a pancreatic cancer.

If Pisa was used to tighten a screw on [Wozniak (myself)](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Piotr_Wozniak "Piotr Wozniak"), my life might go into an entirely different direction.

I have always been envious of Jobs's sense of esthetics and art. His products were so nice that indeed one would want to "lick 'em". This has always been the weakest part of [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). At the age of 14, I excelled in chemistry, but was also making some progress as a budding painter. When high school started, my whole schedule was squeezed and I lost my passion for painting within a few months. My passions for chemistry and biology survived. If I was tested by Pisa at 15, I would do poorly with the exception of some narrow areas of science.

Domain-specificity would affect my scores. In estimating the size of a building, I recall that in youth I was happy when I finally managed to jump high enough to hit the ceiling. Since then I know the height of the single floor. That would help me solve one of Pisa test problems. What if I did not care? That jumping time came around my puberty which came a bit late for my hypothetical Pisa test.

More schooling for better Pisa score would undermine my passions further. I might have had less time for my own learning. My passions would evaporate entirely. Knowledge recycling would be as bad as this is the case in most teens. With fast recycling, I would never care about long-term memory. As a consequence, we might have no [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). It is a bit of a stretch, but there is some validity to the claim " [millions would have been affected](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Exponential_adoption_of_spaced_repetition "Exponential adoption of spaced repetition") " (for worse)

## Real problem solving

Pisa is an interesting research tool, however, we should invest more energy into giving kids some real problems to solve. How about a (voluntary) essay: *"How to build a better brain?"*. Lots of teens could provide some credible inspiration already today. Many would become infected with the concept of getting the best of the human brain, or surpassing the brain with the help of machines. One of those kids could change the world. Those who cram for tests at school will likely be deprived of the toolset needed for such a breakthrough.

We cannot easily test problem solving capacity and compare the essays or ideas or solutions. However, there is a clear and simple pathway in that direction. A pathway that needs no quantification. A pathway that optimizes on its own. That pathway is [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning").

If we free the kids, the innovation will happen! Formal instruction steals their youth and passions

## Schleicher, Waldorf, and free learning

Schleicher has some Waldorf school experience. With some limitations, Waldorf is steeped in many [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") ideas. It is a great philosophy except for inheriting some aspects of schooling and artificially delineated development stages. Schleicher should then have a good sense of the benefits of [unschooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Unschooling "Unschooling"). In [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning"), kids inevitably stay behind at first, and then they catch up, and then they soar. Slow start says little of the final outcome. For Pisa to be a valuable measure, it should be able to show the impact of Waldorf because the test is taken late enough (at 15). What if we could convert 3% of schools in a country to a Waldorf philosophy. Would that register on Pisa? What if we injected 1% of [democratic schools](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Democratic_school "Democratic school"). Sadly, I believe that the democratization would lead to a drop in scores. Democratic schooling and freedom are the true future of education, and Pisa would not detect it.

While freedom has a fantastic impact on creativity and problem solving, it may negatively affect domain-specific test scores

Schleicher in *"Five things I learned"* says *"The skills that are easiest to teach and test are also the skills that are easiest to digitize, automate and outsource"*. I agree. This is why I balk at optimizing schools with the use of benchmarks. Schleicher clearly knows the limitations of Pisa. Like [common core](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Common_core "Common core"), Pisa can be used for research and inspiration. I cannot be used in optimization. Most of all it should not be used in a pathological nationalist competition.

PISA tests are a great research tool. They should not be an optimization tool

Schleicher knows that *"better degrees do not translate to better lives"*. Instead of degrees, he would rather use Pisa for optimization measurements. Pisa is a great tool. One in many. Watching kids grow is another. [Free exploration](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") is best as it is bound to work wonders with or without quality control. When kids are free, we should rather focus on quantifying the conditions for good learning: access to knowledge, health, social environment, etc.

Interestingly, Schleicher noticed:

> In the **highest performing education systems, neither social background nor context makes much of a difference**

It seems that the negative impact of the social context and environment can be nullified by the system of schooling. A different picture might emerge if a larger proportion of kids were free to explore. We might then see what measure could help lift the weaker one's. We could see how investing in a good football field in a neighborhood affects the scores 10 years later. A football field has an obviously positive effect, however, we might perhaps quantify the effect of different competitive measures, perhaps a basketball field is better for fostering teamwork and peaceful conflict resolution? Or the other way around?

The possibility of a minimal impact of social background on Pisa scores is great news. Not all kids will have conditions to employ [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") successfully. This is why we can always take comfort in the fact that all those less fortunate can rely on the school systems without being left behind. At the same time, we must never forget that those at the top of the pyramid may be stifled by the obligations and pressure that are needed to fulfill the performance of a top Pisa scorer.

Schleicher is right that focusing on poorly performing students is beneficial, however, it also reflects the limits of Pisa. It does not measure well the progress at the top of the ladder.

Stratification of the American student population is a good testing ground to explore. Schleicher says:

> The American school system succeeds in preparing some students really well. But there are also many other students falling behind. And much of it has to do with the social context from which students come in which schools operate. And so that's basically the largest disparity in the outcomes

The problem of solving the issues of social context with schooling is multicriterial. [Standardized testing](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Standardized_testing "Standardized testing") does not quantify well the access to knowledge, health, social environment, family relationships, etc.

It is a bit as if we tried to optimize baby shelters. We would notice quickly that kids do worst if they arrive from abusive homes. However, optimization of baby shelters must always include the option of returning kids back to their families. Optimizing schools by Pisa is a bit as if we kept optimizing orphanages instead of focusing on the health of families.

We keep optimizing schools instead of focusing on optimizing the conditions for kids to employ [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning")

## Disconnect between work and learning

In terms of goals, I largely agree with Schleicher. His effort helped some countries make a first big step in the right direction. However, the countries at the top of Pisa ranking now need to refocus on free learning (shortened, emphasis is mine)([source](https://www.tes.com/news/more-young-people-will-be-radicalised-unless-schools-change-pisa-boss-warns)):

## PISA on direct instruction

It is interesting to see how the admirable effort by Schleicher is criticized from all directions. He is criticized by [me](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Piotr_Wozniak "Piotr Wozniak"): Pisa would hardly let [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") compete effectively with traditional schooling. Schleicher is also criticized by the worshippers of direct instruction (which usually means "teachers"). They ridicule the claim that Pisa is about creativity and problem solving. Not because those qualities are hard to measure. Direct instruction is supposedly necessary to make sure kids score well on tests, which are always domain-dependent. The claim is that knowledge is essential and trying to distill problem solving from knowledge is a futile and misguided effort.

The answer to that seeming two-directional criticism paradox comes from the computational understanding of human problem solving, esp. the concept of [mental computation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_to_solve_any_problem%3F "How to solve any problem). A good model makes it easy to see that knowledge is essential, and that its [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence") and applicability are essential too. Humans cannot live without a heart and a brain. Problem solving requires knowledge, creativity, and a problem solving ruleset. Heart of brain? Arguing the superiority of the essentials is fruitless. Direct instruction stifles problem solving capacity. [Standardized testing](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Standardized_testing "Standardized testing") is weak at capturing problem solving capacity. [Free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") is a verifiably perfect solution: it fosters problem solving, and its outcomes do not need to be measured or verified. The [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") is a great optimizer, and lack of success for a given intellectual should not be mourned any more than a lack of success of a particular business on a competitive market. Victims are unavoidable, and [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning") naturally minimizes human losses.

## Educators call for Pisa reform

I am not alone with my worries about PISA. A long list of educators expressed concern in an [open letter to Dr Schleicher](https://www.theguardian.com/education/2014/may/06/oecd-pisa-tests-damaging-education-academics):

> The new Pisa regime, with its continuous cycle of global testing, harms our children and impoverishes our classrooms, as it inevitably involves more and longer batteries of multiple-choice testing, more scripted "vendor"-made lessons, and less autonomy for teachers. In this way Pisa has further increased the already high stress level in schools, which endangers the wellbeing of students and teachers

> By emphasising a narrow range of measurable aspects of education, Pisa takes attention away from the less measurable or immeasurable educational objectives like physical, moral, civic and artistic development, thereby dangerously narrowing our collective imagination regarding what education is and ought to be about

Most importantly, the academics appreciate the good intent behind the project, and emphasize the threat to the [pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning"):

> We assume that OECD's Pisa experts are motivated by a sincere desire to improve education. But we fail to understand how your organization has become the global arbiter of the means and ends of education around the world. OECD's narrow focus on standardized testing risks turning learning into drudgery and ==killing the [joy of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning")==
