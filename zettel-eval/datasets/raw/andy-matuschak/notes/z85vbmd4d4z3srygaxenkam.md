The [[zkpv6qkserdrgqyryvgs2ws|Mnemonic medium]] helps readers remember what they’ve read, and that’s an important first step to understanding ([Deep understanding requires detailed knowledge of fundamentals](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zDxwRLcf5pn3crwNN2EqSYM)). We also use these prompts to help readers acquire conceptual knowledge: [Spaced repetition memory systems can be used to develop conceptual understanding](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/z9Vi7YVx7NzxU2wawNgsJbk). And we’ve gone beyond recall: [[zjowjeprvrjnm2zl3gdxbjg|The mnemonic medium can help readers apply what they’ve learned through simple application prompts]]. So. How might we continue up [Bloom’s taxonomy](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zFFTdRczpgGBy1P5QgeP635) to support more complex types of understanding? Related early experiments suggests that this may be possible: [Spaced repetition memory systems can be used to prompt application, synthesis, and creation](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/z4KxfCZPkVEf2R8nayLJZBG).

One angle would be to focus on [Retrieval practice and transfer learning](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zRTw8KaPErhSCyC2NYGHyP7) (e.g. [The mnemonic medium could include prompts which support near transfer](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zS5Zs5eThXhh4onMEYamBAY)).

A solution is likely to involve adding complexity to prompts over time, perhaps introducing synthesis-oriented prompts after readers already have firm recall. See [The mnemonic medium can be adapted to author an experience which unfolds over time](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zKRrv2DmKp3VyDEp3oXrKXQ), and [Idea-centric memory system](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/z7wCFe7MP9VeCVApcBLC7SN)
