## Bill Gates is an avid reader

Bill Gates is my hero. He is also a genius. He reads a great deal of high quality books. He does not use [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"). Let's see if there is any evidence that he is missing out on the great technology.

## Bill Gates's reading algorithm

Gates's book reading algorithm is simple, conservative, and effective:

- Gates picks a great book that paints coherent models of a fraction of reality that Bill wants to understand. For a genius, making good choices on the basis of reviews is relatively easy. His choices are strong
- Gates reads the book in the [protected mode](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Protected_mode "Protected mode") that lasts no less than an hour, e.g. during the [Think week](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Think_week "Think week") or within a peaceful reading slot. Bill does not often read at the best [circadian phase](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_phase "Circadian phase"). What he gains on social creativity when discussing the world with people at his prime phase, he may lose on a more contemplative and creative aspect of reading
- Gates make notes on the margins to maximize engagement and [creative elaboration](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creative_elaboration "Creative elaboration"); this is fantastic for building models, but these are not [Zettelkasten](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Zettelkasten "Zettelkasten"). Those notes help him arm his memory. However, without [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition"), the risk of [forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") is high. Bill relies on core models of high [applicability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicability "Applicability"). Then he adds layers of knowledge structured in the [working memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Working_memory "Working memory") of his genius mind. However, without the fodder of [diverse](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Diversity "Diversity") factual knowledge characterized by high [retrievability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retrievability "Retrievability") and low [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), his creativity will be hobbled (see: [Knowledge and creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_and_creativity "Knowledge and creativity"))
- Gates reads books to the end. This is why making a good choice is essential. He immerses himself deeply in the concepts, builds models, and restructures his knowledge of the world. This is fantastically valuable

## Reservations

Here are some of my reservations to Bill's algorithm. I realize that a genius mind can largely compensate for many shortcomings of classic linear reading on paper, however, some of the shortcomings might put Bill at some disadvantage.

### Linear progression

Bill believes in the power of schools (see: [Bill Gates is wrong about education](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bill_Gates_is_wrong_about_education "Bill Gates is wrong about education")). He does not seem to be bothered by the shackles of linear reading of a book. Once he makes great picks, he does not mind being lead by the hand of the author. For a while he feels like he attended the best school available in a given topic. However, dendritic progression in reading, and interleaving are unrivalled in [creative elaboration](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creative_elaboration "Creative elaboration"). With a good book, Bill remains creative in the area directed by the author. He stays within predictive rails of the book's reasoning. See: [Books don't work](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Books_don%27t_work "Books don't work")

### Insufficient surprise

The worst part of linear book reading is the lack of stochastic association of remote ideas. Bill is likely to recreate valuable models depicted by the author (i.e. available to others before reading), or elaborate creatively on the content. He is less likely to hit on a breakthrough idea that will change the world. The only substitute for this missing component is his own long-term memory, which is notoriously slow to activate in creative contexts. The only comfort for Bill is that the memory of a genius is so fantastically [applicable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicable "Applicable") that he is unlikely to miss an association that confronts his core models with a concept or concepts activated in his [working memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Working_memory "Working memory") while reading.

### Cost of completion

Bill reads books to the end. Errors in book selection can be costly. He does not throw away a book in the midst of reading. He wants to complete structuring of [models](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Model "Model") depicted by the author. For this, he can pay dearly in time (unless he engages in skimming, which would make good sense). In book reading with a goal of completion, there is no option for incremental revaluation, and for the possibility of changing one's mind on the actual priority of the entire text while reading. I used to experience the same problem with movie watching. Ever since I decided to learn spoken English (around 1986-1987), I made a detailed record of movies watched. Originally, I could only afford a movie a week in the cinema. My first movie was *"Raw Deal"* on Feb 10, 1988. I hardly understood a phrase without reading subtitles (despite [12 years of English at school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schools_are_useless_in_teaching_English "Schools are useless in teaching English")!). At first, movie record keeping was easy. But the abundance of movies kept increasing. First we had cable TV in Poland (for me, 1991), and then also satellite TV. Soon the abundance of movies outgrew my processing power, and then it outgrew my appetites. The habit of recording movies was hard to break. Selection was more important. But I still kept my list of movies watched. As a rule, completing a movie was necessary to put in on the list. A mistake in the selection would not be costly. A movie can be deleted. But once I invested 20-30 minutes into a movie, I showed some kind of loss aversion and plodded to the end. Luckily, I gave up the habit, and now watch movies only occasionally and incrementally during meals. Unless Bill Gates makes a nearly perfect selection or engages in a lot of skimming, the rule of completing books can be very costly. In [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), [priorities](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Priority_queue "Priority queue") change in the course of reading, and may change further in re-reading. Blocks can be skipped for later reading, and receive a priority that depends on the blocks that follow. The range of reading materials is hundreds times wider than the one Bill Gates can afford with paper books. The [learntropy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learntropy "Learntropy") of [increading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Increading "Increading") is out of this world!

### Cost of attention

Bill says *"You need to be careful that you are concentrating"*! That's very wasteful. Creative thinking may be more valuable than reading itself. The mind should wander and depart each time a new idea arrives. In book reading, this might lead to wild disruption that would slow the reading rate. In a sense, book reading is a good metaphor for schooling. Attention is important and creativity may need to be suppressed. Linear reading is actually one of the bad school habits on my list (see: [50 bad habits learned at school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/50_bad_habits_learned_at_school "50 bad habits learned at school")). Genius mind can compensate, but only so much.

Bill needs to focus, and this has a cost. In [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), uncontrollable creativity can be harnessed with a switch to the next reading position. When I read Kurzweil or Wolfram, I let my mind go wild. If this goes too far, I can always switch to [Elizabeth Bartholet](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Elizabeth_Bartholet "Elizabeth Bartholet") 's longwinded legalese. The best [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") -inducing articles can be read again tomorrow or later in the same day. Even a minute-long switch to other texts can be helpful when [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") needs to be reined in.

### Overhead costs

Bill needs at least an hour of [protected mode](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Protected_mode "Protected mode") free from distractions to ensure full immersion and attention. There is some cost to context recovery when reading a book. The mechanical aspects of book reading are costly too. You need to physically grab the book, grab the pen, and locate your last reading point (bookmark). It does not make much sense to spend a minute on reading a book on paper. In [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), the overhead costs are minimum. One sentence of a low priority text may fill any spare minute of time. It may actually fill in just a few seconds. In low brain time, a few low [priority](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Priority "Priority") [items](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item") can also mark progress. There are always materials available for a distracted brain, low mood brain, sleepy brain, angry brain, etc. I keep [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") open all the time, and I switch to using it in a way that is similar to how the brain switches between the creative and attentive modes. I may be prompted by any idea worth investigating, or I may happen to have a spare minute while downloading a file. Working in bursts is not as effective as working in [protected mode](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Protected_mode "Protected mode"), however, it adds some extra productivity and joy to the day. If I happen to get a link to read while being busy with e-mail, I can quickly schedule the reading, or grab a few sentences for instant comments. This seemingly chaotic mode of work adds a great deal of peace of mind. Having found a good thing to read, I never need to disrupt the activity I am busy with. This way, there is no need to ban YouTube, or web browsing. Whatever my brain cannot consume, I delegate to [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo").

### Fatigue

Bill claims to be able to read for 18 hours straight. I doubt this is efficient. The creative mind wanes after 2-4 hours. More reading is possible with improved attention and worsened consolidation. When I sense the loss of the [pleasure of reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning"), I stop reading. The brain gives me a healthy signal that I consumed enough, and rambled around for long enough. Bill pulls out a can of coke and plods along. One of his fears is Alzheimer's disease. His dad is affected. Geniuses are at lower risk, but 18 hours of reading, exercising little, dining at McDonald's, or sleeping under the desk are not the habits one should copy of Gates as the role model. By fueling his brain for long hours with coke, Bill is contributing to his risks (see: [How schools can contribute to Alzheimer's disease](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/How_schools_can_contribute_to_Alzheimer%27s_disease "How schools can contribute to Alzheimer's disease")). A better strategy is to switch to other activities, exercise, or take a nap. It is possible to do more in a shorter time as long as the brain power is taken care of.

### Cost of impatience

When Bill disagrees with the author, his rate of note-taking increases. Disagreements with the rest of the world are a frequent occurrence in the mind of a genius. When Bill disagrees a lot, he may exclaim in his mind: *"Please write something I agree with"*. His impatience comes from the fact that a disagreeable book may take for ever to read. In [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), the only healthy strategy is to switch to another text. If disagreement boosts creativity, reading may be hard. However, when impatience suppresses both attention and creativity, further reading, at least on a given day, makes little sense. Switching is the right weapon, which minimizes the costs of [futile persistence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Futile_grit "Futile grit"). Impatience entails a loss to [learntropy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learntropy "Learntropy"). It is a sign of displeasure, which is the brain's best signal of a drop in efficiency. [Pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning") must be the ultimate guide.

### Value of bad ideas

There is [value to wrong models](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Value_of_wrong_models "Value of wrong models"). Wrong models can inspire. Wrong models help us understand other people's thinking. Incoherent models described in incoherent language provide an insight into other people's minds. I consider perfect reading about perfect models a remnant of bad school habits (see: [50 bad habits learned at school](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/50_bad_habits_learned_at_school "50 bad habits learned at school")). Reading bad ideas helps one gauge the gap between models and culture. Perfect reading as employed by Bill Gates may be too sterile to provide those extra values. I have no idea how much time Bill Gates spends on internet forums, or if he ever visits Facebook. Perhaps he uses messy time for reading messy messages? I believe that integrating messy ideas into perfect reading is important. I try to read a lot about how people perceive [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"). This is important for further progress of technology and its perception. The degree of misunderstanding about the effective ways of reading or learning is astronomical. If I relegated imperfect reading to low brain time (in late [circadian phase](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_phase "Circadian phase")), I might never truly grasp all insights. It would be easy to dismiss erroneous thinking as inconsequential, while minor underlying errors may lead to major errors in models and theory. Finding those basic inconsistencies is essential for the ultimate diagnosis, and for shaping the message that can be better understood.

### Narrow knowledge space

Books do not provide a personal creative knowledge space. Great minds crave memory extension. Notes on a margin are a very poor substitute. They are not searchable unless made on Kindle, etc. (Bill likes to read on paper). [Zettelkasten](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Zettelkasten "Zettelkasten") are an example of a personal indexed inter-connected knowledge space. Incremental reading is the extension of the idea (see: [Inevitability of incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Inevitability_of_incremental_reading "Inevitability of incremental reading")). Bill cannot use [search and review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Subset_review "Subset review"), let alone [neural creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neural_creativity "Neural creativity"). Paper is useless for those purposes. Bill needs to rely on his memory, or on Google. He cannot effectively customize his personal creative knowledge space. There is no bridge between his own memory and Google. A [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") [collection](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Collection "Collection") can be considered a personalized cream of the crop of the web.

## Conclusions

If you look at the list of [advantages of incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Advantages_of_incremental_reading "Advantages of incremental reading"), Bill loses on nearly all fronts. His genius mind may not be able to compensate for the loss. His best chances for solid compensation come in the areas of [consistency](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Consistency "Consistency"), [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence"), stresslessness, attention, [prioritization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Priority_queue "Priority queue"), speed, meticulousness, and some metacognitive skills (e.g. operating on semantics, speedreading), etc.

The most painful differences may show up in [knowledge retention](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_retention "Knowledge retention"), and in the dendritic nature of the growth of knowledge. In addition, I suspect that creativity would suffer too. I would need to live in Bill's brain to be sure, but I posit that even a genius needs to constrain rampant creativity to make book reading efficient. Depending on the degree of that limitation, this might be the biggest shortcoming of them all. With its set of constraints, book reading may come uncomfortably close to [schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schooling "Schooling"). The reader is guided by the hand of the author. It may still meet all the necessary conditions of [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning"), but there must be a drop in the efficiency of the process that is invariant of the reader's intellectual standing.

There is no doubt that a genius mind gets to great books with great joy. Fun is the ultimate litmus test. Bill may lose out on many fields, but he will also compensate on many. The most important component of his successful reading is genius itself. Not everyone can copy his algorithm, but mastering efficient incremental reading isn't easy either, perhaps even harder.

I have no good evidence to show that Bill loses out on reading non-incrementally to a significant degree. His book bag contains a great deal of positions I would never finish reading. They would just not bubble up high on the priority list. [Incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") seems just so much richer than any single book.

That proves nothing: you can always add to [learntropy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learntropy "Learntropy") by good structure and context. However, the crowning argument for me is that if I lost [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") today, I would probably fall into clinical depression. My attachment and love for the concept is so deep, that no amount of best books in the world could compensate. Let me and Bill agree to differ. [Reading books is dead for me](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Why_I_do_not_read_books "Why I do not read books").
