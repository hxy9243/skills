## Renaissance of free learning

Are digital devices a threat to our sanity?

For decades, we dreamt of great education that would lift societies to a new level. For decades we saw knowledge as a panacea for nearly all human problems, incl. death and perhaps even taxes. Today, the era of golden learning has arrived. We have developed many tools needed for the brain to capitalize on its maximum potential. It will only get better. Today, only the brain biology sets the limits.

The availability of human knowledge, access to knowledge resources, cost of education, and learning methodology have all changed beyond our wildest imagination in just three short decades. It takes my breath away to think how much we take today's world for granted. These days, a 4-year-old can Google to find out [how giraffes sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/File:How_do_giraffes_sleep.png "File:How do giraffes sleep.png") (see: [Papert's take on letteracy](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Letteracy "Letteracy")). Today's Google generation can change this world beyond recognition, and help eliminate many ails that afflict populations in all corners of the planet.

Digital devices and Google are a blessing for education

## Harmful psychiatrist

Here, in the times of the expected educational nirvana, comes Dr Manfred Spitzer. Dear doctor says: *children should not learn to access Google before they are 18*. When Nicholas Carr says *" [Google makes you stupid](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Freedom_of_education "Freedom of education") "*, we can dismiss him as a journalist who knows little of neuroscience (see: [Proof: Internet makes us smarter](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Proof:_Internet_makes_us_smarter "Proof: Internet makes us smarter")). If Yuval Harari says *[We have been hacked by algorithms](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Myth:_Our_brains_can_get_hacked_by_algorithms "Myth: Our brains can get hacked by algorithms")*, we can dismiss him as a [historian who struggles to understand the brain](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Yuval_Harari_does_not_understand_the_brain "Yuval Harari does not understand the brain"). However, Spitzer is a neuropsychiatrist, with a pretty good understanding of the brain and hands-on experience with people affected by the new digital media. While, Carr or Harari spark heated discussions, Spitzer has actually done some tangible damage already. His book *" [Digital Dementia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Digital_Dementia "Digital Dementia") "* has become pretty popular in a couple of countries, and is playing on sensitive concerns of anxious parents. I did not learn about Spitzer because of his stellar research. It is the damage that he has done in my circle of friend families that has actually brought his book to my attention. There is a phenomenon that seems to becoming an epidemic: parents confiscate digital devices to coerce children into learning for school. Spitzer himself opposes [coercion in learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion_in_learning "Coercion in learning"), however, his prescription can only ramp up the problem. A healthy child's brain guided by the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") can see a world of difference between the new media, and the excruciatingly boring time at school driven by [asemantic curriculum](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_curriculum "Asemantic curriculum"). This magnifies the sense of the loss of control, which Spitzer himself decries in his writings. That helplessness is the exact toxic force that drives kids into addictions, incl. [gaming disorder](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Gaming_disorder "Gaming disorder"). Inadvertently, Spitzer will make life more of a hell for many kids.

For clarity, Spitzer sports extensive knowledge of neuroscience. I should praise Spitzer for his criticism of German schools, and his good understanding of many harmful aspects of schooling: [coercion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercion "Coercion"), [learned helplessness](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learned_helplessness "Learned helplessness"), [extrinsic motivation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Extrinsic_motivation "Extrinsic motivation"), [reward system](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/School_drive "School drive"), lack of holistic learning, stress, insufficient own [knowledge valuation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_valuation_network "Knowledge valuation network"), etc. However, all his good deeds can easily be undone by his book. Instead of making parents ponder freedom and child's rights, Spitzer will drive more kids into coercive schooling and away from the access to the rich orchard of digital media. Paradoxically, it could lead to more kids in stress, in addiction, and at war with their parents or their school.

## Ignorance that leads astray

Spitzer is pretty vocal and combative in the media. He might be a Boris Johnson of the psychology of learning. He combines the qualities of an erudite, demagogue, scientist, and an ignoramus. If I was to point to key weaknesses in Spitzer's reasoning, and key errors in his knowledge of neuroscience these would be his comments on attention, multitasking, cognitive impact of gaming, long-term memory, learning, and the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"). I briefly explain all points below.

In addition to errors in neuroscientific thinking, Spitzer does not seem to understand gaming, and even promotes a few misconceptions about the art of googling. Why would he even mention 10,000 hits at Google as if reading its top search result was in any way difficult or discouraged? A child can use Cortana and [ask questions about giraffes](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Letteracy "Letteracy"). How can we possibly criticize the best digital teacher on the planet? Book profits notwithstanding, I hope Manfred's motivations are sincere, because some of his claims defy sincere belief.

I am not a gamer myself, but I did spend a few nights gaming in 1986. I do understand the addictive power of computer games. Spitzer's reasoning makes him look like a guy who never held a joystick in his hand. I never held a steering wheel in my hand, and this is why I am cautious when speaking about driving. I know dozens of young gamers and they all help me understand their mind and their motivations. They square nicely with what I know about the brain. Spitzer meets pathological cases of game addiction in person, and this might result in his [sampling bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sampling_bias). I admit, I have not spoken to a true addict, and the reason is simple: I did not meet any. For every case of a serious addiction, there are hundreds or thousands of healthy young men who follow their natural instincts into the world of gaming. There are always side effects, and kids are usually aware of those. There are side effects of exercise too. I do not mind being "addicted".

## Focus and attention

When Spitzer speaks of a gamer who can expect threats appear in different corners of his visual field, he considers that a situation that undermines focus. The opposite is true. A healthy gamer succeeds because of his utter focus on the game. This is actually one of the reasons for criticism of gaming: a gamer will not see the world beyond the game. He may even neglect his physiological needs. Spitzer seems to take "focus" literally. As if anything beyond "focusing on a point" in the visual field was a case of distraction. In reality, attentional control is one of the prime cognitive beneficiaries of gaming.

Focus and creativity stand in opposition. Gaming is an excellent training tool for improving attentional skills. Depending on the type of the game, creativity can benefit too.

## Multitasking

Spitzer makes the same error as Nicholas Carr to whom Spitzer often refers to in his lectures. They both confuse voluntary attentional control ("multitasking"), and involuntary multitasking induced by disruption. Switching focus in attentional control, e.g. [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), multi-tab browser, or [incremental writing](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_writing "Incremental writing"), is a boost to memory, creativity, problem solving, and more. On the other hand, multitasking induced by disruption is a source of [acute stress](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Acute_stress "Acute stress") and working memory overload.

What is worse, in his presentations, Spitzer provides a misleading example: *"you cannot listen to two lectures at the same time"*. This confuses multitasking with divided attention. I often listen to two lectures in parallel in [incremental video](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_video "Incremental video"). This has nothing to do with simultaneous listening, and requires no special skills.

Modern digital world is a good teacher in the area of healthy multitasking. Most of people may suffer from the effects of disruption, however, digital media exposes the problem. As a result, the suffering is obvious, cost is obvious, and the digital media only make the problem patently salient. This is how many people with good self-control quickly arrive at their own solutions. Digital media is a disulfiram of digital addiction. It exposes the addiction, makes it painful, and facilitates the search for remedies. The proliferation of anti-advertising tools is just an example of the adaptation that resulted from the abuse of disruption.

Digital media is the antabuse of multitasking. The pain of disruption makes the need for remedies obvious

## Cognitive impact of gaming

When asked about the cognitive benefits of gaming as evidenced by rich research around the globe, Spitzer asked for specific authors and their conflict of interest (as if he did not know any research that would not raise suspicion). Healthy skepticism is precious, however, with a deluge of evidence, it can turn into a marker of ignorance or ill will. Explicitly, Spitzer insisted that gaming does not improve planning, cognitive control, executive function, hand-eye coordination, fine motor skills, and more. As an unskilled gamer with some basic knowledge of the brain, I do not need research. Benefits are obvious. I do not study the science of walking to know that each step has an influence on honing motor skills involved in walking.

## Memory

I eat memory research for breakfast. Dr Spitzer's claims about memory have nothing to do with science.

Spitzer's statements on the impact of Google on short-term memory, could be paraphrased as *"cars cause muscle atrophy",* *"books cause speech atrophy", "calculators cause a decline in slider rule skills"*, and *"smartphones cause a decline in phone number memorization skills"*. All these claims are true and uninteresting.

A popular claim is that we outsource our memory capacity to digital devices as if having a larger non-associative storage was a straight way to handicapping the living brain tissue. The opposite is true. We no longer need to worry about memorizing trivia and have more room for vital knowledge that leads to abstract rules employable in problem solving. This provides the sense of empowerment, which is highly motivational. This in turn provides better learning, higher quality of memories, better problem solving, and higher individual and collective intelligence. While typing those words in a hurry in [guru wiki](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_Guru "SuperMemo Guru"), I do not need to pause over the time-wasting irregularities of English spelling. As the word *"huyrry"* showed up in the previous sentence, it was flagged and corrected. My sentence was published a second faster, and with a lesser chance for a typo confusion. My memory benefit is obvious: the attentional processor is focused on writing about "digital dementia" instead of the spelling of the word "hurry".

Spitzer confuses outsourcing trivia to outsourcing knowledge in general. This presumes low intelligence on the part of the user of the media. Spitzer says: *You cannot improve your chances to learn Chinese by outsourcing your knowledge of English*. This comparison might have had some sense if there had ever been an individual dumb enough to attempt anything remotely similar.

To say that Google discourages memory training is as valid as a claim that bicycles discourage jogging. Both have some uninteresting validity, and both have negligible impact on the valuation of the tools. What we commit to memory is a matter of a conscious decision (esp. in [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition")). Google is just a tool to access knowledge. In terms of long term memory it may be comparable to book reading. Those who do not know or do not use [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition"), use reading as a crutch. Hitting the same terms and problems over and over again, statistically increases the chances of establishing a long term memory. This is how we have been learning for millennia. The only difference is that now we can do it better. God Bless Google.

Poor hygiene of cognitive effort can contribute to Alzheimer's (see: [Bad learning contributes to Alzheimer's](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Bad_learning_contributes_to_Alzheimer%27s "Bad learning contributes to Alzheimer's")). This is as true of digital media as it is of decades of cramming at school. However, hijacking the term "dementia" is pretty unfair for true sufferers. I would never dare use the term of "school dementia" because the effects of bad schooling may take decades and be minor for a majority of population. I have no doubt that in sum, schools do far more damage to children today that computer games. In psychiatric terms, Spitzer would agree that the key defense of gaming is that it is voluntary. Schooling is a form of modern slavery.

In the late 1980s, I was in love with [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). I saw it as a tool that would change the world. In those days, digging out a golden nugget of knowledge, e.g. about the brain, was hard and expensive. [Forgetting](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Forgetting "Forgetting") the nugget felt like irreparable loss. Storing the nuggets in SuperMemo was my insurance that no value would be lost beyond my natural limits on storing things in long-term memory. Today, Google may play a similar role. Golden nuggets of wisdom can be lost, but at least they can be found at a fraction of the cost as compared with the 1980s. This does not diminish the value of long term memory, however, it is better to walk on a crutch than not to walk at all

## Learning

Spitzer believes that children learn faster than adults, and if you do not fill up your brain with wisdom by 17, you never will. I disagree (see: [Children have an awful memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Precocity_paradox:_early_instruction_may_hurt_the_long-term_growth "Precocity paradox: early instruction may hurt the long-term growth")). Spitzer's belief in adult limitations only amplifies his claims of educational urgency. He insists that we need to take away digital devices to prevent irreparable harm. In reality, the effects of deprivation on the critical periods of cortical development are truly harmful only if a child's environment drastically differs from the environments, in which the child will function as an adult. Growing a child in a city can be deadly if the child is to live in the slums of Monrovia or in the Amazonian rainforest. Growing a child exclusively in a 2D world could be detrimental for its functioning in the 3D world. Growing a child totally dedicated to [schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schooling "Schooling") is detrimental to the life of an entrepreneur, or an artist. To a degree, [schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schooling "Schooling") handicaps kids in preparation to virtually any adult environment, including that of schools themselves (student and teacher [behavioral spaces](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Behavioral_space "Behavioral space") differ drastically). Loss of self-dependence, self-control, and the loss of the joy of learning are indeed on the list of [10 mortal sins of schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/10_mortal_sins_of_schooling "10 mortal sins of schooling"). At the same time, brain's ability to adapt may make incomplete deprivation hard to notice. Complete ocular deprivation has a devastating impact on cortical wiring. 90% deprivation may have little effect. The saturative nature of neural training leads to getting most of the benefit at the very earliest stages of the process.

Spitzer advocates for pencils instead of keyboards as if pencils were cognitively superior, or as if the command of pencils was more important than the command of keyboards in adulthood. He might equally well advocate a return to clay tablets for the cognitive benefit of sculpting cuneiform.

Pencil deprivation in childhood may become a serious handicap only for those who use pencils for a living. Touchpad deprivation will be comparably detrimental for a digital designer. The effect might be tangible only if the deprivation took place during the critical periods of motor cortex wiring in toddlerhood. Transfer of motor skills will reduce the effect even further. The rule of the thumb is to let the kid enjoy her life the best she can.

If deprivation leads to handicaps, deprivation of digital media will handicap a start to adult life in the digital world

## Neuroplasticity

Memory, learning, and neuroplasticity are interlinked. Speaking of one in abstraction of another may only have rhetorical value. Spitzer believes that digital technology stunts neuroplasticity and that effect is hard to recover with education in later years.

In reality, plasticity is a simple reflection of learning. We know that learning is maximized when it is guided by the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"). If a child choses a computer game over a book, it is not because he is dumb or intellectually lazy. The choice is an indicator of cognitive benefits that directly translate to plasticity. Coercive withdrawal of potential rewards of digital technology will have the exact opposite effect. In addition, it will involve stress which Spitzer denounces himself as the killer of brain cells.

## Learn drive

[Learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), popularly known as curiosity, is a wildly misunderstood instinct. To this day, very few scientists and educators understand the power and reliability of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") system that determines the [pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning"). No control system in a living body is perfect. However, along the natural adaptability of neural networks and the brain, [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") is one of the least appreciated powers of the human mind. The whole concept of [passive schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Passive_schooling "Passive schooling") is based on that underestimate.

There is only one good judge of what knowledge is acceptable for a child's brain: the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"). This is why, when a child says: *"I learn more from the game than from my teachers"* (see: [Videogames are better than teachers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Videogames_are_better_than_teachers "Videogames are better than teachers")), we must listen and trust. It is a matter of a basic human right: self-determination in learning.

The accuracy of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") should give children priority in educational choices

Kids are naturally drawn to [videogames](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Videogames "Videogames"), while parents are convinced that school is more important. The kid it told that he cannot argue with the words of a professor. That formula for an ideavirus is very potent. The book is a very useful weapon for a parent.

In his visit to Poland, Spitzer was branded as *"world famous psychologist"* and *"expert of modern education"*. ## Conclusion

Digital media is a blessing for humanity. Spitzer insists that parents should discourage the use of Google until the age of 18. That would naturally increase the interest in print media. In 1500, an educator might discourage books to promote the use of speech and conversation. After all, conversation is social, while books are solo and egoistic. Worst of all, with print, like with Google, we outsource our long-term memory.

Parents have a right to exercise free speech, and to attempt to indoctrinate their kids about the opportunity costs of gaming. As long as they do it by extolling exercise, trekking, books, music, or socializing, they are not encroaching on the child's rights. However, once the tablet or the phone ends up in a locked drawer, once the [coercive learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coercive_learning "Coercive learning") and [schooling](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Schooling "Schooling") receive a priority, the toxic spiral of conflict and addiction begins.

[Internet makes us smarter](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Internet_makes_us_smarter "Internet makes us smarter"), [videogames are better than teachers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Videogames_are_better_than_teachers "Videogames are better than teachers"), the new digital world brings a great deal of unmeasured utility to mankind.

Headline grabbing claims such as Carr's *"Google makes you stupid"* or Harari's *"Brains can be hacked by algorithms"* or Spitzer's *"Electronic devices make us demented"* are born from intellectual arrogance. Fear-mongering authors believe that millions of ill-prepared consumers are dumb enough to fall victim of misuse of the new technology. Many will indeed, however, the source of the trouble is elsewhere. For example, it is harder to resist social media when people experience the loss of control over their own lives (e.g. due to schooling, rat race, poor corporate ethics, etc.). Loss of freedom can derail the best brain.

The world of the new media is breaming with opportunities. Good employment of technology is a matter of adaptation. Brains evolved for precisely that purpose. All we need is the freedom to explore. It is also helpful to be free from scaremongering and wild mythology, esp. when promoted by neuroscientists.

Free choice in tools of learning and in access to knowledge are a basic child's right
