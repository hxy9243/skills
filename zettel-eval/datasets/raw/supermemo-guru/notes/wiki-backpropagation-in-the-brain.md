## How the brain works

Geoff Hinton spent years looking for backpropagation — the learning algorithm behind modern AI — in the brain. He didn’t find it. But what if he was simply using the wrong map?

The brain doesn’t work like a tidy stack of layers sending errors backward to fine-tune weights. Instead, it works more like a living [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") — a web of ideas that grow, connect, and compete. [Concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept") (nodes) predict outcomes, not just sensory inputs. When predictions go wrong, the brain updates its connections — sometimes subtly, sometimes by growing new pathways. Think: structural plasticity, not just weight adjustment.

Learning in the brain is shaped by rewards and penalties — derived from meaning and used to guide motivation. It’s still math, just implemented through biology. When concepts fire together, they wire together. When they go unused, they fade away (*use it or lose it*). Error signals don’t come only from mismatched perception, but also from internal contradictions, failed predictions, or emotional outcomes.

The real engine of deep learning is the reward system. Strong emotions — joy, failure, insight — send powerful signals that drive widespread change. **Dendrites act as curious seekers**, reaching out for meaning. **Axons broadcast messages** that matter. Both grow and weaken based on what’s useful or rewarding. And during sleep, the network reorganizes, amplifies, rewires, and stabilizes.

So, does the brain use backpropagation? Not quite. But it achieves something even more remarkable: a flexible, goal-driven, emotion-sensitive network that evolves with experience.

We don’t need to find backprop in the brain. We need to see the brain on its own terms — and update our terminology so that AI and neuroscience can finally meet in the middle.

## Mechanism

In backpropagation, the input pattern is trained to be associated with the correct output, as in supervised learning (the functional equivalent of [declarative learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Declarative_learning "Declarative learning") in humans).

In such contexts, the brain primarily uses self-supervised learning. While interacting with the environment, it can actively select patterns it wants to associate (e.g., word pairs in a foreign language). These patterns activate [concept cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_cell "Concept cell") or neuronal assemblies (analogous to features in ANNs). The signals are matched in universal early hubs, such as the hippocampus for episodic memories, and in highly connected areas of the association cortex.

Gradually, during sleep, optimal neocortical wiring is established to connect the input and output patterns (see: [Memory optimization in sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Memory_optimization_in_sleep "Memory optimization in sleep")). The association may initially be diffuse, but it becomes [generalized](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") over time in subsequent epochs of learning and sleep. Ideally, through efficient [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization"), one-to-one cell wiring can be achieved in the long run. In such a case, the [neuron responding best to your grandmother](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") co-activates with the neuron responding best to the sound of her name (along with other neurons in the same concept group).

For an example scenario, see how we [[wiki-the-truth-about-grandmother-cells|learn about zebras, and how we forget zebras]].

## Conclusions

We can use the above outline to make [concept networks](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") grow on a digital medium. Instead of physical wiring, weight updates will do just fine. There is no wiring architecture problem. The key metaphor speaks of "curious dendrites" and "loud axons". Their actions guided by the use and rewards determine the wiring in ways well more efficient than backpropagation. Learning is slow, dendritic growth is slow, but [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability"), [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization"), and ultimate [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence") are remarkable!
