## Introduction

[Incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") makes it possible to dramatically increase the speed of reading, while increasing comprehension, [consistency](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Consistency "Consistency"), [applicability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicability "Applicability") and [retention](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retention "Retention") of knowledge (see: [Advantages of incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Advantages_of_incremental_reading "Advantages of incremental reading")). [Incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") evolved as an extension of the [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") method pioneered by [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). It is a fusion of reading and memory techniques that have been largely invented independently before. This text presents the main milestones as seen from the perspective of [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). For a wider historic context see: [Inevitability of incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Inevitability_of_incremental_reading "Inevitability of incremental reading")

## Childhood

In my childhood I shaped some understanding of the basic building blocks of efficient learning:

I can find traces of my appetite for [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") already in my childhood. Around 1972 (aged 10), I liked reading an encyclopedia and follow the "hyper"-links in the text. This allowed for infinite reading pathways guided by interest. Around that time, I also got some kind of encyclopedia of horses. I loved horse riding. I would follow the links in the book and make my own notes on paper. It was a bit like [incremental writing](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_writing "Incremental writing") about horses (more reproductive than creative). It combined learning with making notes that could be reviewed later. I used similar methods with an encyclopedia of mammals, and possibly also birds, fish, reptiles, and amphibians. Having won a chemistry contest in our province (1976, aged 14), I became interested in biochemistry and registered for a biology contest for high schools. Inspired by great biology books such as those by Claude A. Villee, I wanted to write my own that would fit my interests. I probably sensed that I will know biology better if I write it in my own way? Hunger for knowledge, and [passion for biology](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Passion_and_memory "Passion and memory") shaped my initial ideas of building blocks needed to explore and remember knowledge

## Progressive reading

As a student of biology (1980-1985), I used my own *"cross-the-notebook"* method of [cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cramming "Cramming") (see: [Serendipitous impact of mindless cramming](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Serendipitous_impact_of_mindless_cramming "Serendipitous impact of mindless cramming")). I would go through my paper notes and cross out those that I was sure I would remember for the exam. I would make multiple passes through my notes until all details were memorized to perfection. Some remnants might be incomprehensible or unmemorizable, however, each pass would take less and less time as there would be less and less to read. The notes would become useless, but the exam could easily be aced (given good notes). All my past school notes were covered with crossed out shapes that surrounded pictures and portions of text. This "granular treatment" with "processing attributes" can be thought of as an early inspiration for future [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading").

In winter 1983, I had four exams in subjects that I loved. When I passed the first in flying colors, my appetite for more kept increasing. In the end, I aced all four getting more and more enthusiastic for my efficient learning methods.

The four exams:

1. after a few days of procrastination, on Jan 16, 1983, in 11 hours designated for learning, I learned biochemistry for 8h. Next day, I aced the first exam (with Dr Augustyniak, wife of Prof. Jacek Augustyniak)
2. after a few procrastination days devoted to music, on Jan 27, 1983, I spent 604 min. of "cramming" microbiology. The next day, I aced the second exam
3. on Feb 3, 1983, I aced physiology after 3 days of joyful cramming, studying for 8 hours each day
4. on Mar 1-2, 1983, I spent 6-8 hours learning molecular biology using my "text gobbling" method. The next day, bullseye again (with the famous Prof. Jan Michejda)

That's a total of 7 full-time days of learning motivated by pleasure, interest, appetite for success, and minimal pressure of school deadlines. I proved the power of my *"note crossing"* "technology". The taste of efficient learning and the pleasant memories stayed with me for many years. They have certainly shaped a subconscious drive to re-experience something similar in the digital world.

Successful cramming for exams started a drive for future effective learning with pleasure

## Spaced repetition

Long before the emergence of [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"), I was busy with implementing its central component: [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition"):

- 1985: [Development of spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/The_birthday_of_spaced_repetition:_July_31,_1985 "The birthday of spaced repetition: July 31, 1985")
- 1986: [First steps of spaced repetition on paper](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/First_steps_of_SuperMemo "First steps of SuperMemo")
- 1987: [Development of the first spaced repetition application](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_1.0_for_DOS_\(1987\) "SuperMemo 1.0 for DOS (1987)")

Without [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition"), [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") would quickly become unviable

## Theoretical framework

In my [PhD Dissertation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Economics_of_learning "Economics of learning") (1995), I hinted how [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") might look in the future. Within a few years, that vision clarified, and was later simplified to become [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"):

- 1994-1995: [SuperMemo Knowledge Machine](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_Knowledge_Machine "SuperMemo Knowledge Machine")
- 1996: [Integrating SuperMemo with the Internet](https://super-memory.com/english/ol/sm-xxi.htm)

Before 1999, thinking about [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") was slowed down by focusing too much on the **[minimum information principle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Minimum_information_principle "Minimum information principle")**

## Origins of incremental reading

The name " **[incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading")** " first appeared in [SuperMemo 10](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_10 "SuperMemo 10") (2000). The concept originated from combining our natural reading habits with the demands of [spaced repetition](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spaced_repetition "Spaced repetition") in [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"). We rarely pick up a book and read it cover-to-cover in one go. At school we often dig through a number of textbooks used for different courses. At home we stop reading a book to read a newspaper and then stop reading the newspaper to watch TV. A combination of needs and interests determines how far we go with the reading of an individual text (see: [Learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive")). SuperMemo drives the parallel reading concept to an extreme by letting you read just one sentence from one chapter from one book and then go on to reading [extracts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Extract "Extract") from a thousand books and/or web articles. Due to the [spacing effect](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Spacing_effect "Spacing effect"), the result of this seemingly disruptive process is staggering. When employed skillfully, **incremental reading** has a stunning impact on comprehension, [consistency](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Consistency "Consistency"), [coherence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Coherence "Coherence"), [applicability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Applicability "Applicability"), and [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") of knowledge. Most of all, the overall efficiency of the process is sealed by a powerful impact of incremental reading on the [pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning").

[SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") 's contribution here is the management of this parallel, incremental, prioritized, dendritic, and multi-source reading process. As for the creative aspect of incremental reading, it has been known and employed for ages (see: [Inevitability of incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Inevitability_of_incremental_reading "Inevitability of incremental reading")).

The transition from [Knowledge Machine](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_Machine "Knowledge Machine") to [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") required the realization that [cloze deletion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion") is an efficient learning tool even if it does not meet the [minimum information principle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Minimum_information_principle "Minimum information principle"). Clozes can evolve incrementally or still provide a good learning framework by anchoring knowledge in a highly specific context.

Practical employment of [cloze deletion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion") reshaped the reasoning about [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading")

## Birthday

If the first use of [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") were to mark its birthday, the date would be vague and diluted, spanning 1997 to 2000.

I started using the tree dots *"\[...\]"* standard in 1997 (1 [item](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item")). In 1998, I created only 4 items using manually typed [cloze](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze "Cloze"). This became 75 items in 1999 with a true explosion in early 2000. It was also the time I needed to paste in longer texts for use as the raw material for [cloze deletions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion"), but SuperMemo had no [topics](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Topic "Topic") to make it smooth. For a while, I was then pasting texts to the question part of items. This helped generating [cloze deletions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion") from such a skeletal text source. It was then a matter of time before this was becoming cumbersome.

On Mar 27, 2000, I woke up at 7:20 after a very long bout of night sleep. 7h sleep in the year 2000 was not unusual as my understanding of [circadian cycle](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Circadian_cycle "Circadian cycle") was still rudimentary. Today, I usually sleep only 4.5 hours due to higher sleep efficiency, I believe. On that day in 2000, I drank coffee and spent 30 min. of learning with SuperMemo. I made 287 [repetitions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Repetition "Repetition") on this day. Learning occurred in 4 blocks. None of those repetitions contained the text *"\[...\]"*. But reviewing those items, I realized that I started my [cloze](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze "Cloze") habits with plain *"..."*.

In the end I counted 35 such items on that day. Quite a representation! SuperMemo estimated the [retention](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Retention "Retention") on that material, 25 years later, at 90%. A quick test confirmed a good recall due to the triviality of those [items](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item") that do get refreshed in many contexts, e.g. synchronous satellites, age of bipedalism, independence of Namibia, and dozens of items on the physiology of [sleep](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Sleep "Sleep").

Those items might have served as inspiration for [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") ideas that came next (on the morning of Mar 27). After learning with SuperMemo, I spent 65 min. on some programming and the ideas for [SuperMemo 10](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_10 "SuperMemo 10"). I compiled a set of tools I would need for efficient processing of texts. The notes about those ideas might be hard to recover as I started my [collection](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Collection "Collection") for [tasklists](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Tasklist "Tasklist") and SuperMemo ideas only 7 months later. However, while reviewing the [elements](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Element "Element") reviewed on Mar 27, 2000, I found a [topic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Topic "Topic") that clinches the birthday of [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") (see below)! At that time, the [topic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Topic "Topic") must have been an [item](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item"), and it spoke of *"longer items"* instead of *"articles"* or *"topics"*

Here is the unedited copy of notes from Mar 27, 2000:

Reading longer items:
- mark the beginning as read (or add it at the end?)
- extract the beginning
- make the extract take over the learning process from the element
- schedule the element at a desired time for repetition
- choose the next reading for both manually?
- OPTION: JUMP TO THE LAST READING POINT (special char of special color)

Incremental reading was born on Mar 27, 2000

## Timeline of ideas

Some rough notes on the progression of incremental reading ideas:

- 1972: "incremental writing" based on following links in encyclopedia (see: [[wiki-history-of-incremental-reading|Childhood]])
- Jan 16 - Mar 3, 1983: impactful examination session at the university that made me perfect my *"text gobbling"* toolset (see: [[wiki-history-of-incremental-reading|Progressive reading]])
- Apr 14, 1993: idea of combining SuperMemo with hypertext publications from which the user will be able to extract customized SuperMemo databases by means of clicking the interesting pieces of information
- Jun 1, 1993: morning idea of implementing a *"text gobbler"* for reading and *"eating"* items. It was aimed at authors who would predefine [items](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Item "Item") associated with their texts ([Andy Matuschak](https://andymatuschak.org/books) does this these days)
- 1994: introducing occasional [cloze deletions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion") in [Economics of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Economics_of_learning "Economics of learning") examples
- 1995: introducing [topics](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Topic "Topic") for the sake of course presentation in early projects associated with [SuperMemo 8](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_8 "SuperMemo 8") (e.g. in language learning)
- 1998: introducing richly formatted text that encourages copy and paste from the internet, and reading in SuperMemo
- 1999: increasing my copy-and-paste rate in item formulations, and copying a keyword from the question to the answer turned out very convenient, obvious, and inevitable (precursor of cloze)
- May 30, 1999: simple [extracts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Extract "Extract") and [cloze deletions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion") in RTF components
- 1999: [SuperMemo 9.5](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_9 "SuperMemo 9") (aka SuperMemo 99) introduced [reading lists](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Reading_list "Reading list"), first simple [cloze deletion](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion") options, and [branch review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Branch_review "Branch review")
- Mar 27, 2000: thinking about new options for reading longer texts. The couple of ideas qualify this day as **[[wiki-history-of-incremental-reading|the birthday of incremental reading]])**
- Apr 4, 2000: importing complete pages from Internet Explorer
- Apr 10, 2000: implementing **Postpone**
- Apr 14, 2000: implementing [subset learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Subset_learning "Subset learning") (generalized take on branch learning)
- Apr 25, 2000: social pressures spark **Toolkit: [Plan](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Plan "Plan")** as a universal excuse for being always busy
- May 9, 2000: implementing **Add a new page: HTML file** for importing pages from files
- May 12, 2000: window locking at cloze and extract made [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") operations smooth and pleasurable (minor technical detail provided a major boost to usability)
- May 15, 2000: implementing read points
- Jun 20, 2000: proposing a [topic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Topic "Topic") [review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Review "Review") algorithm based on [A-Factors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/A-Factor "A-Factor")
- Jul 5, 2000: 9th anniversary of [SuperMemo World](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_World "SuperMemo World"). Miko says: *"little has changed in [SuperMemo 10](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_10 "SuperMemo 10"). I do not yet see [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading") as a revolution!"*
- Nov 14, 2000: [SuperMemo 10](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_10 "SuperMemo 10") was released. Anyone could try [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"). Few people would. The concept was not well documented and poorly understood. Its significance was largely underappreciated

## Implementation timeline

- **1999**: [SuperMemo 9.5](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_9 "SuperMemo 9") made the first step towards efficient reading of electronic articles by introducing [reading lists](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Reading_list "Reading list") and the first primitive reading tools: [Extract](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Extract "Extract") and [Cloze](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze "Cloze"). Reading lists were [prioritized lists](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Tasklist "Tasklist") of articles to read. Extracts made it possible to split larger articles into smaller portions. Clozes made it possible to convert short sentences into the question-answer format by means of [cloze deletions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Cloze_deletion "Cloze deletion") (i.e. fill-in-the-blank questions)
- **2000**: [SuperMemo 10](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_10 "SuperMemo 10") greatly increased the efficiency of reading by introducing the concept of [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"). Incremental reading makes it possible to simultaneously read dozens of articles. Each article is read in small increments fully controlled and prioritized by the user and/or the default learning process. Components of incremental reading introduced in SuperMemo 10: new [A-Factor](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/A-Factor "A-Factor") -based [topic](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Topic "Topic") repetition scheme (i.e. the learning algorithm), read points, formatting in extracts and in clozes, text highlights, source article link, reading toolbar, [subset review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Subset_review "Subset review"), subset postpone, and support for longer articles (previously, for technical reasons, SuperMemo was limited to 64K articles)
- **2002**: SuperMemo 11 introduced HTML-based incremental reading. For the first time, the user would see little difference between the material in his web browser and in SuperMemo. SuperMemo 11 added automatic import of materials from Internet Explorer, mid-interval [repetitions](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Repetition "Repetition") that make it possible to review portions of material without detriment to the learning process, search-based learning (i.e. [subset review](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Subset_review "Subset review"), in which the subset is defined by advanced search tools), dynamically modified [A-Factors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/A-Factor "A-Factor") that fine-tune the priorities without user intervention, postpone wizard that makes reading lists obsolete, separate topic/item statistics and new incremental reading progress statistics, reference labeling, etc.
- **2004**: SuperMemo 12 was developed solely with the view to perfecting the tools of [incremental reading](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_reading "Incremental reading"). The data collected from months of actual incremental reading was instrumental in enhancing the algorithm and the tools. Fine tuning of the modification of topic A-Factors enhanced the optimization of new material review in a heavily overloaded process. New tools included: rich statistics for monitoring and optimizing the learning process, tools for handling excessive delays in review, browsing sources of extracts and clozes, one-key reference labeling, proliferating remote images, easy integration of remote images, and more
- **2006**: SuperMemo 13 made a major step in rationalizing the material overload in incremental learning by introducing the **[priority queue](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Priority_queue "Priority queue")**. SuperMemo 13 made it easier to import articles from the Internet (esp. from Wikipedia). Material overload could be handled with auto-postpone and auto-sort tools. SuperMemo 13 simplified importing, arranging, compressing, converting, zooming, and trimming pictures. SuperMemo 13 could pick any folder on the user's disk and convert all the file archives into material that can be processed incrementally (e.g. article archives, picture archives, family albums, movie clips, documentation files, or assorted archives). SuperMemo 13 also made it simple to do one-key searches and import of auxiliary learning material on the web with customizable tools (e.g. Google, encyclopedias, dictionaries, picture archives, etc.)
- **2008**: SuperMemo 14 extended incremental learning into the areas of pictures, sounds and video with visual learning and YouTube-based [incremental video](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Incremental_video "Incremental video"). Imports from the web, esp. Wikipedia and YouTube became simpler with set priorities, references, categories, etc. SuperMemo 14 also simplified and automated the process of creating source material references
- **2011**: SuperMemo 15 improved web imports (e.g. eliminating article duplicates), splitting articles (e.g. automatic splitting of Wikipedia articles into separate topics), handling references, and more
- **2013**: SuperMemo 16 consolidated all incremental learning technologies with the last two missing links: incremental audio and incremental video with video files. It added more tools for Wikipedia imports (e.g. downloading full resolution pictures instead of thumbs, etc.), and visual learning (e.g. global paste). It also made it possible to separate extracts from source articles in child browsers. Dozens of improvements to incremental learning process convert quantity into new quality, e.g. a trivial, but extremely useful incremental reading functions: **Delete before cursor** and **Delete after cursor**
- **2016**: SuperMemo 17 introduced [neural creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Neural_creativity "Neural creativity"), [concept maps](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_map "Concept map"), image references, URL deprecation (e.g. for Wikipedia updates), duplicate management, priority coloring for easier priority management in the browser, sorting by text size, auto-updated descendant counts in the [knowledge tree](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_tree "Knowledge tree"), multi-highlight search, and more
- **2019**: SuperMemo 18 is the newest version of [SuperMemo for Windows](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_for_Windows "SuperMemo for Windows") (Apr 2020). It was optimized further to make incremental reading faster, easier and more effective
- **2023**: [SuperMemo 19](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo_19 "SuperMemo 19") makes it possible to work with MS Edge and Chrome to make for a smooth transfer of knowledge from the web to user's memory
