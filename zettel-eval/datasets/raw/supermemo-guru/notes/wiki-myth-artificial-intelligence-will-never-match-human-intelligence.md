## Intro

When writing this text in 2021, I never expected it to grow old as fast as it did. [Artificial intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence") is speeding towards Singularity. Humans can only hold their breath.

Artificial intelligence will rule the world

## Myth

✗ Myth: **Robots lack emotions, and are blind servants of human commands**

A painting made by a robot sold for $700,000 ([NFT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-fungible_token)). The art was painted by [Sophia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophia_\(robot\)), a humanoid robot.

As her starting points in the creative trajectory, Sophia took (1) her own photographic image and (2) a painting made by a **human** artist. She generated a large number of paintings before she sat down to her final act.

The fact that a robot had to learn from an art made by humans was taken as an indication of human superiority.

## Fact

To paint artistically, children also need prior input. They are immersed in culture. All human paintings capitalize on what we observe in prior life, incl. hundreds of prior paintings. The robot needs to see a lot of human paintings or listen to many human symphonies before it can come up with her own. So do children. If music was developed without culture, it might be more creative and yet harder to appreciate (due to our cultural bias). If a child with no knowledge of music creates a composition, it will differ little from one that could be developed by a robot in similar circumstances. When I create a piece of music in 2021, few people like it. It is not up to today's standards. I have been biased by black funk of the 1970s, and it is hard to unlearn your first love. I don't think my intelligence should be questioned only because my genre is a product of a prior influence.

My hero Prof. Tadeusiewicz noticed that computers cannot assess value, or show emotion, or experience the sense of self. He is right only as far as that we just still do not build in the necessary structures into the artificially intelligent brain.

If we make a robot strive at self-preservation and adapt optimally, we may see emotionality and the Self might emerge in the process. If [Boston Dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Dynamics) engineers kick an [intelligent robot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BigDog), the robot regains its balance and keeps executing its plan. However, if the robot detected any damage to its structures or circuits, and the penalty for damage was used in reinforcement learning, the right way to respond to an attack might be to strike back. For a robot, to prevent further damage, the best strategy might appear to be to knock the human attacker out. We do not design robots with such self-preservation adaptation properties because we generally focus on a subset of functionality that would be limited by [Asimov's principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laws_of_robotics).

The emergence of valuations is even simpler. If the robot learns to value knowledge that serves its goals, it will soon value a beautiful painting too (see: [Pleasure of learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Pleasure_of_learning "Pleasure of learning")). All we need to reward is the emergence of coherent concepts in the [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network"), or reward only concepts that favor well-being, preservation, or similarly defined goals. In reinforcement learning, we use an artificially generated reward or penalty, and claim that AI cannot assess the value of knowledge. It is enough to use sensors of pain (e.g. of structural damage), or well-being (e.g. battery charge levels), and use them as feedback in concept valuations. With no external assessment, we can see [knowledge valuation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Knowledge_valuation "Knowledge valuation") emerge on the basis of valuating sensor input.

Last but not least, to make artificial intelligence more human, we need the emergence of empathy. For empathy, we need modelling of other sentient beings. As we have no specialized circuitry for such modelling, it is unlikely empathy is wired into the brain using a genetic blueprint. It is more likely to be a simple descendant of game theoretical thinking, in which, by modelling others, we put our selves into their boots. Empathy is simply a new quality of higher intelligence. With better understanding of the world, we may see suffering of an ant as unpleasant to watch or accept. Empathy is subject to similar concept activation competition as in all other aspects of [conceptual computation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptual_computation "Conceptual computation"). When rooting for a gazelle chased by a cheetah, we can instantly suppress the empathy for a starving cheetah family as the valuations of gazelle's life tend to be more direct and thus stronger.
