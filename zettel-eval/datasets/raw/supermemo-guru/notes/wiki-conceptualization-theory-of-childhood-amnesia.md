## Conceptualization theory

**Conceptualization theory of childhood amnesia** says that the network [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") in an immature brain will inevitably result in difficulty in forming [stable](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") long-term memories.

Neurogenesis hypothesis of infantile amnesia proposed by Frankland is gaining evidence and momentum (see: [Childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia")). I believe that the picture is a bit wider. For example, the [high dendritic spine turnover in development](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dendritic_spine_turnover_is_high_early_in_development "Dendritic spine turnover is high early in development") is another hint at how brain development affects memory. These are just subcomponents of the overall [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") process in a developing brain.

The fact that the young brain is a naïve [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") should indicate that independently of the actual [neural mechanism](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Dendritic_spine_stabilization_corresponds_with_long-term_memories "Dendritic spine stabilization corresponds with long-term memories"), the computational capacity of the network in the process of [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") would follow a sigmoidal curve. Metaphorically speaking, if the set of emerging [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept") is seen as a population, the size of the concept network will correspond with the [carrying capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrying_capacity), and the principles used in modelling population growth can apply to the process of [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization"). If this reasoning is correct, slow growth of the population of [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept") in the early stages of the process will outwardly manifest as [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia").

Neurogenesis, neurite dynamics, and other [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") processes are the likely cause of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia")

## Conceptualization process

Early in the process, before rudimentary [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept"), modules and hierarchies emerge, the network has negligible computational capacity. A newborn's brain is hardly able to recognize shapes, discriminate between sounds or tastes, and her motor ability is based on reflex actions inborn via brain topology.

As shown in the [exponential acceleration in free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Exponential_acceleration_in_free_learning "Exponential acceleration in free learning"), gradual buildup of low level [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept") will quickly result in an exponential acceleration of growth. Once a child can recognize basic shapes, it can start building up a map of the surrounding 3D world and begin the process of visual [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") of objects. Conceptualization proceeds from the brain's input (e.g. visual signals) towards progressively more [abstract layers](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Abstract_knowledge "Abstract knowledge") of the [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") (e.g. recognizing a partially occluded zebra in motion).

At first, a newborn is mentally blind. However, it does not take long for the smiling face of the mom to bring a great deal of happy shivers that are likely initiated by an increasingly specialized and pattern-specific [concept](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept"): the mom.

The exponential acceleration of development in the [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") process will only be limited by the size of the [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network"). For a small system, we will have a clear sigmoidal progress in the network's computational capacity. However, it is not clear if the human brain is small enough to be saturated with [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") by the time of death. Preliminary measurements made with [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo") show no sign of saturation or slow down. Metacognitive and mnemonic powers of the brain seem to increase with the progression of the [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization"). The more we learn, the easier it is to add new knowledge. That progression may continue into retirement.

## Episodic memories

The way we define [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") makes it refer primarily to episodic memory. The way I measure childhood amnesia also relies on episodic memories (see: [[wiki-childhood-amnesia|Measurement example]]). In terms of a [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network"), episodic memories can be seen as strings of concepts organized in an temporal manner (e.g. pet shop -> turtle -> owner -> terrarium, etc.), or simply a set of concepts linked by time coincidence (shop-turtle-light). Those strings/sets may be consolidated in long term storage, however, they will only be remembered if they can be associated with any of stable [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept") in the brain. In an amnesic brain of a child, fluidity of [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept") will easily sever the links with the episodic sequence, which will be lost as a result.

![](https://supermemo.guru/images/thumb/3/34/Temporal_organization_of_episodic_memories.png/250px-Temporal_organization_of_episodic_memories.png)

> ***Figure:** Episodic memories are based on a small sets of associated [concepts](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept "Concept"). The way the concepts are inter-linked in the [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") will determine the temporal arrangements of individual concepts. In a linear arrangement, all concepts need to be recalled, and they can provide a timeline of events. In a network arrangement, individual concepts can, but do not have to be activated. They will be perceived as coincidental (i.e. occurring at the same time). In a network arrangement, incomplete recall may result in forgetting and gradual [generalization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Generalization "Generalization") of the episode. Crucially, all episodic memories need a [semantic anchor](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_anchor "Semantic anchor") to be retrieved. For that reason, episodic memories do not survive past the windows of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia")*

## Semantic aspect of the amnesia

If plasticity expressed by neurogenesis was to fully explain [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia"), we would not observe frequent aberrant cases where [declarative memory](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Declarative_memory "Declarative memory") can somehow find its place in a child's brain and survive the tumultuous period of early [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization"). I have managed to document an interesting case in which the memory of the letters of the alphabet is entirely unpredictable. Some letters may latch onto the [concept network](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Concept_network "Concept network") early and survive the period of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") (~0.7-0.8 year overlap). Memories of other letters may lag, and potentially contribute to dyslexia. For details see: [Semantic aspects of childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_aspects_of_childhood_amnesia "Semantic aspects of childhood amnesia")

![](https://supermemo.guru/images/thumb/8/80/Memory_lapses_while_learning_alphabet_with_SuperMemo_%28ages_2-7%29.png/400px-Memory_lapses_while_learning_alphabet_with_SuperMemo_%28ages_2-7%29.png)

> ***Figure:** This graph proves that [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") has a semantic component. In the presented example from [SuperMemo](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/SuperMemo "SuperMemo"), a child learned the English alphabet before the age of 2. However, until the age of 7.5, many of the individual letters have been forgotten and had to be re-memorized. Some of the letters, however, seemingly showed stable memory. As there are no apparent reasons for which letters are different (incl. the frequency of exposure), the only plausible explanation of this amazing spectrum of [stability](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Stability "Stability") is that some letters have found their [mnemonic anchors](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Mnemonic_anchor "Mnemonic anchor") early in the learning process, while others did not, or were even subject to recurrent [interference](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Interference "Interference"). If memory survival was a function of receding amnesia, the bulk of the letters would have anchored late in the process. The graph shows the reverse. The overlap of declarative memories with the period of episodic [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") spans around 9 months. It is possible to achieve [semantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Semantic_learning "Semantic learning") before the age of two, however, such learning should be guided by the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive") alone. For the theoretical mechanism see: Conceptualization theory of childhood amnesia*

## Educational strategies

The nature of the [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") process calls into question a whole host of modern educational strategies that hurt children.

For our kids, we should seek rich environments, self-directed exploration and [free learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Free_learning "Free learning"). We should encourage play, fun, [videogames](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Videogames "Videogames"), sports, YouTube, travel, socializing, company of adults, and unadulterated freedom.

Instead, we ruin the childhood with [asemantic learning](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Asemantic_learning "Asemantic learning"), which slows down and skews the [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization"). In the effort of trying to make kids smart, we undermine their future [creativity](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Creativity "Creativity") and [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence"). Letters of the alphabet, numbers, memorizing verses and songs, memorizing months and the days of the week, following orders, [maternal separation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Maternal_separation "Maternal separation") ([daycare](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Daycare "Daycare")), etc. Reading and math are great as long as they come by child's own choice. Otherwise they provide no conceptual skeleton for modelling reality. If videos provide more fun than books, by definition of the [learn drive](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Learn_drive "Learn drive"), they lead to better [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization"), and better understanding of the world.

In addition to a natural interaction with the world, modern kids conceptualize best in video, simulations, and games. Audiobooks carry the same message as books, but conceptualize better in pre-literate kids. They get converted into the same symbolic language that provides a better cognitive rate for rich [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization").

Adults are still largely soaked in pre-digital thinking. Educational visionary [Seymour Papert](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Seymour_Papert "Seymour Papert") predicted this all in 1993. He begged to drop harmful letteracy (see: [Papert's visionary call to ditch obsolete letteracy (1993)](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Papert%27s_visionary_call_to_ditch_obsolete_letteracy_\(1993\) "Papert's visionary call to ditch obsolete letteracy (1993)")).

Early academic instruction is harmful. It undermines child's [intelligence](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Intelligence "Intelligence") by interfering with the [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") process of the growing brain

## Origin

I proposed the **conceptualization theory of childhood** amnesia using a simple model of [conceptual computation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptual_computation "Conceptual computation") that implies the inevitability of early amnesia in a naïve network. I have not read about anyone proposing a similar theory even though it seems pretty obvious and necessary. Frankland's work is focused on tangible research that would explain the amnesia at the neural level. Hence his focus on the measurables, such as the neurogenesis. As for the neural network theorists, the apparent absence of similar suggestions might stem from the fact that the whole idea of [conceptual computation](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptual_computation "Conceptual computation") quarrels with connectionist models of the brain. If a large portion of the neural science world still dismisses the [grandmother cell](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Grandmother_cell "Grandmother cell") theory, the idea of amnesia as an outcome of [conceptualization](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Conceptualization "Conceptualization") might seem alien (see: [The truth about grandmother cells](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/The_truth_about_grandmother_cells "The truth about grandmother cells")). Instead, the mere existence of [childhood amnesia](https://supermemo.guru/wiki/Childhood_amnesia "Childhood amnesia") should make connectionists realize there is something wrong with theories derived from thinking in terms of artificial neural networks.
